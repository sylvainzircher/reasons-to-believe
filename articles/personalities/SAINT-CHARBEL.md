# Saint Charbel Makhlouf: The Patron Saint of Lebanon

## Summary of Interview — _Les Rendez-vous de Kate_ with Pierre Jovanovic (Episode 3)

---

## Format & Participants

Episode 3 of _Les Rendez-vous de Kate_, a short-format French-language series on spirituality. Hosted by **Kate**, featuring **Pierre Jovanovic** — journalist, economist, author, and director of _Le Jardin des Livres_. Jovanovic describes Charbel Makhlouf as his personal favourite saint, surpassing even Padre Pio in the extraordinary density of verifiable supernatural phenomena surrounding his life and death.

---

## Who Was Charbel Makhlouf?

**Saint Charbel Makhlouf** (born **Youssef Anton Makhlouf**, 1828–1898) was a Lebanese Maronite Christian monk. He was born in **Bqaa Kafra**, a small mountain village in northern Lebanon at an altitude of over 1,500 metres. In **1852** he entered the Lebanese Maronite Order at the Monastery of Saint Maron and took the religious name **Charbel**. He died on **24 December 1898** at **Annaya**, Lebanon, where his tomb and sanctuary are located today. He was beatified in **1965** and canonised in **1977**.

Jovanovic describes him as a monk who lived in complete obscurity and anonymity throughout his life — unknown beyond a very local radius — and whose worldwide fame came entirely from what happened _after_ his death.

---

## The Maronite Church and Its Distinction from Roman Catholicism

Jovanovic opens with a brief explanation of the Maronite Church's place among Eastern Christian traditions:

- The Maronite Church, like the Ethiopian, Coptic, Greek Orthodox, Russian Orthodox, Serbian, Bulgarian, and other Eastern churches, developed independently of Rome, growing out of the traditions of Byzantium.
- These churches have their own ancient liturgical traditions and hold libraries of manuscripts — many still untranslated and unstudied — that are unique in the world.
- Crucially, **Maronite and Ethiopian churches conduct their liturgies in Aramaic** — the language spoken by Christ and his apostles — making them, Jovanovic argues, arguably closer to primitive Christianity than the Church of Rome.
- He notes that the **Book of Enoch** — destroyed at the Vatican's request in much of the Western world — was preserved in **Ethiopia** and recovered there, illustrating the distinct heritage these churches protect.
- Charbel was not canonised by Rome voluntarily: it took approximately **50 years of pressure** from the Maronite Church and the sustained lobbying of extremely wealthy Lebanese businessmen before Rome agreed to officially recognise him as a saint. Jovanovic points out, however, that Charbel had been performing miracles for decades before any Vatican recognition.

---

## His Life: Anonymity and Solitude

Charbel's life offered nothing spectacular while he was alive. He celebrated Mass with great devotion, and there were some local miraculous reports, but he remained entirely anonymous in the broader world. At around 60 years of age he had a serious health episode, after which he lived as a **hermit in complete solitude** for approximately **nine years** until his death at 70.

When the monks found him dead, they buried him according to the standard practice of their order:

- A shallow grave approximately **60–70 cm deep** in the earth
- No coffin — only his **burlap habit**
- The grave was covered with soil

There was nothing exceptional about his burial. Monks of that order had been buried this way for centuries.

---

## The First Signs: Light and Moisture

Following his burial, two anomalous phenomena were reported at his grave:

1. **Spectral lights** — luminous phenomena visible at night around the site of his burial
2. **Damp earth** — the soil directly over his grave was persistently moist, while the surrounding graves were dry

These signs prompted the monks to exhume the body. What they found initiated everything that followed.

---

## The Incorrupt Body

Upon exhumation, the monks discovered Charbel's body was **completely incorrupt** — supple, fresh, and showing no decomposition — despite:

- The **intense heat of Lebanon** (temperatures reaching 50°C)
- Burial in **shallow, unprotected earth** with no coffin and no embalming
- The **flooding** of the burial site during rainfall (the shallow vault regularly filled with water and became a muddy pool)

Jovanovic emphasises the biophysical impossibility: without the biological defences of a living body, bacteria and viruses inside the corpse normally destroy it from within, eventually rupturing the skin and releasing gases — producing the unmistakeable odour of death. None of this occurred.

The monks placed the body in a **zinc coffin** for protection and reburied it.

---

## The Exuding Oil

Shortly after the reburial, a new phenomenon appeared: **a liquid — described as similar in texture to olive oil — began seeping from the body and flowing out of the coffin**. This continued for years. When the authorities eventually placed the body in a **sealed metal coffin** specifically designed to prevent any liquid from escaping, the liquid continued to seep through regardless.

The monks began collecting this liquid in flasks and selling it at a nominal price to visitors. It was associated with numerous reported healings, including of cancer patients who applied it to their bodies.

Jovanovic notes the parallel with **Saint Nicholas of Bari** (the historical origin of the Santa Claus figure), whose remains similarly exuded a miraculous myrrh-like oil — a phenomenon he discusses in his book _888_.

---

## Multiple Exhumations and Medical Examinations

The body was exhumed **multiple times** — both shortly after burial by the monks and later formally at Rome's request as part of the beatification and canonisation process. Key documented medical examinations include:

**1927 examination:**

- **Professor Armand** and **Professor Jouffried** of the **Faculty of Medicine, Beirut** examined the body and produced a written report describing it as being in a "perfect state" — a phrase Jovanovic highlights as remarkable given the burial conditions and elapsed time.

**1950 examination:**

- **Dr. Chikri Bann**, **Dr. Maroun** (Director of the Lebanese Government Health Service and professor at the Faculty of Medicine), and **Deputy Dr. Joseph Eid** examined the remains **under microscope** and issued a formal declaration: _"Science, in its current state, finds itself unable to explain this case."_

These findings are cited in Jovanovic's book _Enquête sur l'existence des anges gardiens_, alongside a passage from his visit to the **Los Angeles County morgue**, where he asked the director what would happen to a body placed in an aerated tomb. The answer: within two days the smell alone would "remind you that a corpse needs to be buried." He then contrasts this with Charbel's body — which had been laid on a plank barely 25 cm above the ground, repeatedly flooded, and yet was found supple, fresh, and giving off the scent of a living person.

---

## The Canonisation Process: Vatican Delays

Jovanovic attributes the extraordinary length of the canonisation process — beatification took until 1965, canonisation until 1977, roughly **50 years of effort** — to two institutional factors:

1. **Vatican bureaucratic inertia**, combined with a departmental culture of making religious orders wait and pay for accelerated proceedings.
2. **Financial extraction**: Vatican departments effectively charged the Maronite Church and its supporters for "moving the file along," regardless of the volume of documented miracles.

His sources for this claim:

- **Kenneth Woodward**, journalist at _Newsweek_, who reported this practice
- **Roger Peyrefitte**, in _Les Clés de Saint-Pierre_ (_The Keys of Saint Peter_), who reported the same, noting that by the mid-1960s Charbel's dossier was already on the table and the Vatican was simply dragging it out

Jovanovic's conclusion: Charbel's sanctity required no Vatican endorsement. Miracles were occurring — and continue to occur — independent of any official recognition.

---

## Scope and Universality of His Miracles

Between 50,000 and 70,000 miracles have been officially recorded at the sanctuary near Beirut, according to Jovanovic. The phenomenon is notably **cross-religious**:

- **Lebanese Muslims** as well as Christians have always visited his tomb and reported answered prayers.
- Even today, prominent politicians visit — Jovanovic states it is effectively a **Lebanese national tradition** to pray at Charbel's tomb regardless of one's religious affiliation or political position.
- He draws a contrast with Islam, which he observes does not have a comparable tradition of post-mortem physical supernatural phenomena (incorruptibility, exuding liquids, spectral lights) associated with its holy figures, noting this as one of the distinctions between the two traditions.

Regarding the mode of his intercession, Jovanovic compares Charbel to Thérèse of Lisieux and Padre Pio: **one does not need to go to the sanctuary in person**. Prayer alone — addressed to him directly — is sufficient. He also appears to people **in dreams**, according to widespread testimony.

## The Carlos Ghosn Escape: A Modern Miracle

Jovanovic recounts an episode he describes in his book _888_: the wife of **Carlos Ghosn** (the former Nissan/Renault CEO who escaped from house arrest in Tokyo in 2019) went to Charbel's tomb to pray and make offerings for her husband's escape. Jovanovic calls Ghosn's subsequent escape — smuggled out of Japan in a large case past security at **Narita Airport** and through the hypervigilant customs of **Istanbul Airport** (where authorities were especially alert due to PKK concerns) — "a pure miracle," crediting Charbel's intercession with having made possible what he considers statistically near-impossible. He notes that Ghosn's escape dramatically increased Charbel's international profile and popularity.

## A Personal Anecdote: Lost in Beirut

Jovanovic recounts a personal experience of Charbel's intercession. While in Beirut for an interview with Lebanese minister **Charbel Nahas**, he was dropped by his taxi far from the correct address — which had been poorly transcribed and was essentially impossible to locate, as Beirut streets frequently have no numbers or clear signage. Completely lost and on the verge of missing his appointment, he silently prayed to Saint Charbel — noting, with some humour, that the minister shared the saint's first name. Immediately, a stranger approached, took him by the hand without a word, walked him approximately 800 metres to the correct building, pointed him inside, and left. Jovanovic states: "I had perhaps a one in ten thousand or one in a million chance of meeting someone who knew that address."

## Charbel vs. Padre Pio: A Comparison

Jovanovic is direct in his assessment: **Charbel Makhlouf beats Padre Pio** in terms of the cumulative density of verifiable supernatural evidence. He acknowledges this was not obvious to him at the outset. His reasoning:

- Padre Pio's phenomena (stigmata, bilocation, prophecy, healings) are extraordinary but occurred during his lifetime and ceased at death.
- Charbel's phenomena began at death and have continued for over a century: incorruptibility, exuding oil (persisting through a sealed metal coffin), spectral lights, and 50,000–70,000 documented miracles.
- The incorruptibility itself, Jovanovic argues, **retroactively validates the entirety of his existence** — a life spent entirely in prayer and anonymity.

Both, however, occupy what he calls the **"Formula 1 tier"** of saints — at the very top alongside Catherine of Siena, Thérèse of Lisieux, and Thérèse of Avila.

He notes that in **Italian towns**, Padre Pio's portrait is displayed everywhere; in **Lebanese Christian neighbourhoods**, the single surviving portrait of Charbel Makhlouf plays the exact same role. He observed this personally in Beirut, where crossing from a Muslim to a Christian street can be a matter of one trottoir, marked by a five-metre crucifix.

## Charbel and the Odour of Sanctity

Jovanovic uses the Charbel case to explain the French expression _être en odeur de sainteté_ ("to be in the odour of sanctity"), tracing it back to the documented medieval and early modern phenomenon of holy persons dying without producing the odour of death — and sometimes emitting pleasant scents of flowers or perfume instead.

He cites **Dostoevsky's _The Brothers Karamazov_** as a literary illustration: the entire village anxiously awaits whether the elder **Father Zosima**, upon his death, will be found _en odeur de sainteté_ — an expectation rooted in real Orthodox practice, not literary invention. He emphasises this tradition is alive in the Eastern Orthodox Church today.

He reads a passage from _Enquête sur l'existence des anges gardiens_ describing his visit to the **Los Angeles County morgue**, where the stench of a decomposing body nearly made him vomit, to contrast with the fragrance reported from Charbel's body.

## The Question of Living Saints Today

A viewer question from the previous episode (on Padre Pio) — submitted by a commenter named **Cariole1234** — asks whether there are living persons today equivalent to Padre Pio or Thérèse of Lisieux, given the ubiquity of cameras and mobile phones.

Jovanovic's answer:

- The Vatican **actively suppresses** visible supernatural phenomena. Since approximately 80% of Vatican clergy do not, in his view, live according to Christian principles, they are hostile to any visible presence of God — because it implicitly indicts them.
- Living stigmatics and mystics are currently kept in **complete secrecy** by the institutional Church. He believes some exist but will not be revealed until after their deaths, when post-mortem phenomena (incorruption, etc.) force the matter into the open.
- In general, saints are **only recognised as such retroactively** — through the accumulation of post-mortem miracles — precisely because no one around them recognised their sanctity while they lived. This was as true of Padre Pio within his own Capuchin community as of Charbel among the monks of Annaya.

## The Holy Spirit, Akashic Records, and CIA Research

Jovanovic offers his theological and metaphysical framework for understanding how intercession works:

- The **Holy Spirit**, he suggests, is best understood not through the Byzantine theological controversy over whether it proceeds from the Father or the Son (which he dismisses as irrelevant) — but as something like _the air itself_: omnipresent, invisible, recording everything, interconnecting everything.
- This maps onto what Eastern traditions call the **Akashic field** — a universal field of information containing all events.
- **Robert Monroe** and the CIA remote viewing teams identified the same phenomenon, giving it their own name: a zone that "contains the totality." They could not explain it but could demonstrate that it functioned reliably.
- Our prayers travel into this field and are managed from "the other side" — organised into synchronicities and miracle-responses by the saints and the Spirit. Jovanovic references the film **Bruce Almighty** (_Dieu tout-puissant_, with Jim Carrey) as a popular illustration: God hands Jim Carrey his powers, and Carrey is immediately overwhelmed by tens of millions of simultaneous prayer requests flooding his computer — a comedic but apt representation of the scale of what is being managed effortlessly by the Divine.

---

## Thérèse of Lisieux: A Brief Comparative Note

Jovanovic mentions **Thérèse of Lisieux** as sharing the same mode of intercession as Charbel — available everywhere, not requiring physical pilgrimage to her tomb. He cites as evidence:

- She is among the top three saints in terms of churches named after her worldwide, including multiple churches in **Indonesia**, a predominantly Muslim country.
- When her **bones** (relics) were brought to **Dublin, Ireland**, approximately **3 million people** came to venerate them — a figure he finds staggering.
