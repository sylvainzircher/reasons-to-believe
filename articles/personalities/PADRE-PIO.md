# Padre Pio: Mystic, Stigmatic, and Prophet

## Summary of Interview — _Les Rendez-vous de Kate_ with Pierre Jovanovic (Episode 1)

---

## Format & Participants

This is the inaugural episode of _Les Rendez-vous de Kate_, a short-format French-language series on spirituality hosted by **Kate** (surname not given), featuring **Pierre Jovanovic** — journalist, economist, author, and director of the publishing house _Le Jardin des Livres_. Episodes are released approximately every two weeks. This first episode is dedicated as a tribute to **Padre Pio**.

---

## Who Was Padre Pio?

**Padre Pio** (born **Francesco Forgione**, 1887–1968) was an Italian Capuchin friar who bore the stigmata — the wounds of Christ — for fifty years. He is described by Jovanovic as one of the greatest mystical figures in modern history, belonging to what he calls the "Formula 1 category" of saints: those who combine **incorruptibility after death**, **remote vision**, **constant miracles**, and **bilocation**.

He is universally known among Christians and continues to attract enormous devotion globally. His remains are venerated at **San Giovanni Rotondo**, Italy.

---

## His Core Gift: Seeing Past and Future

Jovanovic identifies Padre Pio's most defining supernatural characteristic as his ability to **see both the past and the future of individuals** — a capacity he compares to that of certain highly spiritually advanced figures in other traditions (Tibetan lamas, Lubavitch rabbis) who dedicate their lives entirely to prayer.

**In the confessional:** Penitents would confess their sins, only for Padre Pio to immediately add sins they had omitted or forgotten — confronting them with precise details of their own history they had not disclosed. This "scrutiny of hearts" left people, in Jovanovic's words, "literally stunned."

**With journalists:** When journalists came to attend his Mass, he would approach them before the service — having already known they were present and having already read their intentions — and tell them: _"Stop ruminating on your thoughts. I know what you are going to write. I am here in front of you."_ He reportedly astonished many journalists this way.

---

## Early Vocation and the Military Episode (1916)

Jovanovic is cautious about hagiographic biographies, noting they often contain embellishments. What he states with certainty is that Padre Pio knew he wanted to become a priest from the age of **12** — a characteristic he identifies as common to all the mystics he has studied.

When Padre Pio was mobilised into the Italian army during World War I, desertion was out of the question for him morally. During his military medical examination, his body temperature reportedly climbed to approximately **60°C** — a figure that completely panicked the examining doctors and led to his immediate discharge from service on medical grounds. He was then able to return to his priestly vocation.

Jovanovic frames this as a direct divine intervention: _"God decided it was not his time to do military service — he was more useful in his vocation as priest and monk."_ Upon his transfer to the monastery of **San Giovanni Rotondo**, he recovered.

---

## The Stigmata and Their Disappearance

Padre Pio received the stigmata and bore them for **50 years**, reliving the Passion of Christ every Friday — the same phenomenon observed in Therese Neumann. Jovanovic notes that at his death in 1968, the stigmata **disappeared completely** from his body, leaving no trace, which he considers entirely fitting: _"The poor man could no longer bear living through the Crucifixion every Friday."_

He draws a parallel with Therese Neumann, noting that a 21st-century dermatologist observing her stigmata on a Friday — wounds as severe as if she had been crucified, her skin torn open — and then watching those wounds close completely within three hours, would find it utterly beyond the reach of science to explain.

---

## Incorruptibility

Padre Pio's body was discovered to be **incorrupt** after death, and notably **moist** — a detail Jovanovic highlights as particularly extraordinary. This places him, in Jovanovic's categorisation system (developed in _Enquête sur l'existence des anges gardiens_), in **Category 1** of mystic-saints — the highest tier.

Jovanovic offers a personal anecdote to contextualise incorruptibility across traditions: while visiting a temple in **Kyoto, Japan** — described as the Rome of Asia for its density of temples — he sat quietly behind a monk who appeared to be meditating in the lotus position. Only upon walking past him did Jovanovic realise the monk had died while praying, and his body had remained **incorrupt** in the temple. A notice nearby confirmed the fact. His conclusion: _"When you are connected to a higher power, this operates everywhere, in all religions — but it is a thousand times more frequent among Catholics."_

He also cites the case of **Jacinta Marto**, one of the three child seers of Fatima, who died during the Spanish flu pandemic. Her body was covered in quicklime — used at the time for all flu victims to accelerate decomposition — yet when her sealed, lead-lined coffin was opened some 20–30 years later, she was found **perfectly incorrupt**, her skin having resisted the lime. He calls this "God's dark humour."

He mentions personally having seen the **incorrupt body of Saint Catherine of Siena** in Siena's cathedral approximately 20 years earlier, before it was moved from public view.

---

## Relations with the Church: Persecution and Imprisonment

Padre Pio's relationship with the institutional Church was deeply troubled, particularly during the pontificate of **Pope John XXIII**. Jovanovic is sharply critical of John XXIII, describing him as someone who did not believe in miracles, dismissed the Fatima apparitions as nonsense, and was allegedly a member of a Rosicrucian or Masonic organisation. He also makes accusations about John XXIII's personal conduct.

His assessment: when Padre Pio's fame began to exceed that of the Pope himself, John XXIII had him effectively imprisoned — barred from public ministry.

Jovanovic identifies a recurring pattern across all the mystics he has studied: **whenever supernatural manifestations become too visible, the institutional Church's first response is to declare them diabolical**. He recounts that when he himself began his research into guardian angels for his book, the first thing priests told him was: _"Are you sure you are not interested in the Devil?"_ He draws a parallel to the contemporary political habit of labelling anyone who disagrees with the establishment as a "conspiracist" or "extremist."

He also cites the case of **Archbishop Gagliardi**, who publicly declared of Padre Pio: _"He is possessed by a demon, and the priests who live in incredible luxury are nothing but a band of thieves"_ — accusing Pio of having stolen a statue of the Virgin. Jovanovic notes that many of Pio's enemies within the Church were, in his telling, motivated by jealousy over the enormous flow of donations arriving from around the world, particularly from the United States, which funded the construction of a hospital at San Giovanni Rotondo (still operating today, though later infiltrated by the Italian mafia for financial fraud).

---

## Padre Pio and Karol Wojtyła (the Future John Paul II)

In **1947**, the young Polish priest **Karol Wojtyła** — later to become **Pope John Paul II** — travelled to San Giovanni Rotondo, having heard of Padre Pio's reputation. During his confession, Padre Pio told him directly and without hesitation:

> _"One day, you will be Pope."_

Wojtyła thus knew from 1947 that he would become Pope, though not under what circumstances — he could not have known this would involve the rise of **Lech Wałęsa** and the **Solidarity movement** (_Solidarność_), the political and financial support channelled by the Americans into that movement, and the broader collapse of communist Poland.

Jovanovic notes that this prophecy is one reason John Paul II was so personally motivated to pursue Padre Pio's canonisation, which he completed in **2002**.

---

## The Prophecy Concerning Pope John Paul I

In a related prophetic episode, Jovanovic recounts (attributing it to a book — possibly _Notre-Dame de l'Apocalypse_) that **Karol Wojtyła**, before becoming pope, visited the elderly seer of Fatima — **Sister Lucia** — who also possessed prophetic gifts. She told him:

> _"For you, the days of Christ."_

Christ lived exactly **33 years**. John Paul I, who preceded Wojtyła, reigned for precisely **33 days** before dying — in Jovanovic's telling, poisoned in order to recover money held by the Vatican bank, specifically in connection with the **Banco Ambrosiano** scandal, the death of banker **Roberto Calvi** (found hanging under Blackfriars Bridge in London), and the **P2 Masonic lodge** which included Vatican cardinals and financiers. He attributes the trilogy of films by **Francis Ford Coppola** (beginning with _The Godfather_, starring Marlon Brando) as having been inspired by this world.

---

## The CIA Remote Viewing Parallel

A recurring theme in Jovanovic's work is the connection between the abilities of Catholic mystics and the findings of the **CIA's remote viewing programme**, which he covers in detail in his book _888_. He argues:

- Padre Pio's ability to see past and future events, leave his body, and access remote locations was not unique to Catholicism — it is a human capacity.
- The CIA demonstrated this through its programme, which trained agents using meditation techniques similar to those of Tibetan monks.
- However, the CIA's trained agents are, in Jovanovic's analogy, **amateurs who can finish a marathon**, while figures like Padre Pio and Charbel Makhlouf are **world champions** — operating at an entirely different level.
- The CIA work provided Jovanovic with a secular, scientific framework for understanding and presenting what he had previously thought was purely a gift reserved for saints.
- This capacity is available to anyone who maintains a serious spiritual life and "stays connected."

---

## Padre Pio as the Model for Don Camillo

Jovanovic notes that Padre Pio served as the **model for Don Camillo** — the famously blunt, argumentative fictional village priest created by **Giovanni Guareschi**, known for his direct verbal confrontations with God and his battles with the local communist mayor. He describes this as apt: Padre Pio genuinely did argue with God, as attested by those who knew him.

---

## The Question of Violence: Did Padre Pio Strike People?

The host raises accounts of Padre Pio distributing slaps — some accounts describe light taps on the head, others describe harder blows. Jovanovic confirms he was a **gruff, rough-mannered character** — in the tradition of rural Italian priests of his era. He compares this to Don Camillo, who also distributes blows to the communists in his village. He contrasts this with what he describes as the **effeminate** manner of most contemporary priests, and finds Padre Pio's bluntness entirely characteristic of his type and time.

---

## Canonisation (2002)

Padre Pio was **canonised in 2002 by John Paul II** — the very pope to whom he had prophesied in 1947. Jovanovic sees this as the natural completion of a circle: having been told by Pio that he would become pope, Wojtyła was personally invested in the cause and uniquely motivated to see it through.

---

## Comparative Note: Speed of Canonisation Processes

Jovanovic observes that canonisation timelines vary enormously:

- **Gemma Galgani** (Italian mystic, patron of pharmacists): canonised at remarkable speed
- **Thérèse of Lisieux**: similarly fast
- **Charbel Makhlouf** (Lebanon): took approximately a century or 80 years
- **Therese Neumann**: still not canonised; currently only Servant of God — Jovanovic attributes this to Vatican hostility toward women and to tensions between German Catholics and the Vatican

He states that for him personally, **Charbel Makhlouf** surpasses all others — including Thérèse of Lisieux and even Padre Pio — and promises a future episode dedicated to him.

---

## Closing Reflection

Jovanovic concludes with an observation he considers central to his 30 years of reader correspondence and research: **miraculous phenomena of the type associated with Catholic saints do occur in other traditions**, but are "a thousand times more frequent" in Catholicism — a fact he regards as significant evidence in itself. He attributes this frequency to the particular intensity of Catholic spiritual life and its continuous, unbroken sacramental tradition.

The episode closes with an invitation to viewers to post questions in the comments for possible inclusion in the next episode.
