# Therese Neumann: The Female Padre Pio

## Summary of Interview — _Les Rendez-vous de Kate_ with Pierre Jovanovic (Episode 7)

---

## Format & Participants

This is episode 7 of the French-language series _Les Rendez-vous de Kate_, hosted by Kate (last name not given), featuring **Pierre Jovanovic** — journalist, economist, author, and director of the publishing house _Le Jardin des Livres_, known for his weekly economic press review. The episode was recorded in late September 2024, marking the return from a summer break.

---

## Who Was Therese Neumann?

**Therese Neumann** (1898–1962) was a German Catholic mystic from **Konnersreuth**, Bavaria. She is described by Jovanovic as the "female Padre Pio" and generated extraordinary public interest during her lifetime — over **700 books** were written about her while she was still alive. Her case bridges the pre-modern and modern worlds: born in a rural, largely pre-electricity environment, she lived long enough to be photographed, filmed, and even recorded on the first magnetic tape machines.

---

## Early Life and Miraculous Healing

Neumann came from a poor, uneducated background. She spoke not standard German but a **Bavarian dialect**, and had almost no formal schooling. She was deeply devoted to **St. Thérèse of Lisieux** (the Little Flower).

Her path to mystical life began with a physical accident: she fell from a stool while trying to pass buckets during a fire, sustaining injuries that progressively worsened until she was completely bedridden and immobile. Despite the best medical care available at the time (which Jovanovic notes was not far removed from the medicine of Molière's era), her condition could not be treated.

She was then **miraculously and suddenly healed on the day of the canonization of Thérèse of Lisieux at the Vatican** — to the astonishment of all her physicians. Jovanovic calls this her "Formula 1 qualification lap" — an opening that signalled what was to come.

---

## The Stigmata and the Passion Visions

Following her healing, Neumann received the **stigmata** — wounds corresponding to those of Christ at the Crucifixion. Photographs and early film footage document these visibly: she is seen bleeding from her face, arms, and hands. She also wept **tears of blood**, something Jovanovic describes as occurring in cases of extreme psychological and spiritual suffering.

Most remarkably, she regularly **relived the Passion of Christ**, reportedly doing so approximately **600 times** over her lifetime, each Friday. During these episodes:

- She entered a deeply altered state, enacting the experience as though physically present.
- She spoke aloud what she heard and saw, allowing observers to document her account in real time.
- These accounts were captured both in written transcripts (interrogations and interviews) and on **early magnetic tape recordings** — one of the first uses of this technology for such documentation.

The resulting testimony forms the basis for the book _The New Testament Through the Visions of Therese Neumann_, which Jovanovic's publishing house produced. He describes having wept while reading the corrected translation manuscript — calling it "like a camera travelling through time."

---

## The Aramaic Phenomenon

One of the most scientifically striking aspects of Neumann's case involves **language**. During her visions, observers noted she was speaking in an unknown tongue. It eventually took:

- A priest specialising in **biblical archaeology** and ancient languages, and
- Additional specialists in **Oriental languages from Berlin**

...to confirm that she was speaking **Aramaic of the period of Jesus** — not modern Aramaic, but the specific dialect of first-century Palestine.

She had never studied Aramaic, had minimal formal education, and spoke only her Bavarian dialect in daily life. Jovanovic cites the words she reproduced from the Archangel Gabriel's annunciation to Mary: _"Shalom, Miriam, Gazetta"_ — identified by linguists as authentic Aramaic phrasing.

He notes: "No one who accuses her of fraud has ever explained how she spoke Aramaic."

---

## Remote Viewing and Out-of-Body Travel

A key theme of the interview is the parallel Jovanovic draws between Neumann and the **CIA's remote viewing programme** (discussed in his book _888_ and an earlier episode on the subject). He argues that Neumann had the same documented ability as trained CIA remote viewers:

- **Leaving her body** and travelling to other locations or times
- **Viewing the past** — including events from 2,000 years ago (the Passion)
- **Viewing the future** — including prophetic announcements about the United States and Nazi Germany
- **Accessing knowledge she had no natural means of obtaining**

He concludes: "She could have been the CIA's number one asset for remote viewing."

He also draws a connection to the film **Coppola's _Youth Without Youth_** (_L'Homme sans âge_), noting the film was inspired by the Romanian writer **Mircea Eliade**, who was himself influenced by Neumann's case.

---

## Prophecies About Nazi Germany

Neumann became extraordinarily famous throughout Germany in the 1920s and 1930s because she publicly and repeatedly predicted — **before Hitler had even come to power** — that he would bring Germany to complete ruin. She actively encouraged journalists who came to interview her to warn the German public and to resist the regime.

The consequences were severe:

- At least one journalist who wrote positively about her and reported her warnings was **murdered by the Gestapo**.
- Thousands of articles were written about her across the 1920s and 1930s in the German, French, Spanish, Italian, and American press.

The Nazi regime's response was contradictory and confused: they themselves were deeply invested in the occult and the supernatural, making it difficult for them to simply dismiss her. Their strategy was instead to:

1. **Commission hostile "scientists"** to declare her a fraud.
2. **Pay propagandists** to produce books and articles denouncing her (calling her, among other things, "the wife of a circus clown").
3. **Persecute her associates** and the journalists who supported her.
4. **Conduct a 15-day clinical surveillance** to disprove her claim of living without food.

---

## The Nazi Fasting Experiment

In one of the most documented episodes of Neumann's life, the Nazi authorities **confined her to a hospital for 15 days under 24-hour surveillance** to test her claim that she consumed nothing but the daily Eucharist (the consecrated host given by a priest).

Results:

- She was weighed daily.
- She received only the Eucharist, administered by different priests.
- At the end of the first week she had lost approximately 2–3 kg.
- By the end of 15 days her weight had returned to exactly **55 kg** — her weight on admission.
- No food intake was observed or recorded.

The Nazi authorities were, in Jovanovic's words, "completely disoriented" by the results. They had confirmed what her supporters claimed, while being ideologically unable to accept a supernatural explanation.

Near the end of the war, Nazi forces reportedly attempted to **destroy her village of Konnersreuth** in revenge for her accurate predictions of the regime's fall, but American forces advanced so rapidly that they were unable to carry it out.

---

## Her Character and Public Presence

Unlike most mystics, who lived in seclusion, Neumann was — as Jovanovic puts it — "furiously modern" in her openness:

- She **received journalists, researchers, and the curious** openly, allowing herself to be observed, photographed, filmed, and interviewed.
- She received **thousands of letters per month** from around the world as a result of the press coverage, and spent nights with her household responding to all of them.
- She had a notable **sense of humour**. The journalist Annie Spiegel, one of her close companions, recounts that when a sceptic suggested her stigmata were the result of **autosuggestion**, Neumann replied: _"You are convinced I imagined these wounds into existence. In that case, try imagining that you want horns. They will probably grow on your head."_

She had a notably **gentle, angelic appearance** in her photographs, which Jovanovic encourages viewers to seek out.

---

## Her Death

Neumann died in 1962 in the same manner Jovanovic observes is common to many mystics — from a relatively minor illness (pneumonia or myocarditis), simply, after a life of extraordinary supernatural experience and suffering.

---

## Her Tomb Today

Neumann's tomb in Konnersreuth is described as **perpetually covered in flowers** — overflowing to the ceiling of the space, comparable to the tombs of **Saint Charbel Makhlouf** (Lebanon) and **Padre Pio** (Italy). Jovanovic interprets this as evidence that she continues to respond to prayers after death, in the same manner as other saints.

He encourages French viewers who work in Germany or travel there to visit her tomb.

---

## Beatification and the Vatican

Neumann currently holds the title of **Servant of God** — the first and lowest level of the Catholic canonisation process — but has not been beatified or canonised. Jovanovic is sharply critical of the Vatican's reluctance, attributing it to ideological hostility and pointing to the Vatican's historically compromised record regarding the post-war sheltering of Nazi officers (the so-called "rat lines," facilitated in part by Franciscan monks in Zagreb and others).

He had publicly predicted, when his publishing house released the books about her, that beatification was imminent. He acknowledges this has not occurred.

---

## Incorruptibility

Jovanovic speculates that if Neumann's coffin were opened, her body would likely be found **incorrupt** — in the same condition as the bodies of other stigmatised saints. He notes this is not guaranteed for all stigmatics but draws comparison to:

- **Saint Catherine of Siena**, whose body he personally viewed approximately 20 years earlier when it was still displayed in the cathedral in Siena, and which he describes as completely incorrupt.
- **Saint Charbel Makhlouf**, whose incorruption he has discussed in a previous episode.

He offers a theological interpretation: mystics who have lived "outside of time" through their supernatural experiences are also, in some sense, protected from time after death — attributing this to the action of the Holy Spirit.

---

## Comparison to Anne Catherine Emmerich

Jovanovic explicitly compares Neumann to **Anne Catherine Emmerich** (1774–1824), another German stigmatic and visionary whose detailed visions of the Passion served as the primary source for **Mel Gibson's _The Passion of the Christ_** (with a sequel announced as forthcoming). Both women accessed the same events through the same type of remote visionary experience.

---

## CIA Remote Viewing Parallel

Jovanovic returns repeatedly to the connection between Neumann's abilities and those of CIA-trained remote viewers (discussed in his book _888_):

- CIA agents working in the remote viewing programme were trained using meditation techniques comparable to those of Tibetan monks.
- They were able to leave their bodies and access remote locations, as well as past and future events.
- Their documented abilities closely mirror what Neumann did spontaneously and repeatedly throughout her life.
- The CIA programme thus, unintentionally, provides a secular and scientific framework that **validates** the phenomenon observed in Neumann, Padre Pio, Anne Catherine Emmerich, and others.

## Viewer Comments (Archangel Gabriel Episode Follow-Up)

Two viewer responses from the previous episode (on the Archangel Gabriel) were read:

**1. Julien Herg** — A self-described Cartesian sceptic who bought several of Jovanovic's books after doubting him. The first part of _Enquête sur l'existence des anges gardiens_ did not convince him, but after following Jovanovic's advice to speak daily to his guardian angel, he experienced a synchronicity: the very day he began, his young daughter came home from a leisure centre carrying a pendant in the shape of an angel she had found on a bench, which she gave to him as a gift.

Jovanovic responds with characteristic bluntness, noting his exasperation with self-declared Cartesians — and pointing out that Descartes himself recounted in his memoirs having received three increasingly insistent visits in dreams from his guardian angel, who showed him the scientific method. "Cartesianism comes from an angel," he says.

**2. Odile Pastourel** — A woman who shares a personal testimony: before the birth of her second son, she was advised by her family to abort due to health problems. During a meditation, she was visited by a Divine Being seen from behind, with long hair and a flowing garment, whose outstretched hands filled with a shower of golden sparks that formed a baby. She did not know who this being was until watching the episode on the Archangel Gabriel, which she now believes was the annunciation she received. She kept the child. Her son is now 38 years old and, she writes, "very close to God."
