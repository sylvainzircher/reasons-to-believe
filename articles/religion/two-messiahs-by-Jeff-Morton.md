# _Two Messiahs: The Jesus of Christianity and the Jesus of Islam_

**by Jeff Morton (Jeffrey Jay Morton)**
Published: InterVarsity Press, April 6, 2011 | 163 pages

---

## Overview & Premise

The book opens with a striking premise: Muslims love Jesus. When you meet a Muslim, you meet someone who loves Jesus — Muslims insist that a true Muslim must love Jesus, and that Jesus is not the sole property of Christians. But is that really so?

Morton sets the Jesus of Islam — _ʿĪsā ibn Maryam_, Muhammad's 7th-century conception of the 1st-century prophet and Messiah — in direct conversation with the first-century, historic Jesus of Nazareth.

---

## The Literary Device: A Walk from Emmaus to Jerusalem

The reader encounters ʿĪsā and Jesus on a long walk from the little town of Emmaus to Jerusalem — a journey filled with conversation, questions, brief interactions with other people along the road, and observations from each man about the other. This setting is a deliberate biblical echo: the road to Emmaus is where, in the Gospel of Luke, the risen Jesus walked with two disciples who initially didn't recognize him. By the end of the walk, it is clear to the reader who Jesus is in both Islam and Christianity.

---

## The Two Characters

The book presents two figures who share a name and are venerated by billions, yet are fundamentally different:

### Jesus of Christianity

The true Messiah, the Son of God, divine in nature. He speaks from the perspective of the New Testament and biblical theology, asserting his divinity, his atoning death, and his resurrection.

### ʿĪsā of Islam

A man and servant of his god. In Islamic theology, ʿĪsā is a great prophet, born of a virgin, who performed miracles — but he is not divine, was not crucified, and did not rise from the dead. He is a forerunner to Muhammad, not the Son of God.

---

## The Dialogue & Theological Tensions

The conversation along the road draws out the core theological differences between the two faiths. The dialogue covers topics including:

- **Christology** — the nature of Jesus/ʿĪsā: divine vs. human
- **The crucifixion and resurrection** — accepted in Christianity, denied in Islam
- **The Gospel vs. the Qur'an** — authority and revelation
- **The nature of God / Allah** — Trinity vs. strict monotheism
- **The role of Muhammad** — prophet of God vs. irrelevant to Christianity
- **The Holy Spirit** — its identity and role in each tradition
- **Forgiveness and salvation** — how each religion understands redemption
- **The Dajjal** — the Islamic antichrist figure and its eschatological role
- **Abraham, Moses, and the prophets** — shared reverence, different implications
- **The Torah, the Passover, and scripture** — how each tradition reads the same texts

The two figures find common ground on certain surface-level things — both revere Abraham, Moses, and the prophets — but diverge sharply on the nature of Jesus: his divinity, his death on the cross, and his resurrection.

---

## Morton's Argument

The author's position is that most misunderstandings between Christianity and Islam stem from a lack of information. By putting the two figures in direct, respectful dialogue, Morton allows the differences to emerge naturally rather than through polemical argument.

The book aims to teach Christians about Islam while also revealing to Muslims why Jesus of Nazareth is, from the author's perspective, the true Son of God sent to bring salvation to the world. Morton shines light on the confusions and lack of knowledge surrounding both traditions.

---

## The Appendices

The appendices at the conclusion of the book provide additional ways of exploring the differences between the two figures, offering further theological insight beyond the narrative itself — giving readers a scholarly reference to complement the story format.

---

## Tone & Reception

The differences between Islam's and Christianity's understanding of Jesus are exposed in a manner that is described by reviewers as respectful to Islam, honoring to Christ, and scholarly in its substance. Reviewers praised it as accessible and readable, with one noting it was "a helpful way to compare the differences in a story format" rather than through dry theological argument.

Endorsements from Biola University colleagues highlight both its readability and its theological clarity:

- _"A delightful discussion held by two competing messiahs that results in clarity."_ — Tom Steffen, Biola University
- _"A valuable resource for understanding the difference between the Jesus of the Qur'an and the Jesus of the Bible."_

## About the Author

Jeff Morton is a Professor of Intercultural Studies at Biola University. He has been ministering to Muslims since 1984, first in West Africa and later in Southern California near one of the largest mosques and Muslim communities on the West Coast. His background includes extensive research in apologetics and theology, with a particular interest in presenting the Gospel to those of Islamic backgrounds. He is a member of SIM and i2 Ministries, and has trained missionaries and university students in the U.S., Brazil, Thailand, and Pakistan.
