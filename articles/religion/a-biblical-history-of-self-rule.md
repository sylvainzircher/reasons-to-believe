# Church & Government: A Biblical History of Self-Rule

> **Summary of sermon/lecture tracing the theological and historical roots of American republican government, and the Christian obligation to engage in civic life.**

---

## 1. The Default Form of Government: Kings and Gangs

Across 6,000 years of recorded history, the dominant form of government has been concentrated power — kings, pharaohs, emperors, sultans. Power flows top-down, enforced by fear. The first biblical example is **Nimrod** (Tower of Babel), described as history's first globalist, seeking a one-world government.

God's response — confusing languages and scattering peoples into nations — is framed as a divine check on concentrated power. Nations were God's invention to postpone one-world government.

---

## 2. Ancient Israel as the Original Republic

Ancient Israel (circa **1400–1000 BC**) is presented as history's first notable example of millions of people governing themselves without a king.

**Key features of the "Hebrew Republic":**

- Everyone was taught the law — making Israel the **first literate population in history**, thanks to Moses' simple 22-character alphabet
- The Levite priests functioned as educators, not rulers
- Order was maintained not through fear of a king, but through **conscience** — the belief that a just God watches everyone and holds them accountable
- The annual **Day of Atonement** reset the community's slate, foreshadowing Jesus as the ultimate atonement

This system worked for roughly **400 years** — until the priests stopped teaching the law and the people demanded a king. God told Samuel: _"They have not rejected you — they have rejected me."_

---

## 3. The Bible as Blueprint: Before and After King Saul

A sharp distinction is drawn between two ways of reading the Bible politically:

| Tradition                    | Biblical Reference Point | Model                                    |
| ---------------------------- | ------------------------ | ---------------------------------------- |
| European Kings / England     | After King Saul          | Top-down monarchy / theocracy            |
| American Puritans / Pilgrims | Before King Saul         | Self-governance, personal accountability |

This becomes the theological dividing line between the English model and the American model of government.

---

## 4. The Reformation, the Printing Press, and Congregational Government

The **Protestant Reformation (1517)** and the **printing press** democratized the Bible — everyone could now own and read one. This sparked:

- **Congregational church structure** — bottom-up, where the congregation governs itself rather than submitting to a hierarchy
- **Christian Hebraism** — Puritan scholars studying Hebrew to understand Israel's pre-monarchic political philosophy

**Notable examples:**

- James Madison studied Hebrew for a full year after graduating Princeton (1771), focusing on Israel's political philosophy
- Harvard and Yale taught Hebrew from 1685
- Harvard's commencement address was delivered partly in Hebrew

---

## 5. Puritans Founding America — Church and State as One

When the King of England pressured the Anglican church to conform, **20,000 Puritans fled to New England**. Pastors and churches literally founded cities:

- **Thomas Hooker** → founded Hartford, Connecticut. His sermon became the **Fundamental Orders of 1639** — the first written constitution creating a government, used until 1818
- **Roger Williams** → founded Providence, Rhode Island

Since churches _founded_ the cities, church members _were_ the city government. The "meeting house" served as both church and town hall — just as the synagogue did in ancient Israel. The idea of Christians staying out of politics is therefore historically and theologically incoherent.

> _"How could you say pastor don't preach on politics when it's the pastor's sermon that's their constitution?"_

---

## 6. America's Constitutional Design

The Founders took the concentrated power of a king and:

- Split it into **three branches**
- Separated it **federally to state level**
- Restrained it with the **Bill of Rights** (the "10 handcuffs")

This is framed as scattering the Tower of Babel — dispersing power among the people rather than concentrating it at the top.

**On Romans 13** ("submit to governing authorities"):

> In a monarchy, the authority is the king.
> In a republic, the authority is **the citizen**.

"Citizen" means co-ruler, co-sovereign, co-king. Christian civic engagement is not optional — it is obedience to the same scriptural principle applied to a different system of government.

---

## 7. The Pietist Problem — Two Kingdoms, Christian Withdrawal

**Pietism** — a revival movement within Lutheranism — emphasized personal faith and withdrawal from "worldly" things, including government. While credited for spreading genuine revival, its political consequence was damaging: if all Christians withdraw from government, only non-Christians remain to run it.

> _"It's a self-fulfilling prophecy. Why is government worldly? Because all the spiritual people left."_

**The historical consequence:** Four centuries of "stay out of government" teaching in Germany helped enable Hitler's rise, with churches standing by silently as Jews were loaded onto trains.

**The Muhlenberg Brothers — a case study:**

- **John Peter Muhlenberg** (Pietist pastor) heard Patrick Henry's speech, joined Washington's army, rose to general, then served in Congress. His statue stands in the U.S. Capitol — depicted removing his clerical robe to reveal a Continental officer's uniform.
- **Frederick Augustus Muhlenberg** (also Pietist) initially refused to get involved — until the British burned his church. He became the **first Speaker of the U.S. House of Representatives** and helped pass the First Amendment.

Two pastors passed the First Amendment. They were not voting to silence themselves — they were placing handcuffs on the federal government.

---

## 8. The Doctrine of Silence as Consent

Silence is not neutrality. Scripture and law both treat silence as consent.

**Biblical examples:**

- **Numbers 30** — a father's silence ratifies his daughter's vows
- **Aaron at Meribah** — he said nothing when Moses struck the rock twice, and was barred from the Promised Land (sin of omission, not commission)
- **Paul at Stephen's stoning** — later acknowledged guilt for "consenting" by standing silently by
- **Leviticus 5** — failing to speak up as a witness makes you guilty
- **Leviticus 20** — closing your eyes while children are sacrificed makes you complicit
- **Esther** — Mordecai warns: _"If you remain silent at this time, deliverance will come from another place, but you and your father's family will perish."_

**Legal parallels:**

- Statute of limitations in debt collection
- Adverse possession in real estate law
- Trademark abandonment
- Article I, Section 7 of the Constitution — a bill unsigned for 10 days automatically becomes law

> _Martin Luther King Jr.: "He who accepts evil without protesting it is really cooperating with it."_

> _Leviticus 19:17-18 — "Confront your neighbor directly so you will not be held guilty for their sin" — immediately precedes "Love your neighbor as yourself."_

---

## 9. The Call to Action — Local Politics, School Boards

The practical application is **local engagement**, especially school board elections:

- More people attend church in most communities than vote in school board races
- You do not need a child in public school to run for or vote in school board elections
- Churches across denominations can unite around protecting children even where they disagree on doctrine

> _"As power concentrates into fewer hands globally, God's counterbalance is to get more people involved locally."_

**The current moment** is framed as a stress test — God exposing evil to reveal the condition of people's hearts. Ezekiel's vision is cited: only those who "sigh and cry" over their city's abominations receive the protective mark.

---

## 10. The Closing Challenge — Your Story in Heaven

The sermon closes with an imaginative scenario: arriving in heaven and being asked by Moses, David, Esther, and Paul — _"What did you do when it was your turn on earth?"_

The warning: don't let **fear of man** — fear of social rejection, negative posts online, exclusion from a group — hold you back from courageous action. The early church, when persecuted, prayed not for safety but for _boldness_.

> _"No coach saves the slowest runner to the end of the relay. He saves the fastest. I think all of heaven is sitting on the bleachers, and Jesus is saying: Watch this. I'm going to send in the best."_

---

## Core Thesis

> The American republic is a direct descendant of the Hebrew Republic, transmitted through Puritan covenant theology. Christians are not merely permitted but **obligated** to engage in civic life. Silence is not neutrality — it is consent. The Pietist impulse to withdraw from politics is historically dangerous and theologically unsound. The most important thing is bringing people to Christ; the second most important thing is **preserving the freedom to do the most important thing**.
