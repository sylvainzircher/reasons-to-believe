# The Problem of Evil — A Christian Response

## Overview

This video offers an in-depth Christian apologetic response to the problem of evil — widely considered the most powerful objection to the existence of God. The presenter argues that the problem of evil cannot be answered by philosophy alone and that the full Christian worldview, including the gospel, is necessary to address it adequately.

---

## 1. What Is the Problem of Evil?

The problem of evil has been used for millennia to argue against God's existence. It comes in two main forms:

### The Logical Problem of Evil (Epicurus)

- **Premise 1:** If God is all-good, he wants to stop evil.
- **Premise 2:** If God is all-powerful, he can stop evil.
- **Premise 3:** Evil exists.
- **Conclusion:** Therefore, an all-powerful and all-good God does not exist.

This version is largely **abandoned by modern atheistic philosophers** because it ignores God's omniscience — his potentially having good reasons for allowing evil that humans cannot perceive.

### The Evidential Problem of Evil (Paul Draper)

- **Premise 1:** Gratuitous evil exists.
- **Premise 2:** The "hypothesis of indifference" (that supernatural beings are indifferent to suffering) better explains gratuitous evil than theism does.
- **Conclusion:** Evidence therefore favours the non-existence of God as theists conceive him.

This probabilistic argument is where the real modern philosophical debate lies.

---

## 2. The Free Will Defence

Alvin Plantinga's free will defence responds to the logical problem by arguing:

- A world with significantly free creatures who perform more good than evil is **more valuable** than a world with no free creatures at all.
- God can create free creatures, but **cannot cause them to do only what is right** — if he did, they would not be free.
- To create creatures capable of moral good, God must also permit the possibility of moral evil.
- The existence of moral evil therefore counts neither against God's omnipotence nor his goodness.

Paul Draper concedes this shows it is **at least logically possible** for God to coexist with evil. William Rowe similarly admits it is a "fairly compelling argument" for the logical consistency of God and evil.

---

## 3. Human Nature and Total Depravity

The presenter argues the problem of evil must be understood in light of **human nature** — specifically the Christian doctrine of total depravity. Key points:

- Genocide is not _inhumane_ — it is profoundly, disturbingly _human_.
- The 20th century alone produced more mass murders than any prior era: the Holocaust, the Rape of Nanking, the Soviet gulags, Rwanda, Cambodia, Armenia, the Congo, and many more.
- Most perpetrators of atrocities were **ordinary people**, not monsters or sociopaths.
- The Milgram experiments demonstrated that **65–90% of ordinary people** will administer apparently lethal electric shocks when instructed by authority figures.
- Holocaust survivor and professor Freddie Katz concludes that the vast majority of mass killings are carried out by "plain folk in the population."
- Langdon Gilkey, who believed in humanity's innate goodness until interned by the Japanese, concluded that this belief was "totally in error."
- A recent paper on prehistoric murder rates suggests the human population bottleneck (~5,000–7,000 years ago) may have been driven partly by very high rates of killing.

The conclusion: the Bible's claim that humans are totally depraved is **empirically well-supported**, not a slander.

---

## 4. There Are No Good People

Building on human nature, the presenter addresses the complaint that "bad things happen to good people":

- Much of human behaviour is driven by **self-interest**, even acts of apparent heroism or altruism.
- As the Scriptures state: "There is none righteous, no not one."
- The Christian answer is stark: _there are no good people for bad things to happen to._
- D.A. Carson's exegesis of Luke 13 (the tower of Siloam) shows Jesus taught that all death is in some sense the result of sin and that it is only God's mercy that keeps anyone alive.
- The real question is not "Why does God allow so much evil?" but **"Why does God not simply wipe us out?"**

---

## 5. Why God Does Not Constantly Intervene

Several reasons are offered for why God does not prevent every evil act:

- **Freedom requires real consequences.** A world where God corrected every misuse of free will would make wrong actions impossible, making freedom meaningless (C.S. Lewis).
- **No known better alternative exists.** When Richard Norman was pressed by Clay Jones to describe how God could grant free will without allowing harm, Norman admitted he could not. If critics cannot imagine a better world, it is at least logically possible none exists.
- **Middle knowledge (Molinism).** Given God's knowledge of all possible free choices, there may be no possible world where humans are free and evil does not occur. God actualises the world with the least evil compatible with genuine freedom.
- **Suffering can lead to growth and return to God.** William Lane Craig and J.P. Moreland note that innocent suffering deepens dependency and trust in God. History shows Christianity tends to grow most in periods of severe hardship (e.g. China under communism, now set to be the world's largest Christian nation by 2030).
- **No principled line can be drawn.** If God halved the Holocaust, would 600,000 deaths be acceptable? 6,000? Critics who want "less evil" cannot specify a threshold, revealing the objection is more emotional than philosophical.

---

## 6. Eternity Changes the Calculus

The problem of evil argument often **presupposes a non-theistic worldview** by treating death as final. On the Christian worldview:

- Death is not the end — those who die, including children, can live on in eternity.
- The joy of heaven will dwarf the suffering of this life, as adult pain dwarfs the pain of a childhood fall.
- C.S. Lewis: heaven, once attained, will work backwards and turn even agony into glory.

---

## 7. The Cross as the Ultimate Answer

The presenter argues that the gospel — specifically the cross — is the deepest response to the problem of evil:

- God did not remain distant from suffering. In Christ, God **entered into human pain, was tortured, and died**.
- Atheist philosopher Albert Camus acknowledged that Christ's suffering means evil and death can no longer be entirely imputed to God.
- At the cross, justice and mercy met: God bore the sentence of justice on himself so that mercy could fall on humanity.
- N.T. Wright: the Gospels tell a story unique in world literature — of the Creator taking responsibility for creation's brokenness and bearing its weight himself.
- You cannot honestly evaluate the problem of evil while ignoring the cross.

## Summary of the Argument

| Stage                   | Key Point                                                     |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Logical problem of evil | Refuted by Plantinga's free will defence                      |
| Evidential problem      | Answered by human depravity, middle knowledge, and eternity   |
| Human nature            | We are not good people — depravity is empirically verifiable  |
| Free will               | A world without it would be worse, not better                 |
| God's intervention      | Would undermine freedom; no critic can propose a better world |
| Eternity                | Suffering here is finite; joy in heaven is infinite           |
| The cross               | God himself suffered evil and bore its penalty                |
