# Is the New Testament Reliable? — A Complete Series Summary

## Overview

This is a multi-part YouTube series making a cumulative case for the reliability of the New Testament. It covers: manuscript evidence and textual variants, oral tradition, authorship and dating of the gospels, external corroboration (secular sources, archaeology, historical coherence), internal evidence (non-theological content, embarrassing passages, undesigned coincidences), and responses to common objections (bias, illiteracy, miracles, contradictions).

---

## Part 1 — The Manuscript Evidence

### What We Have

- **5,800+ Greek manuscripts** of the New Testament.
- **10,000+ Latin manuscripts**.
- **5,000–10,000** manuscripts in other languages.
- **1 million+ New Testament quotations** in the writings of the church fathers alone — enough to reconstruct the entire New Testament even without a single manuscript.

No individual or group ever had centralised control over the text. The early church was a persecuted minority with no power to enforce uniform textual transmission. This is in stark contrast to Islam, which had an "Uthman" — a single controlling authority who standardised the Quranic text. Any large-scale corruption of the New Testament would be traceable by comparing manuscripts from different regions.

**Comparison with other ancient literature:**

- The Iliad (second most attested): 1,757 copies.
- Horace: ~200 copies.
- Tacitus's _Annals_: first attributed to him only by Jerome; Suetonius not attributed until Pollio, 200+ years later.
- The New Testament dwarfs all other ancient documents in manuscript attestation.

**Dan Wallace**: "If I'm going to be sceptical about the New Testament and I apply that scepticism to other Greco-Roman literature, we immediately go back to the Dark Ages."

### Proximity to the Originals

- **P52** (fragment): c. 90–125 AD — within 70 years or less of the original.
- **Several larger fragments**: c. 170–220 AD.
- **P75**: ~102 surviving pages from Luke and John; c. 150–200 AD.
- **Oldest complete New Testament**: within 300 years of the original — closer than any other ancient document.
- **~12 fragmented manuscripts** dating to the 2nd century represent ~40% of the entire New Testament.
- **120 manuscripts** within ~300 years of the original.

Compare: earliest copy of the Iliad is 500 years from its original; Suetonius is 800 years from his.

### Responding to Bart Ehrman's Objection

Ehrman claims: "We don't even have copies of copies of copies of the originals."

Responses:

- P52 is within 70 years of the original — it could easily be a first- or second-generation copy. Papyrus in public use lasts on average 100+ years.
- Ehrman himself acknowledges a manuscript can plausibly be a direct copy of one from 100 years prior.
- Tertullian (writing c. 200 AD) says apostolic churches still possessed "authentic writings" — the Latin _authentica_ normally refers to originals. He specifically mentions letters to Corinth, Philippi, Thessalonica, Ephesus, and Rome, and urges readers to visit to see them.
- Irenaeus testifies he had access to some of the earliest copies of Revelation, suggesting early copies were being preserved for accuracy.
- Scribes did not destroy the originals after copying. Manuscripts were valued and kept.
- **P75 and Codex Vaticanus (B)** have exceptionally strong agreement. P75 is ~125 years older than B, yet B was not copied from P75 but from an even earlier common ancestor. Work by C. Porter suggests their shared readings go back to the very beginning of the 2nd century.
- **Darrell Bock and Dan Wallace**: the earliest manuscripts we have probably go back to c. 100 AD.

### The Variant Problem

Total variants across all NT manuscripts: ~400,000. However:

- With ~6,000 Greek manuscripts = ~2.6 million pages of NT text.
- **One variant per 6.5 pages** — not especially significant.
- **75% of variants**: spelling errors that do not affect meaning.
- **15%**: variation in Greek synonyms and word order that cannot even be translated differently.
- **~9%**: affect meaning but are from late manuscripts, easily resolved by earlier ones.
- **Less than 1%**: affect meaning and are from early manuscripts.
- Of these, **none challenge or affect essential Christian doctrines**.

Ehrman himself concedes: "The position I argue for in _Misquoting Jesus_ does not actually stand at odds with Professor Metzger's position that the essential Christian beliefs are not affected by textual variance in the manuscript tradition of the New Testament."

**Dan Wallace** (in three public debates with Ehrman): "By the way, I think you agree with me, Bart, that essential Christian beliefs are not affected by textual variance — he's never disputed it."

**Textual criticism accuracy**: only ~40 lines of the New Testament remain unresolved, giving an accuracy of **99.5%** — by far the best of any ancient document.

### The Major Variants

**1 John 5:7** (the Comma Johanneum — explicit Trinitarian text): acknowledged by virtually all scholars as a late Latin addition; not in any early Greek manuscripts. Its absence does not threaten the Trinity doctrine, which is established throughout scripture by other passages.

**John 1:18** ("only Son" vs. "only God"): either reading is consistent with Johannine theology; neither changes Christian doctrine.

**Matthew 24:36** ("nor the Son" clause in some manuscripts): even if absent from the original, the parallel in Mark 13:32 has no textual doubt and makes the same point.

**Mark 1:41** ("moved with compassion" vs. "moved with anger"): anger may be the original reading; consistent with other angry episodes in Mark; does not threaten any doctrine.

**Hebrews 2:9** ("by the grace of God" vs. "apart from God"): strong cases for either reading; neither affects Christian doctrine on Christ's atoning death.

**John 7:53–8:11** (pericope adulterae) and **Mark 16:9–20** (longer ending): not in early manuscripts; most scholars do not consider them original. Removing them changes no Christian doctrine. As Bock and Wallace write: "These passages change no fundamental doctrine, no core belief."

All disputed variants are noted openly in study Bibles. This is not secret information.

## Part 2 — Textual Corruption: How Serious Is It?

### The "We Can Never Know" Objection

Sceptics sometimes argue: since we don't have 100% certainty, we can never know what the original said.

Two problems:

1. **Self-refuting**: to claim we can _certainly_ never know is to claim a kind of special knowledge — which is itself a knowledge claim that would be unknowable by the same logic.
2. **Undermines all historical investigation**: no historical or scientific investigation ever yields 100% certainty. The standard is the most logical and parsimonious explanation, not absolute certainty.

### The Extrapolation Objection

Some argue: the highest variance rate is in early manuscripts, so extrapolating backwards means even worse corruption in manuscripts we don't have.

Problems:

- **Association fallacy**: scribal habits of 2nd–3rd century copyists do not necessarily apply to 1st century ones.
- The originals and first-generation copies would still have been available as a crosscheck during the early period.
- In the first century, living apostles and church members could correct errors.
- If you extrapolate the _percentage_ of doctrinal variants (0%) backwards, the result is still 0% doctrinal corruption.

### Why the Transmission Was Reliable Despite Decentralisation

**James White**: "There were multiple lines of transmission coming out of the first century, all confirming the same message... there was never a controlling authority that could change or edit the text. The result is we have to look at textual variance, but that is actually the best way to preserve the text."

Multiple independent transmission lines, all converging on the same text, make coordinated corruption impossible. Any major insertion would have been visible when comparing manuscripts from Egypt, Syria, Rome, and North Africa.

## Part 3 — The Oral Tradition

### The Telephone Game Objection

Sceptics often compare oral transmission to the telephone game. This is a fundamental category error:

- The telephone game is **private** (whispered from ear to ear), **sequential** (one-to-one), and **designed to distort**.
- Ancient oral tradition was **public** (heard by the whole community), **collective** (the whole community could correct errors), and **designed to preserve**.

### How Ancient Oral Culture Worked

- The Talmud describes an ideal student as a "well-plastered cistern which would not let even a single drop escape."
- Ancient Greek storytellers memorised the entire Iliad and Odyssey.
- Josephus records Jews could recite their laws more easily than their own names.
- It was not uncommon for a Jewish boy to have the entire Old Testament memorised by age 14.
- Mesopotamian students had to memorise works and reproduce them in correct order.
- **Mary Carruthers**: "A person without memory, if such a thing could be, would be a person without moral character and in the basic sense without humanity."
- **David Carr**: in Mesopotamia, more advanced students had to memorise individual elements and place them in the correct order — similar to how modern actors memorise scripts.
- **Kenneth Bailey** (worked in the Middle East for years): communities can preserve and sustain up to 6,000 wisdom sayings across centuries, accurately and collectively.
- **Tony Lentz**: Western academic bias toward writing has caused scholars to undervalue the precision of oral transmission.
- **Johan Draper**: embedded oral forms allow people to remember astonishing amounts of material while maintaining both stability and flexibility.
- The Zulu royal praise poems (studied by Trevor Cope) show both verbatim stability and appropriate variation from performance to performance.

### Controlled vs. Uncontrolled Tradition

- **Controlled oral tradition**: memorised, identified as preserved tradition, not meant to change; a set teacher and set community guard it.
- **Uncontrolled oral tradition**: living, fluid, subject to variation; no fixed teacher; open to additions.

The New Testament preserves a **controlled tradition**, evidenced by:

1. The culture's strong emphasis on memory and preservation.
2. Jesus's **rabbi-disciple** teaching relationship — disciples expected to obey and imitate.
3. Jesus's words were treated as **divine and sacred** (Harold Riesenfeld: "the words and deeds of Jesus are a holy word comparable with that of the Old Testament").
4. **Memory-enhancing techniques** throughout Jesus's teaching:
   - **Parables**: 35% of synoptic material; easy to memorise and recall (the Good Samaritan, the Prodigal Son).
   - **Vivid visuals**: Mark 10:25 (camel through the eye of a needle), Matthew 7 (log in your eye).
   - **Word play**: Matthew 23 (_gnat_ and _camel_ — a likely Aramaic wordplay on similar-sounding words).
   - **Parallelism**: Robert Stein and David Aune estimate **80% of Jesus's teachings** use _parallelismus membrorum_ — giving a sentence in a similar form so the passage has pattern and rhythm. Examples: Luke 11:9 ("Ask and it shall be given... seek and you will find... knock and it will be opened"), Matthew 7:17 ("every healthy tree bears good fruit, but the diseased tree bears bad fruit").

Sceptics cannot explain why a community inventing theology would include all these memory-enhancement structures. It would be unnecessary for later myth-making and would only serve the purpose of transmitting actual teachings.

### Why the Words of Jesus Were Not Invented to Suit the Church

If Jesus's sayings were invented later to resolve early church disputes, why don't they address actual early controversies such as:

- Whether Gentiles needed to be circumcised.
- The proper role of speaking in tongues.
- What the resurrected body would look like.

These controversies are addressed through church councils and letters — not through invented sayings of Jesus. The absence of convenient invented sayings is strong evidence the tradition was preserved, not manufactured.

### Designated Guardians of the Tradition

- **Luke 1:2** refers to "eyewitnesses and ministers of the word" — the word translated "ministers" is the Greek _hyperetes_ (translating the Hebrew _hazan_), the synagogue official responsible for keeping the scrolls. Luke is identifying specific authoritative guardians of the oral tradition.
- **Acts 1**: Judas's replacement must have been with the ministry "from the beginning" — showing apostolic oversight was considered essential for the transmission.

### Early Oral Creeds

The most significant evidence: Paul quotes **early oral creeds and hymns** in his letters (written in the 40s–60s AD). These creeds predate the letters. The creed in **1 Corinthians 15** is dated by the overwhelming majority of scholars to within **three years of Pentecost**, preserving:

- Christ's death.
- Resurrection on the third day.
- Appearances to named witnesses.
- Divinity of Christ.

Gary Habermas: this creed is the closest we get with any ancient document to the source events.

### How Long Can Oral Tradition Stay Reliable?

- **Gilbert Gahan**: oral traditions can remain reliable for up to 150 years before corruption seeps in.
- **Marlene Klökmann**: sets the limit at 200 years.

Both figures are well within the time frame of when even the latest New Testament books were written.

## Part 4 — Authorship and Dating

### Why Sceptics Date the Gospels Late

The main sceptical argument is circular:

1. Mark 13 contains a precise prophecy of the temple's destruction (70 AD) — therefore it must have been written after 70 AD.
2. This presupposes Jesus could not have made an accurate predictive prophecy — which is the very question at issue.
3. Maurice Casey (atheist scholar) argues extensively that Mark 13 should be dated to c. 40 AD.

**Albert Lord** (oral tradition specialist): the shorter version of a tradition is not necessarily older — it may reflect practical constraints of a different audience (e.g. Mark shortened for Gentiles).

### External Evidence — Church Fathers

Four early church fathers spread across the ancient world all independently attribute the same authors to the same gospels:

- **Papias of Hierapolis** (before 125 AD): Mark was Peter's interpreter; Matthew wrote the _logia_ in Hebrew.
- **Irenaeus of Lyon** (c. 180 AD): Matthew wrote for Jews while Paul and Peter preached in Rome; Mark followed; Luke wrote third; John last at Ephesus.
- **Clement of Alexandria** (c. 180 AD): same sequence; Mark written at Peter's request while preaching in Rome; John last.
- **Tertullian** (c. 200 AD, North Africa): gospels written by apostles Matthew and John, and apostolic men Luke and Mark.
- **Muratorian Fragment** (c. 150 AD): Luke was third; John was fourth, written at the urging of fellow disciples and bishops.

**Tim McGrew**: plotting these four church fathers geographically shows attestation from all over the ancient world — "this is the kind of testimony we dream of receiving with other ancient documents."

**No competing tradition** exists attributing the gospels to anyone else. The Epistle to the Hebrews, which is genuinely anonymous, shows what happens when the early church _didn't_ know an author — they openly disagreed and speculated. The fact that they never speculated about gospel authorship confirms the tradition was known and settled.

**Why not invent more prestigious names?** If the church was fabricating authorship, why attribute two gospels to men (Mark and Luke) who never met Jesus and were later disciples of apostles? Surely they would have chosen James, Andrew, or another prominent disciple. The attribution to Mark and Luke is a mark of authenticity.

**E.P. Sanders**: "The claim of an anonymous history was higher than that of a named work in the ancient world. An anonymous book rather like an encyclopaedia article today implicitly claimed complete knowledge and reliability."

### Internal Evidence for Early Dating (Pre-70 AD)

**1. The temple's destruction is never reported as having occurred.**
All three synoptic gospels record Jesus's prediction of the temple's destruction (Mark 13, Matthew 24, Luke 21). If written after 70 AD — when the event had occurred — the authors would have noted its fulfilment, as they consistently do with other fulfilled prophecies:

- Judas's betrayal is noted as having happened (all four gospels).
- Acts 11: Luke notes that the famine prediction had happened under Claudius.
- John 2:22: John notes that Jesus's saying about raising the temple was understood after the resurrection.

Yet none of the synoptics note that the temple was indeed destroyed. This is inconsistent with their established style unless they were writing _before_ 70 AD.

**2. Pre-persecution tone.**
The Neronian persecution began in 64 AD. After it, anti-Roman literature emerged (Revelation, the Apocalypse of Peter). But the gospels and Acts consistently portray Romans positively: Roman soldiers respond to Jesus, a Roman centurion confesses Jesus at the cross, Paul is protected by Roman officials. Rome is not the enemy — the Jewish religious authorities are. This fits a pre-64 AD date.

**3. Matthew's Jewish audience presupposes Jerusalem still stands.**
Matthew writes for a Jewish audience in Jerusalem — mentioning specific local details (the field where Judas hanged himself, the guard at the tomb) that only matter to people frequenting Jerusalem. After 70 AD the Jewish population was scattered and a centralised Jerusalem audience no longer existed.

**4. Protective anonymity in Mark.**
Many figures in the passion narrative are unnamed (the person who cut off the soldier's ear; the man who fled naked). Scholars Gerhard Theissen and Craig Evans point out these were left anonymous for their protection — if named, the Jerusalem Sanhedrin could have arrested them for actions taken during Jesus's arrest. By the time John was written, the Sanhedrin had lost its power (post-70 AD), and John freely identifies Peter as the one who cut off the ear. The passion narrative in Mark therefore **must predate 70 AD**.

Theissen: "Only in Jerusalem was there a reason to draw a cloak of anonymity over followers of Jesus who had endangered themselves by their actions."

Richard Bauckham: parts of the passion account "would have to have been composed within the generation of the eyewitnesses and their contemporaries — somewhere between 30 and 60 AD."

**5. Acts ends without recording Paul's trial outcome.**
Acts builds extensively toward Paul's trial in Rome then simply ends with Paul waiting in Rome for two years (c. 62 AD). No mention of the outcome, no mention of Paul's death, no mention of Peter's death (64 AD), no mention of James's death (61 AD), no mention of the temple's destruction (70 AD).

The most natural explanation: Acts was completed c. 62 AD while Paul was still waiting. Since Luke explicitly says Acts is his second book, **Luke's gospel predates Acts**, placing it before 62 AD. Since Luke relied heavily on Mark, **Mark was written even earlier**.

### Proposed Datings

| Gospel  | Estimated Date                                                                             |
| ------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Mark    | c. 57–60 AD (possibly as early as the 40s)                                                 |
| Luke    | c. 60–62 AD                                                                                |
| Matthew | c. 50s–60s AD in Aramaic, later translated to Greek; possibly as early as 41 AD (Eusebius) |
| John    | c. 66–96 AD                                                                                |
| Acts    | c. 62 AD                                                                                   |

## Part 5 — External Corroboration

### Secular Historical Sources

**Tacitus** (_Annals_, c. 116 AD): Nero blamed Christians for the fire of Rome. "Christus, from whom the name had its origin, suffered the extreme penalty during the reign of Tiberius at the hands of one of our procurators, Pontius Pilatus." A hostile witness confirming: Jesus existed, was executed, by Pilate, in Judea, under Tiberius.

**Josephus** (_Antiquities of the Jews_, c. 93 AD): The _Testimonium Flavianum_ is widely accepted as partially interpolated by a later Christian scribe; the original form is preserved in an Arabic manuscript: "There was a wise man... called Jesus... his conduct was good... Pilate condemned him to be crucified and to die... they reported that he had appeared to them after his crucifixion... he was perhaps the Messiah." Josephus also separately confirms: James was Jesus's brother; John the Baptist was beheaded by Herod Antipas.

**Babylonian Talmud**: "On the eve of Passover they hanged Yeshu... he practiced sorcery and enticed and led Israel astray." Confirms: crucifixion around Passover, Jesus led many away from religious authorities, a trial occurred, he was believed to perform miracles (attributed to sorcery — meeting the **criterion of embarrassment**).

**Grant McVey**: "There is now almost unanimous agreement among Jesus Questers that the historical Jesus performed mighty works." Even the Jesus Seminar lists Jesus as "a charismatic healer and exorcist" among its basic facts.

**Thallus** (quoted by Julius Africanus): argued the darkness at the crucifixion was a solar eclipse — confirming the darkness happened while contesting its cause. Also referenced by Phlegon (according to Africanus): "an eclipse of the sun occurred during the full moon" in the reign of Tiberius — a physical impossibility (solar eclipses cannot occur at full moon), consistent with a supernatural event at Passover (always a full moon).

### Archaeological Confirmation

**Pontius Pilate Inscription** (1961, Caesarea): "Pontius Pilate, Prefect of Judea" — confirms his existence, title, and region exactly as Luke records.

**Lysanias inscription** (Abila, time of Tiberius): confirms Luke's identification of Lysanias as tetrarch of Abilene — previously considered an error since another Lysanias of Chalcis was known to have lived earlier. Two officials with the same name; Luke was correct.

**Other confirmed figures**: Herod the Great, Herod Antipas, Gallio (proconsul of Achaia, Acts 18), Caiaphas (family tomb discovered, confirming existence), Erastus (treasurer of Corinth — ossuary bearing the name found in 1941), Alexander son of Simon (ossuary possibly bearing the name of the Simon of Cyrene's son mentioned in Mark 15:21).

**Cities confirmed**: Bethlehem, Nazareth (both previously doubted as too small), Capernaum (synagogue and Roman presence exactly as NT testifies), Pool of Bethesda (five porticoes), Pool of Siloam, Galilean fishing boat matching NT descriptions.

**Earthquake during crucifixion**: geological varve analysis of sediment layers in Judea detected a 6.3-magnitude earthquake between 26 and 36 AD, capable of causing the structural damage to tombs and the temple as recorded in the gospels.

**James ossuary** (2002): "James, son of Joseph, brother of Jesus" — later studies including 2014 microfossil analysis concluded the inscription was authentic. NT scholar Ben Witherington: "if it is authentic, it belongs to James the brother of Jesus."

**Crucifixion remains**: an ossuary found in 1968 containing a Jewish man named Johanan with a seven-inch nail driven through both feet and crushed legs — confirming nailing and leg-breaking were actual Roman practices.

**Josephus on burial**: prior to 70 AD, crucified victims were permitted proper burial — consistent with the gospel's account of Jesus's burial.

### Historical and Cultural Coherence

The New Testament writers demonstrate **intimate familiarity** with the culture of first-century Palestine. This is significant because writing accurately about another culture's detailed practices, currency, political titles, and geography is extremely difficult without being organically part of that culture.

**Contrast**: Philostratus's _Life of Apollonius_ (c. 200 AD), attempting to describe a 1st-century sage, is riddled with anachronisms and factual errors that mark it as fiction — a king still reigning in Babylon (which was in ruins), an Indian journey incompatible with known facts.

**Colin Hemer**: 84 facts in the final 16 chapters of Acts confirmed by archaeology and external sources, including correct titles for local officials in different regions: proconsul (Cyprus), magistrates (Philippi), politarchs (Thessalonica), town clerk (Ephesus), chief man (Malta), Areopagite (Athens).

**Craig Blomberg**: 59 facts John gets right about ancient Judea.

**Nathaniel Lardner**: _Credibility of the Gospel History_ — 17 volumes documenting detail after detail the NT writers got correct.

**Richard Bauckham**: name frequencies in the gospels match almost exactly the ratio of names found on Palestinian ossuaries and ancient records. Simon and Joseph are the most popular male names in both; the other nine most popular male names correlate; the top two female names (Mary and Salome) match; the other nine most popular female names also correlate.

**Currency**: Matthew 17 correctly records that one shekel = four drachma — the precise exchange rate for the temple tax period.

**Political accuracy**: the NT navigates the complicated shifting governance of Judea (Herod the Great → three sons → Archelaus removed → Roman prefects → Herod Agrippa → procurators → Herod Agrippa II) without error — even correctly noting that Archelaus was never certified as king but only as ethnarch.

## Part 6 — Internal Evidence

### 1. Excessive Non-Theological Content

Throughout the Epistles and gospels there are passages with no theological significance — Paul asking Timothy to bring his cloak and books, lists of women who follow Jesus (Luke 8), the identification of Simon of Cyrene as the father of Alexander and Rufus (Mark 15:21).

Papyrus in the ancient world cost ~2 dinari (two full days' wages) per scroll. Hiring a scribe added further cost. These passages represent a deliberate _expensive_ choice to preserve material that would only matter to people directly involved — which makes sense if the authors were writing contemporaneously for real communities, but makes no sense if later myth-makers were composing theological documents. The available space would far more usefully have been used to invent sayings of Jesus resolving pressing controversies (circumcision, speaking in tongues, Easter dating). None of that happened.

### 2. The Criterion of Embarrassment

Passages embarrassing to the authors are more likely to be historically true — since authors prefer to omit embarrassing information unless they feel bound to report it honestly.

**Examples:**

- **Joseph of Arimathea's tomb**: the Sanhedrin was the early church's enemy (they crucified Jesus); admitting Jesus could not even be given his own tomb and had to use one from the enemy group is an unlikely invention.
- **Women as first witnesses of the resurrection**: in ancient culture, women's testimony was inadmissible in court (Talmud). Opponents like Celsus attacked Christianity on exactly this point. If the resurrection account was invented, the inventors would never have chosen women as the key witnesses.
- **N.T. Wright**: "If these stories had been made up five years later, let alone 30–50 years later, they would never have had Mary Magdalene in this role. To put Mary there is, from the point of view of Christian apologists wanting to explain that Jesus really did rise from the dead, like shooting themselves in the foot. But to us as historians, this kind of thing is gold dust."
- **Church disputes in Acts and 1 Corinthians**: if you're inventing a story that Gentiles don't need to be circumcised, it's far easier to invent a Jesus saying than to invent a bitter church split requiring a council to resolve.

### 3. Undesigned Coincidences

Revived by philosopher **Tim McGrew** (building on earlier 19th-century work). An undesigned coincidence is a detail in one Gospel that inadvertently fills in a gap or resolves a puzzle left unexplained in another Gospel — without any apparent intention to do so.

**Examples:**

**Matthew 8 + Mark (Sabbath healing)**
Matthew reports Jesus healed Peter's mother-in-law and that _evening_ crowds brought more sick. Why wait until evening? Mark adds: they entered the city on the Sabbath. The Sabbath ended at evening — so people waited. Matthew doesn't explain this; Mark fills it in without appearing to.

**Luke (Transfiguration) + Mark**
Luke reports the disciples told no one what they had seen. Why? Mark supplies the reason: Jesus instructed them not to. Luke's loose end is filled by Mark without coordination.

**Mark 6 (feeding of 5,000) + John**
Mark says they sat on _green grass_ in a _deserted place_ amid a _crowd_. Unexplained: grass is rare in Palestine; why was it green? why so crowded that retreat was necessary? John explains: it was _around Passover_ — spring (green grass); pilgrims flocking (overcrowding). John doesn't mention the green grass; he's not trying to explain it.

**John 6:5 (why Philip?) + Luke 9**
John asks why Jesus specifically asked Philip where to buy bread. Luke explains: the feeding happened near Bethsaida. John later identifies Philip as from Bethsaida — so Jesus asked the local. Luke didn't connect these dots; John didn't explain the question; both are needed.

**Matthew's woe on Bethsaida + Luke**
Matthew pronounces a woe on Bethsaida for rejecting "the mighty works done there" — yet Matthew never mentions any mighty works in Bethsaida. Luke explains: the feeding of the 5,000 happened near Bethsaida. Matthew's woe makes no sense without Luke's detail that he has no reason to provide.

**Matthew 14:1–2 (Herod's servants) + Luke 8**
Matthew records Herod discussing Jesus with his _servants_ — curious, since kings normally consult advisers on theological questions. Luke in a different context mentions Joanna, the wife of Chuza (Herod's household manager), was a follower of Jesus. This explains how the disciples had inside intelligence on Herod's private conversations.

**Mark 15 (temple destruction accusation) + John 2**
Mark reports the Jews accused Jesus of claiming he would destroy the temple and rebuild it in three days — but none of the synoptics explain where this claim came from. John in a completely different context records Jesus saying "destroy this temple and in three days I will raise it up" at the temple cleansing.

**Luke (Pilate's verdict) + John**
Luke: Jesus says "you have said so" to Pilate's question, yet Pilate declares him innocent. Why innocent if Jesus admitted the charge? John explains: Jesus said "my kingdom is not of this world" — a spiritual kingdom that posed no threat to Caesar's physical empire. Luke provides the accusation; John provides the exculpatory answer. Neither accounts for the other's gap.

These coincidences are cumulative — taken together they are far more consistent with multiple independent witnesses reporting history than with coordinated myth-making.

## Part 7 — Responding to Common Objections

### Objection 1: The Authors Were Biased

The person making this objection is also biased — they are biased _against_ the NT. By their own logic, their objection must be dismissed.

Every historian is biased. Caesar's _Gallic Wars_ is described by Michael Grant as "among the most potent works of propaganda ever written" — yet Grant also says "it is extremely hard to fault him on facts." Bias does not prevent accurate reporting.

Richard Dawkins opens _The God Delusion_ admitting his goal is to convert religious readers to atheism. Gerd Lüdemann states his aim was to prove the non-historicity of the resurrection. Are we to dismiss them?

### Objection 2: Illiterate Fishermen Couldn't Have Written This

Ehrman argues: the followers of Jesus were Aramaic-speaking peasants from Galilee; Peter and John are called "illiterate" (Acts 4:13); yet the NT is written in polished Greek.

Responses:

- The NT was not claimed to have been written exclusively by illiterate fishermen.
- **Matthew**: tax collector — required bilingualism in Greek and Aramaic.
- **Mark**: Greek scribe and interpreter for Peter.
- **Luke**: physician — clearly educated.
- **Paul**: trained under Gamaliel — unquestioned.
- That leaves Peter, James, Jude, and John, who could have learned to write after taking leadership roles.

But even if all NT authors were illiterate: Ehrman himself points out in a different context that _everyone_ in the ancient world **dictated to scribes**. So if all the authors were illiterate, they could simply have dictated. The growing Gentile church provided ample scribes and funding. This is not a serious objection and Ehrman uses it inconsistently.

### Objection 3: The NT Mentions Miracles

Almost all Greco-Roman historians mention miracles — Herodotus, Tacitus, Suetonius, Livy. Even Thucydides, who avoids them, is the exception. Dismissing a text because it contains miracles is a presupposition that proves too much. All ancient texts are evaluated case by case. Even if a single miracle claim in a text is found to be false, that does not discredit everything else the text reports — that would be an association fallacy.

### Objection 4: The Gospels Contradict Each Other

Contradictions between eyewitness accounts are **expected and normal** — they show independence (no collusion). A homicide detective interviewing witnesses immediately after a crime regularly finds apparent contradictions. J. Warner Wallace (cold-case detective, former atheist): "It's my job as the investigator to determine why the eyewitnesses appear to contradict one another, even though there is no doubt the event occurred and the witnesses were telling the truth."

**The Nero fire example**: Fonsius says Nero openly sent men; Dio Cassius says he did it secretly; Tacitus implies he wasn't responsible. Scholars do not conclude from these contradictions that Rome was never burned.

**N.T. Wright**: "This is how real life works. If anything, [the variation] rather strengthens the case for the breathless eyewitness theory."

**The colusion point** (Wallace): if all four gospels told exactly the same story, they would be under even greater suspicion of collusion. The variation between accounts is exactly what you expect from independent witnesses. The fact that they had _opportunity_ to harmonise their stories but did _not_ is a mark of reliability.

Overall conclusion of the series: the New Testament is by far the most reliably attested, earliest documented, historically confirmed, and internally consistent document from the ancient world. The onus is on the sceptic to explain this evidence, not to dismiss it.
