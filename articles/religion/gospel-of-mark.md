# The Gospel of Mark — Based on Eyewitness Accounts (Peter)

## Overview

Church history consistently holds that Mark's gospel came from the preaching of Peter. Many modern scholars reject this. This video argues that both the external evidence and a rich array of internal evidence confirm the tradition: Mark's gospel is an eyewitness account that goes back to the apostle Peter.

---

## 1. External Evidence — Church Fathers

The unanimous testimony of the early church is that Mark wrote the gospel as Peter's interpreter and recorder:

- **Papias of Hierapolis** — Mark was a follower and interpreter of Peter who wrote down what Peter preached; Peter often adapted his teachings to his audience.
- **1 Peter** — a "Mark" is mentioned as present with Peter, placing them together.
- **Irenaeus** — "Mark, the disciple and recorder of Peter, has also handed on to us in writing what had been proclaimed by Peter."
- **Tertullian** — Mark was the interpreter of Peter.
- **Anti-Marcionite Prologue** — Mark's gospel came from Mark the Interpreter of Peter.
- **Clement of Alexandria** — Mark was a follower of Peter who wrote down what he preached.
- **Origen** — the second gospel was written according to Peter's instructions.
- **Justin Martyr** — without needing to name the gospel authors, refers to "the Memoirs of Peter" for events only recorded in Mark — indicating he associated Mark's gospel with Peter.

Additionally, Matthew and Luke used Mark as a source but made remarkably few changes compared to the way other ancient biographers treated their sources. This suggests an unusual reverence for Mark — consistent with the view that it came from the authoritative testimony of Peter.

---

## 2. Aramaic Translations in Mark

Maurice Casey's linguistic analysis shows that when Aramaic reconstructions are made of certain passages in Mark, the awkward Greek makes sense — because Mark was **literally translating Aramaic sayings of Jesus into Greek**:

- Passages like Mark 1:39–43 and 11:15–17 show large amounts of parataxis and verb-initial sentences — features more natural in Aramaic than in Greek.
- Casey: "We have found substantial and decisive evidence that parts of Mark's gospel are literal translations of written Aramaic sources."
- This is inexplicable if Mark was writing Greek fiction; it is expected if Mark was hearing Aramaic from Peter and taking notes.
- Some Aramaic passages reflect specifically Jewish concerns about the details of the Law — issues no longer relevant to a predominantly Gentile church, indicating the traditions are early and authentically Palestinian.
- Casey: Mark's Aramaic source predates 40 AD and came from a Jew from Israel — almost certainly a member of the Twelve. Given the church tradition, Peter is the most likely candidate.

Craig Evans: in the ancient world, note-taking was standard practice when interviewing eyewitnesses (Galen and Lucian both describe this). Mark likely took Aramaic notes during Peter's preaching, then composed his Greek gospel from them.

---

## 3. Jewish Customs and Early Palestinian Traditions

James Crossley: Mark preserves accurate Jewish customs in detail:

- Mark 6:56 — Jesus had a fringe/tassel on his garment, consistent with the biblical command (Numbers 15:38).
- Mark 2:23–28 (identified by Casey as Aramaic) — a dispute over plucking grain on the Sabbath is a thoroughly **intra-Jewish** controversy over Torah interpretation, not relevant to later Gentile Christianity.
- Mark 7 — Pharisee washing rituals (cups, pots, copper vessels, dining couches): confirmed in Jewish legal texts; reflects intricate knowledge of Jewish legal thought.
- Mark accurately depicts the political and religious landscape of Judea: scribes as a class separate from Pharisees and Sadducees; scribes headquartered in Jerusalem coming up to Galilee; Sadducees absent in Galilee; distinctions between elders, chief priests, and teachers of the law.
- Greg Boyd: these distinctions ceased to exist after 70 AD. An accurate depiction of this landscape supports a **pre-70 AD** dating for Mark, consistent with the tradition.
- Mark 10:35–43 — Capernaum had a whole group of tax collectors because it was situated at a strategic point on the northern end of the Sea of Galilee, an important tax collection location on Herod Antipas's border — a minor but geographically accurate detail (Peter J. Williams).
- "Hosanna" in Mark 11 — the word had shifted from its original meaning (save us) to a celebratory expression by the time of Jesus. The form in Mark reflects the Hebrew pronunciation of the **first century specifically**, not the psalm era (Williams).

## 4. Protective Anonymity in the Passion Narrative

G.D. Theissen and Richard Bauckham note that many individuals in the passion narrative are left unnamed — the man who cut off the servant's ear, the man who fled naked — despite being central to the story:

- Both had run afoul of the authorities and were still at risk of arrest.
- The ear that was cut off would still have been visible on the servant Malchus; identifying Peter by name as the swordsman could have endangered him.
- By the time John was written (decades later), John names Peter freely.
- Other unnamed figures: those who helped with the triumphal entry (potential co-conspirators in making Jesus king), and possibly Lazarus in the synoptics.

Theissen: this anonymity makes sense only if the passion narrative was **formulated early**, when these individuals still needed protection — somewhere between 30 and 60 AD. This strongly supports early dating and eyewitness-proximity.

---

## 5. Onomastic Congruence

Luke VanderWay's research found that **Mark's gospel achieves onomastic congruence** — the names in the text match the distribution of popular-to-rare names from first-century Judea, with appropriate distinguishing markers for common names:

- Apocryphal gospels, tragedies, and novels lack this feature.
- Even some Greco-Roman biographies fail to achieve it.
- Only Josephus's autobiography, Plutarch's _Lives_, and Suetonius's _Twelve Caesars_ achieve it among biographies.
- Craig Keener identifies these as "the height of historical reliability" in ancient biography.
- Mark placing itself in this company strongly suggests it relied on eyewitnesses, as Josephus, Plutarch, and Suetonius consulted archives and eyewitnesses.
- Raymond Martin's syntactical analysis found Mark contains translated Semitic material alongside original Greek material.
- Names in Mark cluster around the most primitive (earliest) material — consistent with the names having been preserved because they were the actual eyewitnesses involved.

---

## 6. Named Individuals as Living Footnotes

Scott McKnight and Richard Bauckham: in ancient historiography, authors sometimes named individuals irrelevant to the story to signal them as eyewitness sources:

- **Simon of Cyrene** (Mark 15:21) — introduced as "the father of Alexander and Rufus." These names serve no narrative purpose. Paul's letter to the Romans mentions a Rufus — consistent with Mark writing while Peter preached in Rome, where Alexander and Rufus were also present and could confirm the account.
- Bauckham: "the reference to Alexander and Rufus may presuppose that Mark expected many of his readers to know them in person or by reputation."
- This practice is paralleled in Plutarch (_Life of Caesar_ — Asinius Pollio named as eyewitness source), Cornelius Nepos (_Life of Atticus_ — Balbus and Pedus as witnesses), and Josephus (_The Jewish War_ — nieces of Philip identified as eyewitness sources for the fall of Gamla).
- Travis Dero's research on oral traditions in Northern Jordan: the high percentage of names in oral accounts is best explained by their function as informants.
- Women named at the cross and tomb (Mark 15–16): Mark names them as eyewitness sources for the death and burial of Jesus and the discovery of the empty tomb. John Donahue and Daniel Harrington: the imperfect tense ("were observing") suggests these women took in everything from start to finish.

## 7. The Petrine Literary Devices

Richard Bauckham identifies two literary devices in Mark that indicate Peter as the primary eyewitness source:

### Inclusio of Eyewitness

- Mark **opens** by naming Peter (1:16) and **ends** with a specific reference to Peter (16:7 — the women are told to tell "the disciples and Peter").
- This mirrors a technique used in other ancient biographies: Lucian's _Life of Alexander_ opens and closes with a reference to Rutilianus; Porphyry's _Life of Plotinus_ opens and closes with Amelius.
- The inclusio signals to ancient readers who the primary eyewitness source is.

### Plural-to-Singular Narrative Device

- Mark frequently uses a **plural verb** for the movement of Jesus and his disciples, then immediately switches to a **singular verb** for Jesus alone:
  - "They went into Capernaum... he entered the synagogue" (1:21)
  - "They came to the other side... when Jesus had stepped out of the boat" (5:1–2)
  - "On the following day when they came from Bethany, he was hungry" (11:12)
- Matthew and Luke regularly eliminate the plural, referring only to Jesus's movement.
- Bauckham: this device reflects an internal perspective — the narrator was **inside the group moving with Jesus**. If Peter was recounting "we came from Bethany," Mark would write "they" but retain the sense of the group moving together.

### Peter's Central Role Throughout

- Peter is the most frequently named person in Mark after Jesus.
- Peter speaks for the group and is addressed in place of the group.
- Peter's motives are disclosed in Mark 9 — something Mark could only know by hearing it from Peter.
- In Mark 14, Peter's story is told apart from the other disciples.
- Peter's misunderstandings often set the narrative tone (e.g., calling Jesus "Satan" immediately after the great confession; the bold pledge of loyalty followed by the threefold denial).
- When Peter exits the narrative (chapter 15 onwards), Mark introduces other named individuals — women, Simon of Cyrene, Joseph of Arimathea — as sources for the crucifixion and resurrection accounts.

---

## 8. The List of the Twelve

Scott McKnight: in rabbinic texts, lists of disciples include elaborations of their piety and legacy. The Twelve in Mark have none of this. Most of the disciples never speak in Mark and play no prominent role. The list appears to serve one purpose: **preserving the names of the authentic tradents** (those responsible for passing on the tradition).

- The list begins with Peter, then James son of Zebedee, then John identified as James's brother — written from the perspective of someone for whom James was more prominent than John, consistent with a pre-44 AD tradition (James died c. 44 AD; after his death, John would not be identified merely as his brother).
- Matthew and Luke drop this designator, suggesting the tradition in Mark predates James's death.

---

## Conclusion

The internal evidence overwhelmingly confirms what the external evidence already states:

- Mark translated underlying Aramaic sayings that almost certainly came from a member of the Twelve (most likely Peter).
- Mark had intricate knowledge of Jewish customs from Judea and early traditions reflecting the concerns of the **first** Jerusalem Christians, not later Gentile ones.
- Mark achieved onomastic congruence — a naming pattern matching first-century Judea, placing it alongside the most historically reliable ancient biographies.
- Mark employed the literary device of _inclusio of eyewitness_ to frame Peter as his primary source.
- Mark's plural-to-singular narrative device reflects an insider's perspective within the group of disciples.
- Mark named irrelevant individuals throughout to indicate his eyewitness sources — consistent with ancient historiographical practice.
- When Peter exited the narrative, Mark indicated alternative sources for the passion and resurrection accounts.
