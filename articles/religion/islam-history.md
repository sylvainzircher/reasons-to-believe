# Islam: History & Sources

_Based on a lecture by William J. Federer_

---

## The Full History of Islam

### 1. The World Before Muhammad

Before Islam, two great empires dominated the Middle East:

- **Byzantine (Christian Roman) Empire** — stretched from Europe through the Middle East, with Christianity spreading as far as India, Persia, and China (Tang Dynasty)
- **Persian (Zoroastrian) Empire** — present-day Iran, believed paradise was filled with virgins and kept djinn (genie) mythology

These two empires fought each other for centuries, exhausting both and leaving a **power vacuum** — the opening Islam would fill.

---

### 2. Muhammad's Origins (570–610 AD)

Muhammad was orphaned early: his father died before his birth, his mother when he was 6, his grandfather at 8. Raised by his uncle Abu Talib, a merchant, he traveled trade routes and absorbed fragments of many belief systems:

| Source                                | What Muhammad absorbed                                                                                                                        |
| ------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Manichaeism                           | Idea that there's a little truth in every religion                                                                                            |
| Persian Zoroastrianism                | Paradise filled with virgins; djinn (genies)                                                                                                  |
| Arabian Paganism                      | The Kaaba's 360 gods; the moon god Hubal; the crescent calendar; kissing the black meteorite stone                                            |
| Jewish oral tradition (Talmud/Mishna) | Stories of Moses and the prophets — he couldn't read the written Torah                                                                        |
| Heretical Christian sources           | The Infancy Gospel of Thomas (Jesus making clay birds fly, speaking from the cradle); the belief that the Trinity was Father, Mary, and Jesus |
| Christian monasticism                 | Praying at set times of day (from St. Benedict's rule); living in caves to seek God                                                           |

At 25 he married **Khadijah**, a wealthy 40-year-old widow. No longer needing to work, he retreated to a cave to pray.

---

### 3. The Revelation (610 AD)

In the cave, a spirit appeared three times and commanded him to "Read" (the word _Quran_ means "recitation"). Because he was illiterate, the spirit squeezed him until he began to recite. Muhammad initially feared he was demon-possessed. His wife Khadijah tested the spirit by a physical modesty test — and concluded it was an angel. Her cousin Waraqa, a follower of the Ebionite heresy (which held Jesus was a created being, not God), suggested it might be the same spirit that appeared to Moses.

---

### 4. Stage 1 — Religious Leader in Mecca (610–622 AD)

Muhammad preached in Mecca, believing his message contained something for everyone. In 12 years he made only **70 converts**. When his uncle/protector Abu Talib and his wife Khadijah both died, the Meccans drove him out. He fled to Taif — where they pelted him with stones.

**The two kinds of Quranic verses:**

- **Meccan verses** — earlier, more moderate, considered "weak"
- **Medinan verses** — later, more political and military, considered "strong" and superseding the earlier ones

This is the reverse of the Bible's structure (Old Testament violence → New Testament peace). In Islam, the peaceful verses came _first_ and were replaced by militant ones.

---

### 5. Stage 2 — Political Leader in Medina (622 AD)

Muhammad emigrated (_Hijra_) to **Medina** (then called Yathrib), a city with three Jewish clans: Banu Qaynuqa, Banu Nadir, and Banu Qurayza — who were locked in inter-tribal rivalry.

He was welcomed as an immigrant. He organized a Muslim community (_umma_), grew his following in minority neighborhoods, and pressured the Jews into a treaty. He became the city's political leader.

Then his followers — expelled from Mecca — flooded into Medina as refugees.

**The "Hudna" principle enters Islamic DNA:** Make treaties until you are strong enough to discard them.

---

### 6. Stage 3 — Military Leader (624–632 AD)

Muhammad permitted his followers to **raid caravans** heading to Mecca in retaliation for their expulsion — establishing the principle that victimhood justifies aggression. Fighters were promised:

- **Booty** (including women taken captive) if they survived
- **Paradise** if they died

He received **1/5 of all spoils** personally (Quran 8:41).

**Battle of Badr (624 AD):** 300 Muslims defeated 1,000 Meccans — a shocking 3-to-1 upset that convinced Muhammad he had divine backing. He went on to fight **66 battles and raids**, killing an estimated **3,000 people** in his final 8 years.

---

### 7. The Fate of the Jewish Clans in Medina

This is the core pattern the speaker calls "Immigrate → Increase → Eliminate":

| Clan         | Fate                                                                        |
| ------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Banu Qaynuqa | Accused of violating the treaty; expelled                                   |
| Banu Nadir   | Accused of violating the treaty; expelled                                   |
| Banu Qurayza | Surrounded for 25 days; male warriors beheaded; women and children enslaved |

**Within 5 years of Muhammad arriving as an immigrant, not one Jew remained in Medina.** They had been expelled, killed, or enslaved.

---

### 8. The Death of Muhammad (632 AD)

Muhammad died from the effects of poisoned lamb he had eaten at Khaybar — given to him by a Jewish woman whose father, uncle, and husband he had killed. He reportedly said, "I still feel the pain...I feel as if my aorta is being cut from that poison."

Within 5 years of his death, **every pre-existing culture in Arabia was conquered**.

---

### 9. The First Century of Expansion (632–732 AD)

The early caliphate used two military advantages to conquer at breathtaking speed:

- **The stirrup** — invented in Mongolia/India, perfected in Persia with metal and leather, gave cavalry warriors total control on horseback
- **The scimitar** — Damascus steel, folded and hammered dozens of times, thin as a razor, capable of cutting a man in half at full gallop

**What fell to Islam in roughly 100 years:**

| Region          | Notes                                                                    |
| --------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| All of Arabia   | Within 5 years of Muhammad's death                                       |
| Syria           | The first country fully evangelized by Paul; "Christian" was coined here |
| Jerusalem       | Christians given dhimmi status — second-class citizens paying a tax      |
| Egypt           | Christian for 6 centuries; Coptic language eventually exterminated       |
| Persia          | Weakened by Byzantine wars; fell quickly                                 |
| North Africa    | 250 Catholic dioceses erased; St. Augustine's homeland                   |
| Spain           | Conquered in 10 years after landing at Gibraltar (Jabal Tariq)           |
| Southern France | Stopped at the **Battle of Tours, 732 AD** by Charles Martel             |

The speaker notes: **Islam is largely responsible for Europe entering the Dark Ages** — by cutting off papyrus trade across the Mediterranean, causing a paper shortage and fewer books. They also destroyed the great Library of Alexandria.

---

### 10. The Battle of Tours (732 AD) — The Turning Point in the West

Charles Martel positioned his men on a hill in a defensive square. The Muslim cavalry couldn't effectively charge uphill. He then sent troops to raid the Muslim camp and free the captive women — causing Muslim warriors to abandon the fight to reclaim their plunder. The Muslim commander was killed in the confusion. The army retreated to Spain.

---

### 11. The Islamic Golden Age (750–1100 AD)

The Abbasid Caliph Harun al-Rashid in Baghdad created a moderate court with Buddhists, Christians, and women advisors. Scholars like Avicenna and Averroes flourished. It nearly sparked a Renaissance.

But it was shut down by **Al-Ghazali**, considered the second most important Muslim after Muhammad, who warned that even studying geometry could corrupt faith by drawing students into Greek philosophy. Islamic scholarship stagnated.

---

### 12. The Crusades (1095–1291 AD)

The Crusades were triggered by:

- The Fatimid Caliph Hakim destroying the **Church of the Holy Sepulchre** in Jerusalem (1009 AD)
- Hakim forbidding Christians from praying publicly, making Jews wear bells, banning women's shoes, and ordering everyone to sleep during the day
- The Seljuk Turks wiping out all **seven churches of Revelation** (Ephesus, Smyrna, etc.)

Pope Urban II, receiving the remains of St. Nicholas in 1095, called the **Council of Clermont** and launched the First Crusade. Nine major crusades followed over two centuries. The Reconquista to drive Muslims from Spain took **700 years**.

---

### 13. The Fall of Constantinople (1453 AD)

The last Byzantine emperor Constantine XI was betrayed — his own brother Demetrius brought Muslim Turkish forces to the city walls in a dynastic dispute. Sultan Mehmed II's cannons (supplied by European arms merchants) breached the walls.

The **Hagia Sophia**, the largest Christian church in the world, was converted to a mosque. Trade routes to Asia were cut off — forcing Columbus to seek a sea route west, leading to the discovery of the Americas. The speaker's argument: **Columbus sailed because of Islamic jihad**.

The Greek scholars fleeing west brought their manuscripts with them — triggering the **Renaissance**, and eventually the **Reformation** (Erasmus translated Greek texts that helped Luther see "repentance" had been mistranslated as "penance").

---

### 14. The Gates of Vienna (1529 and 1683 AD)

- **1529:** 100,000 Ottoman troops surrounded Vienna under Suleiman the Magnificent
- **1683:** 200,000 troops surrounded Vienna — stopped by Polish King Jan Sobieski's cavalry charge on September 11

Two cultural footnotes from the 1683 victory:

- **Coffee** — Ottoman soldiers left behind sacks of beans; Pope Clement VIII tasted it and declared it too good to leave for the Muslims, "baptizing" it for Christian use
- **The croissant** — a Viennese baker heard the Ottomans tunneling under the walls, raised the alarm, and was rewarded with the exclusive right to make pastries shaped like the Ottoman crescent moon

The **Muslim Brotherhood** (founded 1928) formalized a two-phase plan mirroring Muhammad's own pattern: enter as a religious refugee (Meccan phase), then transition to political militancy (Medinan phase).

### 16. Sunni vs. Shia

The split began in **680 AD** at the **Battle of Karbala**, when Muhammad's grandson Hussein (son of Ali) was killed by the Sunni:

| Branch    | ~% of Muslims | Origin                                                                            |
| --------- | ------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Sunni** | 85%           | Followers of Abu Bakr, Muhammad's companion and father of his youngest wife Aisha |
| **Shia**  | 15%           | Followers of Ali, Muhammad's son-in-law; mostly in Iran                           |

Shia "Twelver" theology holds that the 12th imam (a child who disappeared in 874 AD) has been hiding in a cave for 1,200 years and will emerge during a world war — which is why the speaker argues Iran's leadership has a theological motivation to _want_ global conflict.
