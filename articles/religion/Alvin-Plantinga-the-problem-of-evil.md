# Plantinga's Free Will Defence and the Problem of Evil — Dr Owen Anderson (ASU)

## Overview

A philosophy lecture by **Dr Owen Anderson** (Professor of Philosophy, Arizona State University) presenting and critically evaluating **Alvin Plantinga's** solution to the problem of evil. The lecture plays two clips of Plantinga explaining his approach, then Anderson analyses the strengths and weaknesses of both Plantinga's free will defence and his "great making property" argument, and proposes an alternative he calls the **doxological solution**.

---

## 1. The Logical Problem of Evil

The standard formulation:

1. God is all-good.
2. God is all-powerful.
3. Evil exists.

These three premises are said to be in logical tension — they cannot all be true simultaneously. If God is all-good and all-powerful, he would prevent evil. Since evil exists, either God is not all-good, or not all-powerful, or does not exist.

One can resolve the tension by surrendering any one of the three premises:

- Deny God is all-good (or all-powerful)
- Deny that evil is real (Voltaire's target in _Candide_, critiquing Leibniz's claim that this is "the best of all possible worlds")

Plantinga's response: introduce a **greater good** that permits evil. If there is a good great enough that its achievement requires allowing evil, then God being all-good and all-powerful is consistent with permitting (not causing) evil.

## 2. Plantinga's Free Will Defence

### Core Argument

Plantinga uses the framework of **possible worlds**: logically conceivable states of affairs.

- There are possible worlds in which all free creatures do only what is right.
- But perhaps God **cannot actualize** such worlds — they may be beyond his power to create.

### Transworld Depravity

Plantinga introduces the concept of **transworld depravity**: for any free creature God might create, and for any possible world in which God places that creature, the creature would perform at least one wrong action.

If transworld depravity holds for every possible free creature, then:

- God cannot create a world with free creatures and no evil.
- Every world containing free creatures will contain some evil.
- Therefore, the existence of evil is compatible with God being all-good and all-powerful — it is the unavoidable price of creating genuinely free creatures.

Plantinga suggests this may be "a good exchange" from God's perspective: free creatures are worth the cost of the evil they produce.

### Anderson's Critique of the Free Will Defence

**1. The tension in Plantinga's possible worlds framework**
Plantinga says (a) there are possible worlds where creatures only do what is right, but (b) God cannot create those worlds. This creates a puzzle: if they are possible, why can't an all-powerful God actualise them? This points toward a deeper problem: what lies _outside_ God, and the question of divine inability.

**2. What does "free" mean?**
Plantinga defines freedom as _uncaused_ — if God causes me to act, I don't act freely. Anderson challenges this definition:

- In ordinary language, "free" means able to do what you _want_ — your will is unrestricted.
- Freedom is compatible with causation: all kinds of causes go into what I want (upbringing, environment, character, God's creation of me).
- The distinction that matters is between _acceptable causes_ (my own desires, character, history) and _unacceptable causes_ (a chemical in my food that bypasses my normal desires, an alien altering my neurons). But even these cases are disputed — if someone changes your neurons and you now genuinely want something, you still want it.
- The search for an utterly _uncaused_ free act is the search to be one's own god — to be an _unmoved mover_. Only God is the unmoved mover. Every human being is entirely dependent on God: personality, history, circumstances — all are created by God.
- The Westminster Confession identifies God as the **primary cause** and human will as a **secondary cause**. This is compatible with genuine human wanting and acting.

**3. The Romans 9 argument**
Paul's discussion in Romans 9 (why does God still find fault?) is sometimes misread as mere divine authoritarianism ("God can do whatever he wants"). Anderson reads it differently: the person asking "why does God still find fault?" is someone who _wants to do wrong but doesn't want the consequences_. They are not asking a genuine philosophical question — they are seeking a "square circle": wrong-doing without consequences. The demand for an uncaused free act is similarly a logical contradiction.

**4. Free will is a means, not an end**
Even if the free will defence succeeds, free will itself is not a terminal good — it is a _means_ to something good. The question remains: what is the ultimate good that free will and even evil are meant to serve?

## 3. Plantinga's "Greatest Story" Argument — Incarnation and Atonement as Great-Making Property

### The Argument

In the second clip, Plantinga moves beyond free will to a richer answer. He proposes that what makes this world the _greatest_ possible world is the **incarnation and atonement** — the fact that God himself became human and died for creatures who had rebelled against him. This is called "the greatest story ever told" — Plantinga says it is the greatest story that _could ever be told_.

Any world containing incarnation and atonement necessarily contains sin, evil, and suffering. Therefore, if God wanted to create the greatest possible world, he would create one with incarnation and atonement — and this world, with its evil, is that world.

### Anderson's Evaluation

**1. Theological label**
This view corresponds to **supralapsarianism** in Reformed theology: the logical order of God's decrees is incarnation/atonement first, creation/fall second (rather than the infralapsarian order of creation/fall followed by the decision to redeem). Anderson notes Charles Hodge's systematic theology covers both positions well.

The key clarification: supralapsarianism is not about temporal sequence — it describes a _logical_ (timeless) order, not a historical one. It does not imply God "changed his mind" after the fall. It simply holds that the purpose of incarnation and atonement is logically prior to the creation.

**2. Living with God vs. Knowing God**
Plantinga says the goal is _living with God forever_. Anderson points out that the Judas and the Pharisees literally lived with Christ — and it did them no good. Living with someone is not in itself a good.

The correct framing, Anderson argues, is found in **John 17:3**: _"This is eternal life, that they may know you, the only true God, and Jesus Christ whom you have sent."_ The goal is _knowing God_, not merely residing in his presence.

**3. The great-making property is still not fully identified**
Plantinga hasn't yet identified what specifically makes the world with incarnation and atonement the greatest. Incarnation is a means; atonement is a means; free will is a means. What is the _end_ these means serve?

## 4. Anderson's Alternative — The Doxological Solution

Anderson proposes what he calls the **doxological solution** (from _doxa_, Greek for glory):

**The great-making property is the self-revelation of God — the display of the glory of God.**

This is the terminal good to which all other goods (free will, incarnation, atonement, even the fall) are means. It is the _end in itself_.

### How This Works

- God permits the fall — this aims at revealing his **justice** and his **mercy**, in a way that would not otherwise be possible.
- The cross is the supreme event: at a single point, both divine justice (the full punishment of sin) and divine mercy (forgiveness for the guilty) are displayed with maximum intensity.
- Creation as a whole, and all of redemptive history, aims at filling the earth with the knowledge of the glory of God.
  - Isaiah 11:9 — "The earth will be full of the knowledge of the Lord as the waters cover the sea."
  - Habakkuk 2:14 — "The earth will be filled with the knowledge of the glory of the Lord."
- The new heavens and the new earth, the resurrection of the dead — these are not a gnostic escape to a purely spiritual realm but the glorification of creation itself.

### Comparison with Plantinga

| Feature                                     | Plantinga                                                                | Anderson (Doxological)                                                                               |
| ------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Greater good identified                     | Vaguely: free will, or incarnation/atonement                             | Specifically: the self-revelation of God's glory                                                     |
| Terminal good or means?                     | Free will = means; incarnation not fully analysed                        | Glory of God = end in itself                                                                         |
| Problem of "for all we know"                | Partially guilty — doesn't fully specify the greatest good               | Resolves this by naming the specific highest good                                                    |
| Foreknowledge                               | Molinism — God knows what free creatures would do in each possible world | God orders all things to this end; foreknowledge is not "looking into the future" (Calvin on Romans) |
| Free will compatible with divine causation? | No — "if God causes it, it isn't free"                                   | Yes — freedom = doing what you want; God made you and your wants; both can coexist                   |

### On Foreknowledge and Free Will (Calvin vs. Molinism)

Anderson cites Calvin's commentary on Romans: **foreknowledge does not mean God looked into the future to see what someone would freely do and then decided to create them**. That is the Molinist framework Plantinga uses. The alternative: God _made_ things and _ordered_ them to their end — including human free will — without this eliminating genuine human wanting and acting. The difference between "God can't make a triangle with four sides" (a logical impossibility) and "God can't make Adam choose not to eat the fruit if Adam is free" (not obviously a logical impossibility) is a category mistake in Plantinga's framing.

## Summary

| Position                        | Core Claim                                                                                                                                                                                                              |
| ------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Logical problem of evil         | God all-good + all-powerful + evil exists = contradiction                                                                                                                                                               |
| Plantinga's free will defence   | Transworld depravity: any free creature would do at least one wrong thing; therefore God cannot create free creatures without evil                                                                                      |
| Plantinga's greatest story      | Incarnation and atonement is the greatest great-making property; any world with that property contains evil                                                                                                             |
| Anderson's critique             | Free will as "uncaused" is incoherent; free will is a means not an end; living with God ≠ knowing God; incarnation not fully identified as the terminal good                                                            |
| Anderson's doxological solution | The terminal good is the self-revelation of God's glory; evil permits the display of justice and mercy; the cross is the supreme display; all creation aims at the earth being filled with the knowledge of God's glory |
