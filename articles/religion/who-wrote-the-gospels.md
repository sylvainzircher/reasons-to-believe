# Were the Gospels Originally Anonymous?

## Overview

A video essay examining the scholarly debate over whether the four canonical gospels — Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John — were originally written without named authors, with titles only added later in the second century to lend them authority. The presenter surveys the mainstream critical position, then argues at length against it, drawing on recent scholarship to contend that the gospel titles were most likely original and the gospels were never truly anonymous.

---

## 1. The Mainstream Critical Position

The consensus among many New Testament historians today holds that the gospels were composed anonymously and only later attributed to Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John:

- **Bart Ehrman** — argues the gospels were written anonymously and only later ascribed to authors who probably did not write them.
- **David Carr & Colleen Conway** — maintain all four canonical gospels were originally anonymous; the superscriptions attributing authorship were added only in the second century when the gospels were collected together.
- **Dale Martin** — similarly holds the gospels were published anonymously and the names were attached later to link them to disciples (or associates of disciples) of Jesus.

**Christian tradition**, by contrast, holds:

- **Matthew** and **John** — written by direct disciples of Jesus.
- **Mark** — written by a follower and interpreter of Peter.
- **Luke** — written by a traveling companion of Paul.

---

## 2. The Argument from Internal Anonymity — and Why It Fails

The primary reason traditional authorship is denied is that none of the four gospels internally names its author, either in a preface or within the body of the text. But **Simon Gathercole** (2019) argues this is entirely irrelevant to the question of whether the gospels circulated with named titles.

Internal anonymity was the **standard norm** for ancient literary works:

- **Xenophon** did not include his name in the _Anabasis_.
- **Josephus** omitted his name from _Antiquities of the Jews_.
- **Polybius**, **Diodorus**, **Horace**, and Latin authors like **Sallust**, **Livy**, and **Tacitus** did not mention themselves internally in their works.
- **Julius Caesar** wrote his _Commentaries on the Civil War_ entirely in the third person and did not identify himself as author — a parallel to how the author of John's gospel may have written.
- **Elmar Herkimer** noted that omitting one's name from the body of a work was simply the standard practice.

The same holds specifically for **Greco-Roman biography** — the genre most closely matching the gospels:

- **Philo** omitted his name from his biographical works on Abraham, Joseph, and Moses.
- **Plutarch** makes no mention of himself in _Parallel Lives_.
- **Porphyry**, **Philostratus**, **Nepos**, and **Tacitus** (in his biography of Agricola) all wrote without internal self-identification.
- **Lucian** names himself in only one surviving work.

**Lovday Alexander** also notes that ancient technical and medical prologues frequently lack the author's name, and that Luke's prologue closely matches this convention.

**Conclusion:** Internal anonymity is what we should _expect_ of the gospels given the literary culture of their time. Its absence proves nothing about whether named titles accompanied them.

---

## 3. How Ancient Authors Were Identified Externally

Authors were routinely identified through external means rather than within the text itself:

- A title or superscription above the main body
- A subscription at the end
- A table of contents
- A running header
- A name tag attached to the scroll
- An end-title on a title page signed by the author

The gospels would have needed such identification as soon as they were copied into the private library collections of local churches. As **Simon Swain** observed, an author's name was expected to be known from the "usual devices" outside the text itself.

---

## 4. Reasons to Think the Titles Were Original

Several converging lines of evidence suggest the gospel titles were attached from the beginning, not invented later:

**1. Practical necessity**
The gospels would have required identification when copied and distributed to church libraries. This is how all ancient works functioned.

**2. Luke's prologue and Theophilus**
Luke's gospel was sent to a named recipient, Theophilus. It is inconceivable that Theophilus received a gospel-length document with no indication of who wrote it. As **Richard Bauckham** noted, the author's name would have featured in the original title or been known to the dedicatee through the act of presentation.

**3. Circulation and verification**
As gospels spread between churches, recipients would have demanded to know their provenance. The early church father **Papias** explicitly said he gave no stock to any tradition that could not be traced back to a reliable source — a disciple of Jesus or a known elder. Anonymous biographies of Christ would not have passed this standard.

**4. Unanimous and geographically widespread attestation**
Every early Christian source, across the whole Roman world, agrees the four gospels came from Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John. No church father ever attributes them to anyone else, and no gospel manuscript has ever been found bearing a different name:

| Witness                   | Evidence                                                                         |
| ------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **P66** (2nd century)     | Attributes the fourth gospel to John                                             |
| **P4** (2nd–3rd century)  | Attributes the first gospel to Matthew                                           |
| **Clement of Alexandria** | Lists Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John throughout his works                         |
| **Polycrates of Ephesus** | Identifies the Beloved Disciple of John's gospel as John                         |
| **Muratorian Fragment**   | Identifies Luke and John as gospel authors                                       |
| **Irenaeus**              | Names all four gospel authors                                                    |
| **Theophilus of Antioch** | Indisputable testimony to John's authorship                                      |
| **Papias**                | Names Matthew and Mark as gospel authors; information likely from John the Elder |

Additionally, texts from the Nag Hammadi collection (_Acts of Peter and the Twelve_) imply the Beloved Disciple was named John. The _Gospel of Thomas_ singles out Matthew as an authoritative figure on Jesus alongside Peter, hinting he was understood as a gospel writer.

**5. Consistent titles across manuscripts**
**James D. G. Dunn** notes the titles show remarkable consistency across manuscripts — all using the form "The Gospel According to" or simply "According to." If the titles had been assigned independently by different recipients of anonymous writings, we would expect significant variation. The uniformity strongly implies the titles were attached early, before the gospels spread widely.

**6. The names are unlikely forgeries**

- **Matthew** — a tax collector, a despised profession among the Jewish audience the gospel addresses. A forger would not have chosen him. He also plays only a minor role within his own gospel.
- **Mark** and **Luke** — neither was a direct disciple of Jesus. If names were being invented to lend authority, forgers would have chosen apostles, not secondary figures. The gospel of Mark could easily have been attributed to **Peter** (whom tradition says was Mark's primary source) — but was not.

---

## 5. The Hebrews Analogy

**Richard Bauckham** observes that if the gospels were genuinely anonymous in the early period, the patristic debate about their authorship should resemble the debate over **Hebrews** — a work that is truly anonymous:

- **P46** implies Pauline authorship.
- **Tertullian** attributed it to Barnabas.
- **Origen** (via Eusebius) noted uncertainty.
- Others assigned it to Clement of Rome or Luke.

That diversity of attribution — churches proposing competing candidates for an anonymous text — is precisely what we do _not_ find for any of the four canonical gospels. Instead we find **unanimous agreement** with no competing traditions.

---

## 6. Variation in Minor Details Confirms Independence of Tradition

Some church fathers disagreed on the _order_ the gospels were written:

- **Irenaeus** and later fathers: Matthew → Mark → Luke → John.
- **Clement of Alexandria**: Matthew and Luke → Mark → John.

This variation on secondary details demonstrates the traditions did not all flow from a single source — yet all traditions agree on _who_ the authors were. This makes a second-century invention of the names much harder to sustain.

---

## 7. Comparative Attestation

The gospels are **better attested** than many ancient works whose authorship no one seriously questions:

- The best attestation for **Plutarch's** authorship is the _Lamprias Catalogue_, written more than a century — perhaps two centuries — after his death, and falsely attributed to his son.
- The first person to identify **Tacitus** as author of the _Annals_ is **Jerome**, writing over 300 years after Tacitus.

No comparable skepticism is directed at those works.
