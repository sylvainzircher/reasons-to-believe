# The Gospel of Matthew — An Eyewitness Account

## Overview

Of all four gospels, Matthew's authorship is the most contested among modern scholars. Most doubt that Matthew the disciple wrote it. This video argues the opposite: the external evidence unanimously attests to Matthean authorship, internal objections are weak or answerable, and the internal evidence actively supports the conclusion that the author was Matthew the tax collector and eyewitness disciple of Jesus.

---

## 1. External Evidence — Church Fathers

Every early church father who addresses the question attributes the gospel to Matthew:

- **P4 fly-leaf** (c. 200 AD) — "The Gospel according to Matthew."
- **Clement of Alexandria** — explicitly identifies Matthew as author.
- **Irenaeus** — Matthew published a writing of the gospel "among the Hebrews in their own dialect."
- **Tertullian and Origen** — confirm Matthean authorship.
- **Eusebius** — records the tradition that Pantaenus found a Hebrew Matthew among Indian Christians, left there by the apostle Bartholomew.
- **Papias of Hierapolis** — "Matthew put the sayings in an ordered arrangement in the Hebrew language."

The tradition also refers to an earlier Hebrew or Aramaic version of Matthew, which the Greek canonical version may not be a direct translation of. A plausible solution: Matthew originally composed a collection of sayings (possibly related to the Q source) in Aramaic, then later compiled a fuller Greek gospel incorporating Mark and his own earlier material. Craig Blomberg tentatively agrees this is plausible.

A 14th-century Hebrew manuscript by Shem-Tov Ben Isaac preserves Matthew's gospel in Hebrew and does not appear to be a straight translation of the Greek — possibly preserving traces of an older Hebrew tradition. Jerome also reported a Hebrew copy of Matthew at the library of Caesarea.

## 2. Scholarly Objections and Responses

### "Why would Matthew use Mark as a source?"

- Mark preserved the eyewitness testimony of Peter, the leader of the Twelve.
- Matthew would not want to contradict Peter's account, and it would make sense to incorporate testimony from someone who was present for events Matthew was not.
- Charles Quarles: given Peter's prominence, it is not surprising Matthew utilised his account.

### "The Greek Matthew shows no Semitic origin"

- The original Greek character of the canonical Gospel of Matthew is widely accepted.
- However, WD Davies and Dale Allison list roughly five pages of identifiable Semitisms in the Greek Matthew.
- Blomberg notes Matthew's Q passages regularly show signs of careful Semitic parallelism.
- Quarles: "the idea there is no Semitic element in Greek Matthew is ill-founded."

### "Matthew borrowed his own conversion story from Mark (Levi vs Matthew)"

- Richard Bauckham argues it is unlikely one person would have two Semitic personal names.
- But Quarles documents several examples of Jews having two Semitic names (e.g. Joseph Caiaphas, Joseph who was called Barsabbas, Simon called Matthias).
- Jesus renamed Simon to Peter and gave James and John the nickname "Sons of Thunder" — he likely renamed Levi to Matthew ("gift of God") as a sign of new identity.
- Matthew 9:9 uses the word "called" (_legomenos_) in a way normally reserved for nicknames, rendered: "a man sitting at the toll booth — Matthew, as he was called."
- Chrysostom and Jerome: Mark and Luke used his birth name Levi to protect his identity from his former life; Matthew himself used his apostolic name.

### "Matthew had formal rabbinic training (Beth Midrash) — incompatible with being a tax collector"

- Quarles: the evidence for formal rabbinic training in Matthew is exaggerated. Nothing in Matthew requires or is best explained by Beth Midrash training. His instruction from Rabbi Jesus and interaction with synagogue leaders was sufficient background.

## 3. Internal Evidence Supporting Matthean Authorship

### Matthew Enhances Mark with Greater Accuracy

- Even Dale Allison (not a supporter of Matthean authorship) acknowledges Matthew sometimes represents the past better than Mark.
- Matthew alone mentions _phylacteries_ — small leather boxes containing scripture worn by Jews — which were in use in Jesus's day.
- Matthew uses the more precise term _tetrarch_ for Herod Antipas; Mark uses the general "king" — Matthew is more historically accurate (Gareth Theissen).
- Matthew gives more sensible context for why Herod did not immediately kill John the Baptist.
- On the clean/unclean foods dispute: Matthew omits Mark's editorial comment "thus he declared all foods clean," doing a better job at representing what Jesus actually meant (Michael Patrick Barber, Gareth Theissen).
- On the robe draped over Jesus: Mark says purple; Matthew says scarlet — which better matches Roman soldiers' garb. Davies and Allison deem Matthew's version more historical.
- On the name change of Simon to Peter: Matthew 16:17–19 preserves an early tradition that corresponds to what Paul says in Galatians 1–2 and alludes to in 1 Corinthians 3:11.
- Jacob Neusner: Matthew's account of Jesus on cleaning the outside of the cup reflects Shammaite Pharisee purity laws originating before 70 AD — from a time when Shammai's school was dominant in Galilee. This saying could only have originated in that pre-70 AD Galilean context.
- Peter J. Williams: Jesus's saying about the Pharisees tithing mint, dill, and cumin reflects specific knowledge of rabbinic debates about tithing these exact plants — precise knowledge consistent with an eyewitness.
- Craig Keener: Matthew adds a line to Mark's Blasphemy of the Holy Spirit account — "but if it is by the Spirit of God that I cast out demons, then the kingdom of God has come upon you" — that is authentic ancient Israelite anthropomorphic language equating the finger of God with his Spirit.

### Matthew's Use of Hebrew

- WD Davies and Dale Allison: the data irresistibly leads to the conclusion that Matthew knew Hebrew.
- Matthew's genealogy uses a _gematria_ structure — three groups of 14 generations — where 14 is the numerical value of the name David in Hebrew (Dalet = 4, Vav = 6, Dalet = 4). The gematria **only works in Hebrew, not in the Greek text**, meaning the author had to have known Hebrew well enough to construct and expect readers to understand it.
- Matthew 1:21 — gives a descriptive definition of Jesus's name to bring out its Hebrew meaning (the Greek form does not carry the meaning "he will save his people from their sins").
- Matthew 2:23 — says the Messiah will be a "Nazarene": a play on the Hebrew word _netzer_ (branch, Isaiah 11) that only works in Hebrew.
- Matthew uses _tote_ ("after this" / "then") about 90 times — far more than the other gospels — reflecting Aramaic connective usage.
- Matthew uses the term "Israel" about 12 times for the Jewish people; John uses it only 4 times. After c. 80 AD, Jews increasingly preferred "Jews" over "Israel." Matthew reflects the earlier usage.
- Matthew uses "kingdom of heaven" (plural in Hebrew, _shamayim_) rather than "kingdom of God" — used over 30 times, unique to Matthew, reflecting Hebrew Old Testament usage.

### Matthew's Special Interest in Money and Finance

- Matthew discusses coinage and monetary issues more than any other gospel.
- On paying taxes to Caesar, Matthew uses the precise phrase "state coin used to pay the poll tax" rather than the generic _denarius_ used by Mark and Luke.
- Matthew alone records the Temple Tax story (two-drachma tax; the stater found in the fish's mouth — accurately recording the coin equivalence).
- Matthew 5:26 — accurately specifies the smallest Roman coin (the copper coin / _lepton_) in a story about paying off debt in prison.
- Matthew 10:29 — mentions an _assarion_ and accurately describes its worth as 1/16 of a denarius.
- Matthew accurately notes that a denarius is equivalent to a labourer's average daily wage.
- Peter J. Williams notes Matthew alone records: the Magi with rich gifts, the parable of hidden treasure, the parable of the pearl, the scribe bringing out old and new treasures, the temple tax, the parable of the unforgiving servant (10,000 talents vs. 100 denarii), the parable of the workers in the vineyard (one denarius), the parable of talents, Judas's betrayal money and what was purchased with it, and the bribe given by the chief priests to the guards at the tomb.

This sustained financial precision is consistent with the author being a tax collector.

---

## Conclusion

The external and internal evidence together form a strong cumulative case for Matthean authorship:

- Unanimous early church attestation.
- Internal objections are weak and answerable.
- The author knew Hebrew and Aramaic at a high level, including using gematria structures only functional in Hebrew.
- The author shows extraordinary precision about coinage, taxes, and monetary transactions — consistent with a tax collector.
- The author enhances Mark with greater historical accuracy in Jewish contexts — consistent with an eyewitness who was present.
- The author preserves early traditions corroborated by Paul's letters.

Matthew likely took Mark's gospel (based on Peter's testimony), enhanced it with his own eyewitness knowledge, his Aramaic source material, and his expertise in Jewish customs and monetary matters to produce a gospel aimed at a Jewish audience.
