# Gospel Reliability — Undesigned Coincidences and the Case for Eyewitness Accounts

## Overview

This is the final video in a series on gospel reliability. After establishing in prior episodes that the gospels are ancient Greco-Roman biographies, that the authors are reliably identified as Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John, that memory and oral tradition were strong in the first-century Jewish context, and that the synoptics date to the late 50s–early 60s AD, this video focuses on two remaining lines of evidence: the gospels' mutual corroboration, and the argument from undesigned coincidences.

---

## 1. Summary of Prior Arguments in the Series

- The gospels fit the genre of ancient Greco-Roman biography, which aimed to preserve historical facts.
- Church fathers unanimously identify the authors as Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John — there is no indication they were ever considered anonymous.
- First-century Jewish culture fostered strong oral memory; the disciples could reliably retain Jesus's teachings for decades.
- Paul's letters contain a gospel tradition already in existence that coheres with all four gospels.
- The synoptic gospels date to the late 50s and early 60s AD.
- Jesus likely taught in Greek on occasion, meaning some original Greek sayings are preserved directly.
- Naming patterns in the gospels and Acts match first-century Judean prosopography in ways fiction writers could not replicate.
- Internal evidence confirms the church tradition of authorship for each gospel.

---

## 2. The Gospels Corroborate Each Other

Paul Anderson identifies 31 units of material found in all four gospels, including John. Given the criterion of multiple attestation used in historical scholarship, this broad agreement supports the reliability of these events.

Additionally:

- John has at least 44 memorable sayings in common with Mark.
- John has as many as 13 possible contacts with the Q tradition.
- While John's material is distinctive, it is by no means isolated from the synoptic tradition.

---

## 3. Jesus's Teaching Style and Memorability

Robert Stein demonstrates that Jesus taught in a way specifically designed for memorisation:

- He used overstatements, hyperbole, puns, similes, metaphors, proverbs, riddles, paradoxes, irony, questions, poetry, and parables.
- He employed synonymous, antithetical, climactic, and chiasmic parallelism.
- David Aune notes that 80% of the synoptic material has some kind of poetic form.

David Carr: modern teenagers can memorise hundreds or thousands of lines of song lyrics through repeated exposure — ancient youth in communities structured around memorisation were even more capable. Poetry and rhythmic prose are the means by which words are written "on the heart."

About 35% of the synoptic material is in the form of parables. Scholars are confident the parables go back to the historical Jesus because:

- There is no precedent in Second Temple Judaism for a rabbi using parables at this frequency.
- The early church did not continue the practice of creating parables after Jesus.
- The parables are dissimilar to both pre-Jesus Jewish teaching and post-Jesus church practice — suggesting they originated with Jesus himself.

Stein concludes Jesus must have deliberately organised his teachings into memorisable forms, and that his disciples were expected to retain what he said.

## 4. Undesigned Coincidences

### Definition

An undesigned coincidence is a notable connection between two or more independent accounts that was not planned by those giving the accounts. The accounts fit together like puzzle pieces, each filling in gaps in the other without appearing to do so deliberately.

The concept was revived and developed primarily by **Lydia McGrew** in her book on the topic. Craig Keener also endorses the argument as "effective."

### Anne Frank Analogy

Luke VanderWay illustrates the concept using accounts from survivors of Bergen-Belsen: Hannah Elisabeth Pick-Goslar recalled throwing Red Cross packages over the fence to Anne Frank; Rachel Van Amerongen and Frank Fordor stated that Anne and Margot regularly went to the fence, believing Anne knew someone in the free camp. Neither account is trying to explain the other, but together they give a fuller and more credible picture.

### Gospel Examples

**Luke + John — The Last Supper (Foot Washing)**

- Luke records a dispute at the Last Supper about who was greatest; Jesus says he is "among you as the one who serves."
- John adds that Jesus dressed as a servant and washed the disciples' feet — explaining what Luke's Jesus is referring to.
- Luke in turn explains why John's Jesus felt the need to wash feet: there was a dispute about status.
- Neither account appears to be filling in the other's gaps deliberately.

**Luke + John — Jesus Before Pilate**

- Luke records Pilate asking if Jesus is the king of the Jews; Jesus replies "you have said so," yet Pilate declares him innocent.
- John adds that Jesus said "my kingdom is not of this world" — explaining why Pilate found no guilt.
- Luke in turn provides the accusation from the Jews (that Jesus claimed to be a king) which explains why Pilate asked the question — something John omits.

**John + Mark — "The Cup"**

- In John 18, Jesus says "shall I not drink the cup the Father has given me?" — with no prior explanation in John.
- Mark explains this, showing Jesus praying earlier to "remove this cup" and teaching that the cup refers to giving his life as a ransom.

**Luke — Why Peter Had a Sword**

- Why did the disciples have swords if Jesus was not planning violence? Luke alone explains: Jesus commanded it so that Scripture could be fulfilled.

**Luke — The Healed Ear**

- Jesus claims his servants would fight if his kingdom were of this world — but Peter had just attacked Malchus.
- Luke supplies the answer: Jesus healed the ear, removing the evidence of the attack. John does not record the healing.

**Luke — Matthew's Knowledge of Herod's Court**

- Matthew records Herod speaking to his servants; Luke, without trying to explain this, mentions in chapter 8 that Joanna, the wife of Herod's household manager (Chuza), was a follower of Jesus — explaining how Matthew had inside information from Herod's household.

**Luke + Mark — Mighty Works in Bethsaida**

- Matthew says "woe to you Bethsaida" for rejecting the mighty works done there — yet never explicitly mentions any miracles in Bethsaida.
- Luke places the Feeding of the 5,000 near Bethsaida; Mark reports the healing of a blind man there.
- Mark's account of sending the blind man away from Bethsaida after healing becomes explicable by Matthew's woes against the city.

**John — Why Philip Was Asked About Bread**

- John records Jesus asking Philip where to buy bread for the 5,000; earlier in John, Philip is identified as being from Bethsaida — the very region where Luke says the feeding took place.

**John + Mark — Green Grass / Passover / Barley**

- Mark notes people sat on green grass (unusual for Israel). John explains: the Passover was near — spring, after the winter rains.
- John 6:9 specifies the loaves were barley, which aligns with the barley harvest occurring around Passover.
- Mark says many people were "coming and going" without explaining why; John's Passover setting explains the pilgrim traffic.

**Mark + John — Counting the Men**

- Mark says the crowd sat in groups of hundreds and fifties, making them easy to count.
- John specifies it was specifically the men who were seated — explaining how the other authors knew the estimate of ~5,000 men.

**John — The Temple Accusation in Matthew and Mark**

- Matthew and Mark record the accusation that Jesus claimed he would destroy the temple and rebuild it in three days, without explaining its origin.
- John 2 records Jesus saying "destroy this temple and in three days I will raise it up" — in reference to his body — early in his ministry, explaining where the garbled accusation came from.

**John — The Scribes Coming to Galilee (Mark 3:22)**

- In Mark, scribes travel from Jerusalem to Galilee to denounce Jesus with no prior contact or reason given.
- John's earlier account of Jesus cleansing the temple explains why Jerusalem authorities were already hostile to this Galilean rabbi.

**John — The Messianic Secret in the Synoptics**

- In the synoptics Jesus repeatedly tells people not to reveal he is the Messiah, with no reason given.
- John 6:15 provides the answer: the crowd tried to make him king by force — explaining why Jesus kept his identity quiet.

## 5. What Undesigned Coincidences Do and Don't Prove

Lydia McGrew is careful to clarify the limits of the argument:

- Undesigned coincidences support the **reliability and earliness** of the gospel accounts.
- They do **not** by themselves prove miraculous events occurred.
- They are part of a **cumulative case** — individually they prove little; collectively they form a web of interconnectivity far more consistent with history than with fiction.
- They support the argument that the gospels reflect what actual eyewitnesses claimed, not that stories evolved over time through credulous retelling.
- To argue the resurrection occurred, one must use the full historical case for the resurrection; gospel reliability aids but does not replace that argument.

---

## Conclusion

When the undesigned coincidences are combined with all the other evidence in the series — genre, authorship, oral tradition, dating, naming patterns, and the internal testimony of the texts — the cumulative case for the gospels being reliable eyewitness accounts is strong. The reasonable conclusion is that the gospels are history, not mythology.
