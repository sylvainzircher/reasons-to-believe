## Who is Dr. Thomas Madden?

Dr. Thomas Madden is a professor of medieval history at **St. Louis University**, where he also directs the **Center for Medieval and Renaissance Studies**. He has been teaching for over 30 years, training both undergraduates and doctoral students.

He originally planned to be an astrophysicist but discovered a passion for history through a Western Civilisation class at the University of New Mexico. His specialisation in medieval history was sparked by a one-paragraph synopsis of the **Fourth Crusade** — an event so bizarre it consumed much of his career.

After **9/11**, he became the primary media expert on the Crusades in America, giving hundreds of interviews and writing a piece for _National Review_ correcting widespread misconceptions. Studying church history also gradually drew him back into practising Catholicism, which he had drifted from in college.

## Debunking the Common Myths

The popular image of the Crusades — greedy knights sent by a corrupt church to steal Muslim lands — is largely a **modern invention**, not a historical reality.

### Origins of the Myth

This caricature emerged from:

- **17th–18th century Enlightenment thinkers** like Voltaire and Gibbon, who saw the church as a racket exploiting simple minds
- **19th century Marxism**, which reframed the Crusades as early European colonialism and imperialism

> _"The Crusades were a completely medieval phenomenon that had nothing to do with colonialism."_ — Dr. Madden

### What the Crusades Actually Were

- **Defensive, not offensive.** By 1095, Islam had already conquered two-thirds of the old Christian world over five centuries. Every major crusade was called in direct response to a Muslim attack or territorial conquest.
- **Not motivated by greed.** Crusaders spent **two to three times their annual income** just to participate. Casualty rates were around **50%**. Any booty gained rarely covered the cost of the expedition.
- **Not colonial.** There was no home government, no supply lines, no nation-state backing. Over **90–95% of crusaders went home** after fulfilling their vow. Staying behind was considered a sacrifice, not a prize.

### The Real Motivation: Pious Idealism

Cambridge historian **Jonathan Riley Smith** demonstrated that crusaders were driven by what he called _pious idealism_. Medieval knights:

- Killed people for a living and knew they faced a heavy reckoning in purgatory
- Sought ways to do penance — donations to monasteries, pilgrimages, acts of charity
- Saw the Crusade as a unique opportunity to use their only skill (fighting) as an **act of charity** in service of God
- Were offered a **plenary indulgence** — full remission of temporal punishment for sin — by Pope Urban II

## The Cultural World That Launched the First Crusade

### The Spiritual Foundation

Medieval European society was deeply penitential. The culture of chivalry, developed through monasteries, taught knights that their violence had spiritual consequences. Noble families maintained close ties with monasteries — donating land, sending children to be raised there, and receiving prayers for their souls in return.

### The Trigger

- **1071 — Battle of Manzikert:** The Seljuk Turks defeated the Byzantine army and pushed toward Jerusalem, capturing it from the Arabs and making Christian pilgrimage nearly impossible.
- The **Church of the Holy Sepulchre** had already been deliberately destroyed in 1009 by the Fatimid Sultan Al-Hakim. The Turks refused to allow it to be repaired.
- Byzantine Emperor **Alexius Komnenos**, running out of options, swallowed his pride and appealed to Pope Urban II for help.

### The Council of Clermont, 1095

Urban II brought the plea to a council in Clermont, France, describing in vivid terms the suffering of Eastern Christians. He framed their plight as Christ being crucified again in the suffering of his people. He offered crusaders a plenary indulgence if they would take the vow of the cross and march east.

> _"From a medieval perspective, that was an amazingly good deal."_ — Dr. Madden

## The First Crusade (1096–1099)

### Scale and Structure

- Approximately **30,000–40,000 crusaders** marched
- No kings participated — decisions were made by **council of barons**, which led to constant internal friction
- No supply lines, no home government, no formal command structure
- The only bond holding everyone together was **their shared vow to God**

### The People's Crusade

Before the main army departed, roughly **10,000 followers of Peter the Hermit** — mostly poor knights and peasants — marched ahead. The Turkish Sultan destroyed them almost entirely. Peter the Hermit was reportedly the sole survivor. When the main army arrived, the Turks initially assumed it was more of the same.

### Military Advantage: The Frankish Charge

The Turks had no experience with Western European warfare, particularly the **Frankish cavalry charge**:

- Heavy armoured knights on large warhorses
- Long lances, saddles with stirrups fusing rider to horse
- Entire lines charging simultaneously at full speed

Byzantine Emperor Alexius's daughter, Anna Komnene, wrote: _"The walls of Babylon could not withstand the Frankish charge."_ Against infantry, it was devastating. The Turks eventually developed a counter — light mounted archers — but not in time for the First Crusade.

### The March

The army marched to Constantinople, then across the straits into Turkish territory. Key engagements:

| Event                | Outcome                                                                             |
| -------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Siege of Nicaea      | Captured; returned to Byzantium                                                     |
| Battle of Dorylaeum  | Crusader victory over the Turks                                                     |
| Siege of Antioch     | Taken after almost a year; kept by crusaders after Byzantine emperor abandoned them |
| Capture of Jerusalem | July 1099 — vow fulfilled                                                           |

Dr. Madden describes the whole campaign as _"constantly teetering on the edge of utter disaster — and yet miraculously successful."_

### The End of the Crusade

Once the crusaders prayed at the **Church of the Holy Sepulchre**, their vow was complete. The vast majority went home as heroes. Staying behind to administer the new territories was considered an enormous personal sacrifice.

## The Military Orders: Templars and Hospitallers

### The Knights Templar

Founded in the years after the First Crusade by **Hugh de Payens** and a small group of knights who wanted to devote their lives to protecting Christian pilgrims. The King of Jerusalem gave them the Al-Aqsa Mosque on Temple Mount as their headquarters — which they mistakenly believed was the **Temple of Solomon**, hence the name.

**St. Bernard of Clairvaux** championed them in his tract _On the New Knighthood_, calling knights to live in monastic community while devoted to active military protection rather than cloistered prayer.

Templars took vows of:

- Poverty
- Chastity
- Obedience

They provided a **permanent defensive presence** in the crusader states, built fortifications, raised funds across Europe, and recruited new members.

### The Dissolution of the Templars (14th Century)

King **Philip IV of France**, deeply in debt after expelling Italian bankers and Jews and confiscating their assets, turned on the Templars:

- Accused them of devil worship, sodomy, secret rituals, and keeping demons as pets
- Had them **mass arrested** and **tortured** until they confessed
- Other kingdoms investigated and found no such problems — only France had the issue
- The French Pope **Clement V** ultimately suppressed the entire order
- Their property was confiscated

The conspiracy theories about the Templars were largely **invented by Freemasonry** in the late 17th century, which claimed to be their true spiritual successors and portrayed them as opponents of both kings and popes.

### The Hospitallers

Originally a **hospital order** dedicated to caring for sick and poor pilgrims in Jerusalem — seeing Christ in the poor, providing free care and Christian burial. They later developed a separate military arm.

They built massive hospitals in Jerusalem and constructed castles throughout the crusader states. Unlike the Templars, they survived and exist today as the **Knights of Malta**.

## The Second Crusade (1147–1149)

### Why It Was Called

In 1147, the city of **Edessa** — the first crusader state, established before Jerusalem — fell to Muslim forces and its population was massacred. Pope Eugenius III called a new crusade.

By this time, the First Crusade had become legend — _"their greatest generation."_ The King of France, the German Emperor, and **Eleanor of Aquitaine** all took the cross.

### St. Bernard of Clairvaux's Preaching

Bernard argued that God was offering Christians a magnificent gift — a chance to serve him and receive a plenary indulgence. He said God _"feigns to need our aid"_ as an act of generosity, to give his people the opportunity to be rewarded. His preaching moved crowds to tears. The German Emperor Conrad III fell to his knees weeping after hearing Bernard preach, and declared himself ready to serve.

### Why It Failed

- More than half the crusaders **died in Asia Minor**
- Only a fraction of the force reached the Holy Land
- The surviving leaders made the disastrous decision to attack **Damascus** — which was actually allied with the crusaders
- The siege of Damascus lasted **less than a day** before retreat
- Bernard was scandalized

### The Theological Explanation

Both Christians and Muslims gave the same explanation for the outcome: God was punishing his people for their sins. Just as the Old Testament prophets explained that God used the Babylonians as a scourge, the medieval mind saw military defeat as divine correction.

## The Third Crusade and Fall of Jerusalem

### 1187 — The Fall of Jerusalem

In 1187, **Saladin** defeated the crusaders at the **Battle of Hattin** and shortly after recaptured Jerusalem. The Pope died of a heart attack upon hearing the news. The papal bull issued in response was called _Audita Tremenda_ — "This Tremendous News."

For Europeans, the success or failure of the Crusades had become the **moral barometer of Christian Europe**. Jerusalem's fall was a sign of deep collective sinfulness.

This period saw the rise of the **mendicant orders** (Franciscans and Dominicans), partly as a response to the spiritual crisis — attempts to match the popular piety and zeal of movements like the Waldensians while keeping them within orthodoxy.

### Richard the Lionheart

The Third Crusade is the most famous — **Richard I of England vs. Saladin**. Richard reconquered the entire coastline but could not retake Jerusalem itself, which was inland. He planned to return but died from a battle wound in **1199**.

## The Fourth Crusade (1202–1204)

_This is Dr. Madden's primary area of specialisation._

### Background

Pope **Innocent III** called the crusade immediately upon becoming pope in 1198 — his very first act as pontiff. Northern French barons organised it but refused to march overland through hostile Byzantine territory. They contracted with **Venice** to build a massive fleet.

### The Contract with Venice

- Venice agreed to build **400+ major vessels** — the largest fleet since ancient Rome
- Provisions for the entire army were to be supplied and paid for in advance
- Price was negotiated **per head**, based on an expected **33,500 crusaders plus 10,000 horses**
- The Pope ratified the agreement

### The First Problem: Not Enough Crusaders

Only about **11,000 crusaders** arrived in Venice — one third of the expected number. The fleet was three times too large. Venice had borrowed heavily to build it. The crusaders sat on an island outside the city for months, eating through their provisions, unable to leave.

Word spread that the crusade was in trouble. Other crusaders, hearing reports of men stranded and indebted, simply sailed from other ports directly to the Holy Land.

### The Zara Detour

Venice proposed a compromise: help retake the rebellious city of **Zara** (modern Zadar, Croatia) — formerly Venetian — in exchange for Venice carrying the debt forward. Innocent III sent a letter forbidding the attack under pain of excommunication. The letter arrived just before the assault. They attacked anyway.

> _They had no choice — if they didn't, the fleet went home and the crusade was over._

The entire crusade was **excommunicated**. The rank and file were not told. Leaders sent representatives to Rome to sort it out.

### The Constantinople Detour

While wintering in Zara, a young Byzantine prince — **Alexios IV**, around 13–14 years old — approached the crusade leaders. His father (the rightful emperor) had been blinded and imprisoned by his uncle, who had usurped the throne. Alexios offered:

- Enormous financial payment
- Byzantine military support
- His armies joining the crusade
- Reunion of the Eastern and Western churches

He promised that **the people of Constantinople loved him** and would overthrow the usurper the moment he arrived. The leaders agreed. Most rank-and-file crusaders were opposed — they had taken a vow to go to the Holy Land, not Constantinople.

### Constantinople, 1203

The crusaders — about 8,000–9,000 knights plus 10,000 Venetians with 50 war galleys — sailed to Constantinople. The people did not rise up. The usurping uncle fled. Alexios was installed as emperor alongside his blind father.

He paid **half the promised money**, then reneged entirely and told the crusaders to leave or face destruction.

### The Sack of Constantinople, 1204

After further Byzantine political turmoil (Alexios was strangled; a new hostile emperor was installed), the crusaders attacked the city in 1204. A small group found a gap in the sea walls, slipped through, and the Byzantine defenders — culturally conditioned to accept regime change — assumed a new emperor had arrived. They came out in formal procession to offer the city to the crusade's leader, Boniface of Montferrat.

**The crusaders had no idea what this ceremony meant.** They assumed it meant the city had surrendered and sacked it for three days.

### The Sack

- Constantinople was the **wealthiest city in the world**
- Palaces and homes were looted; owners expelled
- Enormous amounts of **relics** were carried back to Europe — Dr. Madden believes the **Crown of Thorns** (now in Notre-Dame) and the **Shroud of Turin** both came from Constantinople during this sack
- Rape occurred, particularly by Italian merchants who had been expelled from the city and knew where valuables were stored
- Senator **Nikitas Choniates** left a firsthand account of fleeing the city

> _"It's really no exaggeration to say that a good chunk of the relics currently in Europe came from Constantinople."_ — Dr. Madden

### Innocent III's Reaction

Initially hopeful that Constantinople coming under Catholic control was divinely ordained, Innocent III grew furious as details of the sack arrived. He wrote condemning:

- Convents broken into, nuns violated
- Murder of innocents
- Theft from the poor

He noted bitterly: _"You have given the Greeks reason to think that those who follow the pope are worse than dogs."_

### The Long-Term Consequences

- Constantinople's population fell from roughly **one million to around 20,000** within twenty years
- The city never fully recovered and fell to the Ottoman Turks in **1453**, when the Hagia Sophia was converted into a mosque (which it again became in recent years)
- Greeks still remember 1204 — it is regularly raised in Catholic-Orthodox dialogue
- Pope **John Paul II** apologised for the Fourth Crusade in 2004, on its 800th anniversary
- Both Greeks and Latins agreed it was an act of God — each blaming the other's sinfulness

## The Children's Crusade (1212)

### What It Actually Was

> _"The Children's Crusade was not a crusade and was not made up of children."_ — Dr. Madden

The Latin word _pueri_, used by monastic chroniclers, meant "boys" or "lower-class people" — not literally children. It was a **popular uprising of peasants and commoners**, not a formal crusade.

### What Happened

A teenager named **Nicholas of Cologne** attracted thousands of followers who believed that God would give victory to the pure and poor where knights and kings had failed. He claimed God had told him to march to the Mediterranean, where the sea would part and they would walk to Jerusalem.

They marched south along the Rhine, growing in number, celebrated everywhere they went. They crossed the Alps. When they reached the Mediterranean — **the sea did not part**.

Stranded with no money to go forward or return home, many dispersed. Some reportedly accepted free passage from Genoese merchants, only to be sold into slavery in Alexandria. There are still families in Genoa today who claim descent from the survivors.

Innocent III issued a statement saying this was **not a crusade**, while praising the participants' zeal and wishing the knights showed similar devotion.

## Big Picture Conclusions

### What Would Have Happened Without the Crusades?

> _"I think if there had never been a crusade, all of Western Europe would have been conquered by Islamic powers."_ — Dr. Madden

The Islamic world in the Middle Ages was the dominant superpower — wealthy, expansionist, unified by an ideology of jihad. Western Europe was fractious and weak. The Crusades never stopped Muslim expansion, but they **slowed it enough** to give Europe the breathing room it needed to survive.

By the 16th century, under **Suleiman the Magnificent**, Islam controlled all of southeastern Europe, had conquered Hungary, and was making a bid for Vienna. Without the Crusades, Dr. Madden believes Europe would likely have been fully conquered by the 16th century, with Christianity surviving only as a small minority religion — much as the Patriarch of Constantinople exists today in Istanbul, dependent on Turkish governmental approval.

### The Current Situation in Europe

Dr. Madden draws a parallel between the medieval theological explanation for crusade failures (sinfulness and abandonment of God) and the current **de-Christianisation of Europe**. Much of Western Europe has become post-Christian — churches empty or converted to museums, faith reduced to cultural artifact.

However, he invokes Augustine: empires rise and fall, but the City of God is eternal. If Europe no longer wants the faith, the Holy Spirit will go where it is welcomed.

### The One Thing to Know About the Crusades

> _"They were defensive wars that were a devotional practice. They were not colonialist, not imperialist. They were done by men who believed this was what God wanted them to do — and they gave Europe the breathing room it needed to ultimately flourish."_ — Dr. Madden
