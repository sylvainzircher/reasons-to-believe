# Why Does God Allow Evil and Suffering? — John MacArthur at Ligonier

## Overview

A conference lecture by **John MacArthur** at a Ligonier Ministries event. The talk addresses the classic philosophical objection to Christianity — the problem of evil — and argues that Scripture not only provides a satisfying answer but one that leads to heightened worship. MacArthur works through a sequential theological argument, critiques inadequate responses, and lands on the Reformed answer: God ordained the existence of evil for the display of His own glory.

---

## 1. The Question and Its Weight

MacArthur opens by noting this is the single objection that his friend Larry King always returns to as a reason he cannot believe. The standard sceptical syllogism:

> God is all-loving, all-good, all-holy, all-knowing, and all-powerful. Massive evil exists in the world. Therefore the biblical God does not exist.

He dismisses the instinct to deflect to Deuteronomy 29:29 ("the secret things belong to the Lord") as punting. Scripture, he argues, provides not just an escape but a complete and joyful answer.

---

## 2. Three Forms of Evil

MacArthur catalogues evil under three headings:

**Natural evil** — disease, disasters, viruses, tidal waves, volcanic eruptions. He illustrates with the 1918 influenza pandemic (_The Great Influenza_ by John Barry): ~100 million dead in 24 months, caused by a virus that jumped from birds to pigs to soldiers at a Kansas military base, and spread globally via World War I troop movements. Many victims died because their own immune systems — at their strongest between ages 20–40 — killed them in the effort to fight the virus. The world is a very dangerous place to inhabit.

**Moral evil** — internal, personal, spiritual wickedness. Not just headline atrocities (Darfur) but the universal corruption in every human heart. Romans 3: "There is none righteous, no not one." All the thoughts of the heart are only evil continually. Sinful people colliding in broken marriages, rivalries, and wars.

**Supernatural evil** — demonic beings as old as creation. Satan is the prince of the power of the air with a temporary, delegated sovereignty over the world system. Revelation 12 says a third of the holy angels fell and constitute the demonic host. 1 John 5:20 says the whole world lies in their lap. They are primarily the purveyors of false religious systems — "doctrines of demons." MacArthur cites Mormonism as a current example, noting it resembles Hinduism (millions of gods, humans becoming gods) far more than Christianity, with a moralistic façade making it more dangerous.

A fourth form is briefly noted: **the evil of hell** — evil that lasts forever for all who end there.

---

## 3. God Exists and Is Absolutely Sovereign

MacArthur affirms the full biblical doctrine of divine sovereignty: God created everything from nothing, controls everything, and will consummate everything. There is not one molecule out of line with His purposes. Key texts:

- Psalm 115:3 — "He does whatever He pleases."
- Daniel 4:35 — "None can stay His hand."
- Exodus 4:11 — God made man mute, deaf, seeing, blind.
- Psalm 105:16 — God called for a famine on the land.
- 2 Kings 17:25 — God sent lions.
- Lamentations 3:37–38 — "From the mouth of the Most High both good and ill come forth."
- Amos 3:6 — "If there is calamity in a city, will not the Lord have done it?"
- Isaiah 45:7 — "I form light and create darkness, I cause well-being and create calamity."

MacArthur's point: God is not asking to be rescued from responsibility for evil. He takes full responsibility in Scripture. The attempt to protect God from this conclusion is itself a form of blasphemy — creating a diminished deity and calling him the God of the Bible.

He points to the Flood: God personally drowned millions of people and took full responsibility for it. Liberal theology and semi-evangelical theology desperately try to rescue God from this reality. God does not want to be rescued.

---

## 4. Inadequate Answers — and Why They Fail

Several popular attempts to get God "off the hook" are examined and rejected:

**"Adam and Eve are responsible, not God"** — but this only pushes the question back: why did God create beings capable of sinful choices? He created them from nothing; He could have made them otherwise.

**"Satan is responsible"** — same problem: why did God create angels knowing one would fall, become the devil, and lead humanity into sin?

Both answers ultimately circle back to God. You cannot stop the question at man or at Satan.

**Process theology** — God is "in process," developing as history gives Him more information. He is getting better at doing His God-work. MacArthur dismisses this as absurd — it produces a deity less than adequate to the biblical portrayal.

**Openness theology** — God does not know the future because the future cannot be known before it happens. MacArthur notes the incoherence: Psalm 22 describes the crucifixion in explicit detail centuries before it happened. Was the crucifixion a surprise to God? Did He come up with the substitutionary atonement idea only when Jesus was already dying? Isaiah 53 predicts it in detail. The entire concept of a God who reacts to surprises collapses under the weight of biblical prophecy.

**The free will defence** — God prized human autonomy so highly that He had to allow evil to protect it. MacArthur challenges this: it still doesn't explain why God created beings who would use their freedom destructively, knowing in advance they would. Furthermore, a God who would rather humans do their will than He do His is not the God of Scripture. The cultural bias toward democracy and individual rights distorts our theology here — people in medieval or totalitarian contexts understood unilateral sovereignty more clearly.

All of these responses produce a **diminished God** — limited in power, limited in knowledge, or subordinate to human choices. Scripture commands us to exalt God, not diminish Him.

---

## 5. The Answer: God Willed Evil to Exist for His Own Glory

MacArthur's positive answer, grounded in the Westminster Confession:

> "God, from all eternity, did, by the most wise and holy counsel of His own will, freely and unchangeably ordain whatsoever comes to pass. Yet so as thereby neither is God the author of sin, nor is violence offered to the will of the creatures... Sinfulness proceeds only from the creature, and not from God, who, being most holy and righteous, neither can be the author and approver of sin... All that God decrees and providentially brings to pass is all to the praise of His glory."

**The key claim: God is made infinitely more glorious because evil exists.** The question to ask is not "why does evil exist?" but "what does God demonstrate through the existence of evil?"

Scripture identifies three things God displays:

**His righteousness** — Romans 3:5: "Our unrighteousness demonstrates the righteousness of God." The cross is the supreme illustration. God is so righteous that even while fully willing to forgive sinners, He had to pour out infinite punishment on an infinite substitute. The three hours of darkness at the crucifixion may represent the fullness of divine judgment being executed — not the absence of God but His presence in judgment. Without sin, we would never have seen the cross; without the cross, we would never have known how righteous God truly is.

**His wrath** — Romans 9:22: God "endured with much patience vessels of wrath prepared for destruction" precisely to put His holy anger on display. God gets glory from judgment. Without evil, divine wrath would be an abstraction.

**His mercy** — Romans 9:23: God makes "known the riches of His glory upon vessels of mercy." Without vessels of wrath, there are no vessels of mercy. Without sin, the word "mercy" is meaningless. The full range of God's attributes — wrath, mercy, righteousness, love — require evil to be fully and truly known.

MacArthur summarises: we would never know mercy without seeing it contrast with wrath; we would never know love without seeing it triumph over sin; we would never know righteousness without seeing it satisfied at the cross. Evil is the canvas on which the full portrait of God's glory is painted.

The **greatest evil in history** — the crucifixion of the Son of God — was itself ordained by God. Acts 2:23: Jesus was "delivered up by the predetermined plan and foreknowledge of God." Acts 4:27–28: everything Herod, Pilate, the Gentiles, and Israel did to Jesus was "whatever Thy hand and Thy purpose predestined to occur." The worst thing men ever did was planned by God — and became the highest display of His glory.

In Revelation 15, after the full outpouring of divine wrath, the glorified saints sing the Song of Moses and the Lamb: _"Great and marvellous are Thy works, O Lord God Almighty. Righteous and true are Thy ways."_ God's righteous acts in judgment and redemption become the substance of eternal worship.

---

## 6. Job — The Final Word on Asking the Question

Job 38–42: God answers Job not with an explanation but with a cross-examination that exposes the absurdity of calling God's purposes to account.

_"Where were you when I laid the foundation of the earth?"_
_"Have you ever commanded the morning?"_
_"Can you bind the chains of the Pleiades?"_
_"Who cleft a channel for the flood?"_

MacArthur characterises this as a "diatribe against the folly of asking stupid questions and thinking God needs to explain Himself to you."

Job's final response (42:2–6): _"I know You can do all things, and no purpose of Your hand can be thwarted... I had heard of You with the hearing of the ear, but now my eye sees You. Therefore I repent in dust and ashes."_

The encounter with the full sovereignty of God does not produce bitterness — it produces deeper worship.

---

## 7. The Pastoral and Doxological Conclusion

MacArthur closes with the logical consequence: if God were not in control of evil, there would be no guarantee evil would not reappear in heaven. The only reason heaven can be what Scripture describes — no sin, no corruption, no death — is that the same God who ordained evil's existence will sovereignly end it when its purpose is complete.

The choice, he argues, is binary: either you believe in a God who is in complete control of evil, or you believe evil is in control of God and He is reacting the best He can. The second is not the God of Scripture.

The existence of evil — properly understood — does not undermine worship of God; it deepens it. Every attribute of God that is most precious to us (mercy, love, righteousness, grace, justice) is only knowable because evil exists and God has acted to overcome it.
