# Can I Trust the Bible? — A Cold Case Detective Applies the Eyewitness Test

## Overview

This is a full public talk by **J. Warner Wallace** (former cold-case homicide detective, Christian apologist) at MTSU, hosted by The Point Campus Ministry. Wallace applies the investigative framework used to assess eyewitness reliability in criminal trials directly to the Gospel accounts. The talk covers the four-part eyewitness reliability test, circumstantial vs. direct evidence, early dating of the gospels, manuscript transmission, chain of custody, and the absence of bias — followed by a substantial Q&A.

---

## 1. Two Forms of Evidence — Clearing Up a Common Misconception

Every criminal trial in America relies on only two forms of evidence:

- **Direct evidence** — eyewitness testimony.
- **Indirect (circumstantial) evidence** — everything else: DNA, fingerprints, surveillance footage, physical objects.

Common misconception: "It's just circumstantial" implies the evidence is weak. Wallace's response:

- Every case he has worked is circumstantial. He has never had a case with anything other than circumstantial evidence.
- California jury instructions explicitly state: "Both direct and circumstantial evidence are acceptable types of evidence and neither is entitled to any greater weight than the other."
- There is no such category as "hard evidence." DNA is indirect/circumstantial — it is not an eyewitness account.

### The Murder Case Illustration

Wallace walks through a hypothetical murder case, building from zero to conviction purely through circumstantial evidence:

1. Suspect fits the general build.
2. His alibi is contradicted by his friends.
3. His baseball bat has been bleached.
4. His jeans have been spot-cleaned on the thighs.
5. No forced entry — only three people had a key; one is dead, one is the suspect.
6. He admits having a temper, hitting the victim, and threatening to kill her the day of the murder.
7. He has a pair of unusual boots — only 30 pairs sold in the last three years, from one manufacturer — and he owns a pair.
8. He was apparently suicidal the day after the murder, writing an unfinished note about something that "changed his life forever."
9. He owns a 1972 yellow Volkswagen Karmann Ghia matching the witness's description.

The principle: no single piece explains everything, but **the suspect is the one common explanatory cause that unifies every piece of evidence**. The defence can offer eight separate explanations for eight separate pieces — but requiring eight unrelated coincidences to align perfectly is far less reasonable than one explanation that accounts for all of them. This is a **death by a thousand paper cuts** case.

**Standard of proof**: not _beyond a possible doubt_ (anything is possible) but _beyond a reasonable doubt_ — because anything is possible but not everything is reasonable.

## 2. The Four-Part Eyewitness Reliability Test

Derived from the 13 jury instructions judges give jurors when evaluating eyewitness testimony, Wallace distils these into four areas:

1. **Were they present?** — Did the author have access to what they claim to have witnessed?
2. **Can the testimony be corroborated/verified?**
3. **Has the account been corrupted or changed over time?**
4. **Is the witness biased?**

Wallace covers areas 1, 3, and 4 in detail in this talk (area 2 is noted as a separate large lecture, sent as a resource).

## 3. Were They Present? — Dating the Gospels Early

Wallace's concern as a sceptic: if the gospels were written near the Council of Laodicea (363 AD, ~330 years after Jesus), they cannot be eyewitness accounts. The question is whether they were written early enough.

### The Book of Acts as a Dating Anchor

The Book of Acts (written by Luke) describes the early church in detail — yet contains three conspicuous absences:

- **No description of the destruction of the Jerusalem Temple in 70 AD** — a catastrophic event that Jesus predicted, and which Luke would certainly have noted as a fulfilment of that prophecy.
- **No description of the deaths of Peter or Paul** — both of whom die in the 60s and are central figures throughout Acts. Luke describes the death of James son of Zebedee (44 AD) — a far less prominent figure — with no problem.
- **No description of the death of James the brother of Jesus** — who dies in 61 AD and led the Jerusalem church.

The most natural explanation for all three absences: **none of these events had occurred yet when Luke finished Acts**. If Luke wrote Acts conservatively just before the first missing event (the death of James in 61 AD), that places Acts at approximately **60–61 AD**.

### Luke's Gospel Predates Acts

Luke's gospel explicitly says in its opening lines: "In my former book I wrote about everything that Jesus did before his ascension." Acts is the second book; the gospel is the first. Therefore the gospel of Luke was written **before Acts** — placing it at approximately **60 AD or earlier**, possibly as early as **53 AD**.

### Paul Quotes Luke's Gospel as Scripture

- In **1 Timothy** (written c. 63 AD), Paul quotes two texts as "scripture" together: one from Deuteronomy (OT), and one from the Gospel of Luke ("the worker deserves his wages" — Luke 10:7). For Luke's gospel to be quoted as scripture by 63 AD, it must have been written, widely circulated, and accepted well before that.
- In **1 Corinthians** (written c. 53–57 AD), Paul corrects the Corinthian church's practice of the Lord's Supper by reciting back what he taught them when he was there (c. 51 AD) — and the version he quotes matches **Luke's account of the Last Supper**. This suggests Paul had access to Luke's gospel as early as **53 AD**.

### The Optional Words in Luke's Preface — Forensic Statement Analysis

Luke's preface (Luke 1:1–4) contains three optional words (adjectives/adverbs) that reveal important information through what they choose to emphasise:

- **"Carefully"** — Luke writes that he "carefully investigated." He did not need this word. Its presence signals that he is distinguishing his account from an earlier, less careful one. That earlier account is **Mark's gospel** — which is notably shorter and less detailed.

- **"Most excellent" Theophilus** — A term reserved for civic leaders or officials. Luke's dedication reveals that Theophilus was a person of importance and would have known who the author was — making anonymous authorship essentially impossible.

- **"Orderly"** — The Greek word (_kathexēs_) means "in correct chronological order." Why would Luke need to specify this unless there was another early account _not_ in correct order? That other account is again Mark. **Papias** (early bishop) explicitly says of Mark's gospel that it is "accurate if not orderly" — using the _same Greek word_. Luke is signalling he is putting Mark's material into the correct chronological sequence.

These three optional words together demonstrate that Mark's gospel existed before Luke's and that Luke was familiar with it — further anchoring the early dating of both.

### Memory and Significance

Not all memories are equally retained. Ordinary repeated events (routine fishing days, routine Valentine's Days) fade. Singular extraordinary events are remembered in detail. The disciples of Jesus experienced singular, life-defining events — not a routine Tuesday. And once you begin retelling those events repeatedly for years, the memory is rehearsed and consolidated further.

Early dating matters because: **if the gospels are written within 25 years of the events, there are people still alive who would know if the account is false.** You cannot write a lie about events from 25 years ago while eyewitnesses are still living. The further into the future you push the writing, the easier it becomes to fabricate.

## 4. Has the Account Changed? — Chain of Custody

### The Problem of Manuscript Variants

- We have no original (autograph) gospel manuscript.
- We often do not have the first copy, or a copy of the first copy.
- The oldest complete manuscripts date to around the 3rd–4th century.
- When ancient manuscripts are compared, they differ in **thousands of places**.
- Bart Ehrman's provocative statement: "There are more variations among our manuscripts than there are words in the New Testament." — This is technically true.

Wallace's response — the **text message illustration**:
He sends his son David a series of autocorrected, garbled text messages trying to communicate: "meet me at Starbucks on Main Street on Wednesday at 4pm, I'll bring $5,000." Each text has errors. None is inerrant. There are more variations between the texts than there are letters in any one text.

Yet: **you know exactly what he was trying to say** — the business, the day, the time, the location, the amount.

Why? Because the question is not how many variants there are, but **how many manuscripts there are**. Bible translators are not choosing between two documents; they are comparing hundreds and thousands of manuscripts and returning to the original through a process of textual criticism. The volume of manuscripts makes this _more_ reliable, not less.

Additionally, even Bart Ehrman concedes: none of the thousands of variants affects the nature of Jesus, salvation, or any orthodox claim of Christianity.

### The Chain of Custody

Wallace uses the criminal trial concept of **chain of custody** — demonstrating that evidence has not been tampered with by tracing it through every hand that held it — to show the Jesus tradition was not changed over time.

**Starting from John:**

- John writes his gospel.
- John's three personal students: **Ignatius, Polycarp, and Papias**.
  - 7 letters from Ignatius survive — showing what he learned from John.
  - 1 letter from Polycarp (to the church at Philippi) survives.
  - No existing letters from Papias, but **Eusebius** writes about him.
- Ignatius and Polycarp had a joint student: **Irenaeus** — who wrote extensively and already had a list of 24 canonical New Testament books he used with his students. (The canon was not invented at the Council of Laodicea in 363 AD — it was merely _confirmed_ at that point; it had been in use for centuries.)
- Irenaeus's student: **Hippolytus** — wrote before dying in custody.
- Wallace ends the chain here (Origen attended one talk by Hippolytus; this does not constitute a student relationship).

**Key finding**: the Jesus described at the end of this chain — in Hippolytus's writings — is **identical to the Jesus described at the beginning** in John's gospel. There is no detectable drift or evolution toward a more mythologised figure. Every element of Jesus is present early and unchanging through the chain.

Wallace also draws chains of custody from **Paul** and **Peter** in his book, with the same result: if you removed all four gospels and relied only on the first-generation students of the eyewitnesses, you would still arrive at the same Jesus.

### Large-Scale Variants (Textual Artifacts)

The questioner asks about large additions like **John 7:53–8:11** (the woman caught in adultery) and **Mark 16:9–20** (the longer ending of Mark).

Wallace's response on John 8:

- The pericope adulterae does not appear in any 3rd, 4th, or 5th century manuscript.
- When it does appear (6th century), it sometimes appears in Luke rather than John.
- It is probably a late addition ("textual artifact").
- Does this change anything about Jesus, salvation, or Christian doctrine? No.
- Wallace would not preach from it or use it as a proof text.
- He distinguishes **evidence** (what the case rests on) from **artifacts** (things found at a scene that are not part of the case). He builds the case from the evidence; he removes the artifact.
- Inerrancy applies to the **autographs**, which we do not possess. We can return reliably to the autographs through textual criticism — and doing so is itself an honest, evidence-based process.

## 5. Is the Witness Biased? — Motive Analysis

There are only three motives for lying — the same three motives behind any misbehaviour or crime:

1. **Financial greed** — money.
2. **Sexual lust** — relational/sexual gain.
3. **Pursuit of power** — authority, respect, control, self-interest.

If you cannot identify a motive for the lie, you have reason to say it is probably not a lie.

**Applying this to the disciples:**

- Did they get rich from the story? No.
- Did they attract romantic partners? No.
- Did they gain power? This is the most common claim — that they became leaders within a new religious community.

**Paul as the test case:**
Paul (the primary author of the New Testament) started as a respected Jewish authority — so highly placed that he had papers authorising the arrest and execution of Christians. One day he decides to:

- Abandon his existing position of power and authority.
- Join a small, persecuted group.
- Spend 30 years being beaten, imprisoned, and ultimately executed.
- In hopes of someday returning to the position he already had.

This is not a rational path to power. It is the opposite of what a power-seeking liar would choose.

### The Willingness to Die — What It Does and Doesn't Prove

Wallace addresses this carefully:

- A Christian's willingness to die for their faith has **zero evidential value**. Many people die for what they believe without knowing it is a lie (e.g. suicide bombers, cult members).
- But the disciples are in a **different category**: they are the people who would have known whether the resurrection was a lie, because they were there. If they were willing to die for what they personally knew to be false, that is a different calculation entirely — and it has **high evidential value**.

## 6. Q&A — Selected Questions

### The Old Testament

The manuscript evidence for the Old Testament is harder to reconstruct because it is far older. However, Wallace argues this is not fatal to Christian confidence:

- If the New Testament case for the resurrection is sound, then Jesus is in a unique category (a resurrected being has different authority than a normal historical figure).
- Jesus explicitly affirmed the historical reality of Adam, Noah, Moses, Jonah, and other OT figures and events.
- Therefore, Jesus's endorsement of the Old Testament "comes along for the ride" once you establish who Jesus is.
- The OT answers the first two worldview questions (How did we get here? Why is everything so broken?); the NT answers the third (How do we fix it?).

### Non-Canonical/Gnostic Gospels

- Many other gospels exist: Gospel of Mary, Gospel of Judas, Gospel of Philip, Infancy Gospel of James, and many more.
- None pass even the first part of the four-part test. All emerged in the 2nd–5th centuries — they cannot have been written by eyewitnesses.
- The Gnostic gospels lack the accurate regional naming patterns and minor city details of first-century Jerusalem because their authors were not from the region and were writing late.
- Their proliferation is expected: enormous figures always attract legendary accretion (Wallace compares it to the volume of inaccurate Elvis biography that has been published since his death).

### The Book of Mormon, the Quran, and Other Religions

- Wallace applied the four-part test to all major religious scriptures before becoming a Christian, including purchasing and reading the entire LDS canon.
- The Book of Mormon fails quickly at part 1 (dating/eyewitness access) and part 2 (no archaeological corroboration of any touchpoint).
- You are not obligated to examine every theistic worldview before accepting one — just as a prosecutor is not obligated to prove every other person of similar description innocent before convicting the suspect the evidence points to.
- Notably: **every major world religion incorporates Jesus** — the Quran, the Baha'i writings, Buddhism (post-1st century), and Hinduism (post-1st century) all find ways to include or praise Jesus. Jesus never returns the favour — he makes no space for other systems. This asymmetry is itself worth noting.

### The Post-Truth Culture

- Wallace distinguishes **subjective truth claims** (grounded in the subject — "chocolate chip cookies are the best") from **objective truth claims** (grounded in the object — "isoniazid cures TB").
- "My truth / your truth" language applies only to subjective claims.
- "God exists" and "Jesus is the only way to God" are **objective claims** — they may be false, but they cannot be "your truth" or "my truth." They are either true or false, independent of anyone's opinion.
- Treating the gospel like a personal preference (a cookie) rather than a factual claim about reality (a cure) is the core error of post-truth thinking.
- If someone is dying of TB, you do not say "well, Nyquil is your truth" — you argue from evidence that isoniazid is the actual cure. The same applies to the gospel.

### The Relationship Between Evidence and Faith

Wallace distinguishes between:

- **Belief that** — intellectual assent based on evidence (even demons believe _that_ Jesus is who he said he is; they are not saved).
- **Belief in** — personal trust and commitment.

The evidence only gets you to _belief that_. The shift to _belief in_ happened for Wallace when he stopped reading the Bible to examine what it said about Jesus and started reading it to see what it said about _him_ — and found his own spiritual condition described accurately on its pages. The evidence knocked down the barriers; the scriptures did the rest.

**Jesus as an evidentialist:**

- John 10: "If you do not believe what I'm saying, at least believe on the evidence of these miracles" (_ergon_ — the Greek word for evidence).
- Acts 1: Jesus spends 40 days giving "many convincing proofs" (_tekmēria_ — the Greek word for evidence) to the disciples after the resurrection.
- John 20: To Thomas — "Blessed are those who don't have to stick their hand in my chest to know this is true."
- Jesus selected eyewitnesses as apostles; eyewitnesses get to write scripture; eyewitnesses lead the early church. This is an evidentialist enterprise from the beginning.
