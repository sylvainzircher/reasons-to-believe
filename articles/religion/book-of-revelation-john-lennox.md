# The Book of Revelation in an Age of Intelligent Machines — John Lennox

## Overview

A conference lecture by **John Lennox** (Oxford mathematician, Christian apologist and author) introducing his book _God, AI and the End of History_ — an exposition of the Book of Revelation set against the backdrop of the rise of artificial intelligence and global authoritarianism. The lecture was delivered to an audience of pastors, predominantly in Europe. Lennox argues that Revelation is not a cryptic code book to argue over but a pastoral document designed to generate worship, throbbing expectation of Christ's return, and realistic hope — and that its central concerns are urgently relevant to the present age.

---

## 1. Why the Future Matters — Three Unanswerable Questions

Lennox opens with a 2009 observation from the eminent biologist **EO Wilson** (an entomologist):

> "The real problem of humanity is the following: we have Paleolithic emotions, medieval institutions, and godlike technology, and it's terrifically dangerous. Unless we answer those huge questions of philosophy that the philosophers abandoned a couple of generations ago — where do we come from? who are we? where are we going? — rationally, we are on very thin ground."

Wilson's three questions (origin, identity, destiny) map onto past, present, and future. Science can address them partially, but it cannot answer the question of _meaning_ — the question "why?" Secular thinking has produced a world where we understand how almost everything works and know the meaning of nothing.

The Christian faith answers all three through the biblical meta-narrative: creation → redemption → consummation. Pastors are called to give people a **grounded, rational hope** — an expectation for the future that will bear weight and not disappoint.

---

## 2. The Central Christian Hope — The Return of Christ

The heart of Christian expectation is the return of the Lord Jesus. Lennox insists this is not peripheral but central. Before returning to the Father, Jesus promised:

> "If I go away, I will come again and take you unto myself, that where I am, there you may be also." (John 14:3)

Significantly, he did not say "I'll take you to heaven" — the focus is _the person_, not the place. The goal of Christian salvation is to be with Christ, not merely to be in a pleasant location.

Most critically, Jesus made this claim _publicly at his trial_. When challenged by the high priest, he said:

> "You will see the Son of Man sitting on the right hand of God and coming on the clouds of heaven." (Matthew 26:64, echoing Daniel 7)

They crucified him for saying that. His public claim to return — framed in the language of Daniel 7's Son of Man vision — was the heart of his final public witness. Lennox notes: since they crucified him for saying it, the challenge to not be ashamed of it is considerable.

---

## 3. The Framework of Revelation — Parentheses of the Coming

The **structure of Revelation** is given by three mentions of Christ's coming at the start (chapter 1) and three at the end (chapter 22):

**Chapter 1:**

- "Grace from him who is, was, and _is to come_" (God is to come — not merely "will be")
- "Look, he comes with the clouds"
- "I am the Alpha and the Omega, who is to come"

Note: the expected phrasing would be "who is, was, and _will be_" (three tenses of being). Instead it says "is to come" — emphasising that coming is fundamental to God's identity.

**Chapter 22:**

- "Look, I am coming soon" (×3, Jesus speaking)
- Response of the church: "Even so, come Lord Jesus."

The book is bracketed by this theme. Revelation is not primarily about schedules or codes or arguments — it is designed to produce in the reader a _throbbing expectation_: "Come, Lord Jesus. I can't wait." It is also **full of singing**: if you finish it without singing, read it again.

## 4. How to Read Symbolism — "Not Who, But What"

The biggest obstacle to reading Revelation well is naïve dismissal of its symbolism ("it's all fantastical beasts and weird imagery, it must be irrelevant"). Lennox's key principle, learned from **C.S. Lewis**:

> **Metaphors and symbols always stand for a reality.**

The mistake is treating symbols as mere codes to crack ("lampstand = church, got it, moving on"). The correct approach is to ask not _who does this represent?_ but _what does this say about the reality it represents?_

**Example 1 — The Lampstands**
The seven churches are described as lampstands (oil lamps). The question is not "which church is this?" but "what does it say about the church that it is a lamp?" The church is a _source of light and witness_. Some of the churches had their light going out; the letters from Christ are intended to increase their light. And Christ — whose face shines like the sun, who holds seven stars in his right hand — is himself the supreme source of light. Why then does he need lampstand churches? Because he chooses to shine his light _through_ people.

**Example 2 — The Stars**
The angels of the seven churches are identified as stars. Rather than debating whether they are angelic hosts, apostles, or prophets, ask: what does it mean to be a star? Stars throughout history guide and navigate. The New Testament uses stars as images of those who lead others to righteousness. The stars (pastors, leaders, guides) are **held in Christ's right hand**. When John collapsed in fear at the vision, it was the right hand — in which the stars were held — that was placed on his shoulder. Christ was literally holding a star (John himself) at that moment. _You are being held_ by the one who is the greatest star of all.

**Example 3 — The Lion and the Lamb**
The Lion of Judah has overcome — and John turns and sees a lamb as if slain. The point is not to identify these as code names for Jesus but to think about _what it means_ that Jesus is both lion (power, kingship) and lamb (sacrifice, redemption). The symbols convey the reality of what Christ has done and who he is.

## 5. The Seven Churches — What Jesus Cares About

Revelation 1–3 follows a deliberate order: _judgment begins at the house of God_ (1 Peter 4:17). The risen Christ appears as judge (white hair, eyes of flame, sword from mouth) and addresses the churches _before_ the world-scale judgments begin.

Each letter applies specific aspects of the vision from chapter 1 to a specific church. Jesus evaluates every church on three dimensions:

1. **What they believe** (theology — not a dirty word)
2. **How they behave** (ethics consistent with theology)
3. **The temperature of their love for him** (relationship — the foundation of both)

The churches range widely: some are commended with nothing negative to say; others are in desperate spiritual condition with rampant immorality and false doctrine. These were real, simultaneous, contemporary churches — not a nice collection of model congregations. The range applies directly to churches today.

## 6. The Geometry of the Throne — Revelation 4–7

Chapter 4 opens with a throne in heaven. Not a force but a _person_ occupying it. Lennox calls this "the geometry of the throne" — four concentric features: (1) the throne itself; (2) a **green rainbow** encircling it vertically (360°); (3) **24 elders** on 24 surrounding thrones; (4) **seven lamps of fire** (the seven Spirits of God) before it, and a **sea of glass** before the throne.

Each element rewards the same "what?" question:

**The rainbow**
In Scripture, the rainbow is God's sign of limiting judgment (the Noahic covenant). An emerald rainbow _entirely surrounding_ the judgment throne says: _God always limits his judgment_. However fierce the judgments of Revelation become, there is always a restraint around them.

**The 24 elders**
Rather than debating whether these are angels, apostles, or Israel's 12 tribes combined with the 12 apostles — ask what they _are_: they are elders, creatures of _delegated_ authority arranged around the central throne. Like an executive floor of offices surrounding a CEO, they represent a government that _believes in delegation_. God does not do everything himself: he delegates authority to parents, pastors, elders, kings, and workers. This challenges leaders who cannot let go of control, churches that centralise everything in one person, and parents who micromanage.

**The seven lamps / seven Spirits**
The Holy Spirit — sent by the ascended Christ — is the source of light in God's government. God governs by _light_ (transparency), not by darkness. Authoritarian regimes keep people in the dark. God's throne does not. The pastoral application: do you govern by light or by keeping people in the dark? This applies to marriage, family, church leadership, and pastoral ministry.

**The sweep of chapters 4–7**
The section moves from the throne alone (God as creator, praised at the climax of ch. 4) → the Lamb on the throne (redeemer, praised at the climax of ch. 5) → the opening of the seals (wrath of God and of the Lamb) → the sealing of 144,000 and the uncountable crowd before the throne (ch. 7: "the Lamb will be their shepherd and God shall wipe away all tears"). The entire arc is: **creation → redemption → consummation** — the three pillars of the Christian faith, moving like an operatic sweep from beginning to end.

The key question of the whole section: _how does the lone throne at the start become surrounded by an uncountable crowd by chapter 7?_ Answer: the Lamb.

The most moving detail: in chapter 5, John weeps because no one is found worthy to open the scroll. If no one is found worthy, the miserable world will go on forever. Then one is found worthy. Lennox: _"The universe without a lamb is something we are frightened to contemplate. But the universe with a lamb — we are bought for God."_

The praise of the redeemed: "He bought men and women for God." What would you buy for God? Christ bought _you_ — for God. This is the basis of human dignity and pastoral empowerment.

## 7. The Monster — 666 and What It Tells Us

In preparation for the reader to face the coming world power described in Revelation 13, the vision of God's throne (chapters 4–7) provides the contrast. There will be _another_ throne — the throne of the Beast (the "wild monster").

The number 666 is widely speculated about in terms of _who_ it identifies. But the text explicitly says what it is: _"It is the number of a man."_ The monster is a human being with devilish powers — which is more hideous than a supernatural monster. It is the culmination of a world authoritarian regime where technology plays a central role.

Lennox notes this is not hard to believe is coming: the pieces are falling into place. A world government enabled by technology is not fantasy in the 21st century. The whole arc of Revelation prepares the reader not to be destabilised by this but to hold on to the reality of the throne of God and the coming of Christ.

The triumph: when the Lord returns, he will destroy the throne of the monster _by the brightness of his coming_ (2 Thessalonians 2:8).

## 8. Pastoral Applications

Throughout the lecture Lennox draws pastoral applications from each symbol:

- **You are a star being held in Christ's right hand.** However difficult the year, you are being held.
- **Theology, ethics, and love for Christ are the three non-negotiables.** Jesus evaluates every church on all three. Sound doctrine without love is dead. Right behavior without relationship is performance.
- **God believes in delegation.** If you cannot delegate, your church, your marriage, or your family will suffer. God didn't bring your children up for you. He delegated the authority to you.
- **Govern by light.** Do you share what you are doing with those in your sphere of authority, or do you control by keeping people in the dark?
- **You exist because God wanted you to be.** The throne declares: _"Because of your will, they were and were created."_ This is worth preaching to people who feel unwanted and unloved.
- **You have been bought for God.** This is the source of human dignity. Nothing knocks people down more than feeling unvalued — and nothing restores them more than knowing the Lamb purchased them for God.
