# The Gospel of Luke — Based on Eyewitness Accounts

## Overview

Luke was a Gentile convert, a physician, and a travelling companion of Paul. Paul himself did not follow Jesus during his earthly ministry. On the surface this makes Luke appear to be three or more steps removed from Jesus. This video argues the opposite: when the full internal and external evidence is examined, Luke was much closer to the eyewitnesses than commonly assumed, and his gospel is strongly supported as a reliable, historically careful account based on genuine eyewitness testimony.

---

## 1. External Evidence — Church Fathers

The unanimous attestation of the early church is that Luke wrote both the Gospel and the book of Acts:

- **Clement of Alexandria** — confirms Luke as author.
- **Irenaeus, Tertullian, Origen** — all agree.
- **Muratorian Fragment** (c. 150 AD) — "The third Gospel book, that according to Luke. This physician Luke, after Christ's Ascension, since Paul had taken him with him as an expert in the way of the teaching, composed it in his own name."
- **Anti-Marcionite Prologue** — Luke was a Syrian, a doctor, a disciple of the apostles, and later followed Paul until his martyrdom.
- **Paul's letters** (Colossians, Philemon, 2 Timothy) — mention a physician named Luke as a companion of Paul.

Leon Morris: Luke was not a prominent enough figure in the early church for two substantial volumes to be falsely attributed to him. If the tradition were invented, a more prominent apostle would have been chosen.

---

## 2. The Preface — Luke's Historical Method

Luke opens with one of the most polished sentences in the New Testament, explicitly modelling his work on Greco-Roman historiography:

- Greg Sterling: the preface reflects a conscious appropriation of the style of Herodotus, Thucydides, and Polybius.
- Luke claims his information came from **eyewitnesses from the beginning** and **servants of the word** (a term linked to the Hebrew _hazan_, those responsible for preserving the sacred oral tradition in the synagogue).
- Clare Rothchild analysed ancient historical prefaces and found six common claims; Luke uses five of them: truth, accuracy, research from the beginning, order, and reliance on eyewitness testimony.
- Luke uses a Greek word (_parakolouthein_) meaning to obtain complete knowledge, used in juridical and administrative contexts — implying a high degree of confidence in accuracy.
- Eve-Marie Becker and Steve Mason both note the weight of this terminology in ancient historiographical contexts.
- John J. Peters: Luke's preface follows the method of Thucydides, who stressed the importance of eyewitnesses and being an eyewitness from the beginning.

Luke's failure to cite his sources throughout the narrative was standard ancient practice. Thucydides did the same; John Marincola notes this was done to achieve a smooth narrative flow.

---

## 3. Luke as a Travelling Companion of Paul — The "We" Passages

In Acts 16:10, the narrative suddenly switches from third-person to first-person plural ("we"), corresponding to when the author joined Paul. The pronouns return to third person when the author is absent and to first person when he rejoins Paul:

- **We sections**: Acts 16:10–17; 20:5–21:18; 27:1–28:16.
- Colin Hemer: the use of "we" involves a claim to personal participation.
- The most parsimonious explanation is the author was present with Paul for these sections and noted it.

Arguments that the "we" passages come from someone else's diary are rejected:

- Why preserve another person's first-person pronouns in an otherwise seamless narrative?
- Why not insert "we" in the gospel too, to fake eyewitness status?
- The "we" sections align exactly with where church tradition says Luke was present with Paul.

---

## 4. Accuracy in Acts — 84 Confirmed Facts

Colin Hemer examined the last 16 chapters of Acts (Paul travelling through the Gentile world) and found at least **84 facts confirmed by archaeology and external sources**, including:

- Proper names of ports, local industries, border lines, slang terminology, landmarks.
- Local variations in language; proper titles for local officials in each region.
- The governor of Cyprus called a _proconsul_; Philippi's officials called _magistrates_; Thessalonica's rulers called _politarchs_; Ephesus's chief magistrate called the _town clerk_; Malta's ruler called _chief man_.
- Accurate sailing times, distances, directions, meteorological factors, nautical jargon.
- Richard Hanson: Luke's accuracy and vividness in the shipwreck account exceeded Thucydides.

Luke also accurately navigates the **complicated political landscape of Palestine under Herod the Great and his successors** (Archelaus, the Roman prefects, Herod Agrippa I, procurators, Herod Agrippa II) — a political history so complex it trips up even Roman historians, yet Luke depicts it correctly without it being his main focus.

---

## 5. Opportunity to Interview Eyewitnesses

The second "we" section ends with the group arriving in Jerusalem. The narrative then implies the author was separated from Paul for roughly two years while Paul was in Roman custody. This period would have given Luke extended time to interview eyewitnesses in and around Jerusalem — perfectly placed to obtain the material found in his gospel.

---

## 6. Onomastic Congruence

Luke VanderWay's research on naming patterns demonstrates that Luke-Acts achieves **onomastic congruence** — the names in the text match the ratio of popular-to-rare names from first-century Judea, with appropriate distinguishing markers for common names and no need for them for rare ones.

- Apocryphal gospels, various tragedies, and novels lack onomastic congruence.
- Even some Greco-Roman biographies fail to achieve it.
- Only Josephus's autobiography, Plutarch's _Lives_, and Suetonius's _Twelve Caesars_ achieve it — biographies Craig Keener identifies as representing the height of ancient historical reliability.
- **Luke-Acts achieves onomastic congruence to an even greater degree than Mark's gospel.**
- With statistician Jason Wilson, VanderWay demonstrated in a statistically significant way that Luke could not have achieved this naming pattern through fabrication, even with access to Josephus and a large pool of Jewish names.
- The gospels and Acts collectively contain a naming pattern **statistically closer to the general population than Josephus does**.

---

## 7. Semitisms

James Edwards found that the synoptic material **unique to Luke** (not shared with Matthew or Mark) contains **400% more Semitisms** than material shared with Matthew and Mark. This independent material also contains 28 of the 44 named individuals in Luke.

- The Semitic flavour of Luke's independent material indicates it came from Aramaic-speaking eyewitnesses.
- Albert Huck and Adelbert Denaux found Luke intentionally varied his Semitisms and infused them with the rhetorical aims of his prologue.
- Luke typically writes "Simon Peter" in its Hellenised form but switches to the Semitic form when quoting James — a distinction between his own voice and his sources.
- Raymond Martin: wherever Luke adds to Mark's Transfiguration account, the additions are significantly more Semitic — unexpected if Luke was simply inventing material.
- The infancy narrative (including the Virgin Birth) is replete with Semitisms — not a late invention.
- Richard Fellows: Luke uses the Semitic variant of Mary's name throughout, but switches to the Greek form in Luke 2:19 and Acts 1:14 — suggesting those are Luke's own editorial notes, distinct from the Semitic tradition he inherited.
- Steven Farris: the hymns in the infancy narrative are very similar in style to the Septuagint and have an apparent Semitic nature.
- The "we" sections of Acts contain **very few Semitisms** — a clear distinction between Luke's own travel account and the Semitic source material he received on Jesus.

## 8. Medical Language

The church tradition that Luke was a physician is supported by a pattern of medical vocabulary unique to his gospel:

**Historical debate:**

- W.K. Hobart (19th century) documented numerous medical terms throughout Luke-Acts.
- Henry Cadbury (1919) responded that many of these terms appear in other ancient authors (Josephus, Plutarch, Lucian, the Septuagint).
- Cadbury was correct that Hobart overstated his case — but this does not eliminate the argument.

**What can still be argued:**

- Luke uses named illnesses more frequently than documentary papyri (Thomas Graff).
- Annette Weissenrieder updated the argument: focus should be on _how_ Luke uses medical language, not just which terms he uses.
- Luke VanderWay: the _Thesaurus Linguae Graecae_ (11,000+ ancient Greek documents, 125 million words) shows some of Luke's words are used **exclusively by medical writers**.
- Luke 18:25 changes the Greek words for both "eye" and "needle" — this exact combination appears in only three other ancient texts: Galen's medical glossary of Hippocrates, another Galen text, and a text by the physician Orosius.
- Luke uses a Greek word for "upper" in Acts that appears elsewhere **only in medical writings**, yet uses it in a non-medical context — suggesting he used it without thinking.
- Luke's opening sentence parallels the way Hippocrates opens a medical treatise: "As many have undertaken to speak or to write concerning The Healing Arts."
- Luke is the only gospel to record the saying "Physician, heal yourself."
- Luke softens Mark's criticism of physicians in the account of the woman with the issue of blood — making only a clinical statement rather than saying she had "suffered many things from many physicians."
- Luke specifies it was a **high fever** (Matthew and Mark say only "fever") — Physicians of the time distinguished between high and slight fevers.
- Luke says a leper was "full of leprosy" (Matthew and Mark just say "a leper") — a physician noting the advanced state of the disease.
- Luke is the only gospel to record a healing of dropsy, using the correct medical term.
- Luke adds that the Gadarene demoniac went into the desert and lived alone for a long time — consistent with Aretaeus and Silenus Elianus on the disease of mania.
- Luke consistently notes the **duration of illnesses** — critical for ancient medical diagnosis (Corpus Hippocraticum).
- Weissenrieder: "a statement of the duration of the illness, sometimes specifying a concrete number of years, is extremely relevant to the diagnosis of an illness in the Corpus Hippocraticum."
- In the raising of Jairus's daughter, Luke arranged the account to fit an ancient medical description of Hysteria (symptoms: difficulty breathing, inability to eat, paralysis) and may have understood Jesus's statement "she is not dead but sleeping" as literally correct.
- After the raising, Luke adds that Jesus "ordered some food for her" — a feature of Greek medicine.

Comparing Luke to larger corpora like the Septuagint or Josephus is unfair given their much greater length. The fairer comparison is to the other three gospels, which are similar in size and describe the same events — and Luke stands out markedly in his use of medical language.

---

## Conclusion

The evidence forms a strong cumulative case that Luke-Acts was written by Luke the physician, a travelling companion of Paul, who:

- Explicitly claimed to have interviewed eyewitnesses.
- Had a substantial window of time to do so in Jerusalem.
- Wrote with the historical methodology of Greco-Roman historiography.
- Achieved onomastic congruence at a level surpassing even Mark's gospel.
- Received his Jesus-material from Aramaic-speaking eyewitness sources, as evidenced by the Semitisms.
- Wrote with a vocabulary reflecting medical training not found in the other three gospels.
