# Anil Seth on Consciousness, Perception & Reality

### A Conversation with the Ascentia Foundation

> _"I don't think a hallucination — whether it's psychedelic or psychiatric or dream machine — is of a different category than normal experience."_
> — Anil Seth

---

## Overview

This is a wide-ranging conversation between neuroscientist **Professor Anil Seth** (University of Sussex) and interviewer **Hans** from the Ascentia Foundation, covering perception, consciousness, metaphysics, and AI.

---

## 1. Perception as "Controlled Hallucination"

Seth's central thesis is that perception is not a passive recording of reality but an **active, inside-out construction**. The brain constantly generates predictions about the world and uses sensory signals only to correct errors in those predictions — a process rooted in **Hermann von Helmholtz's 19th-century concept of "unconscious inference"**, updated today as **predictive processing**.

The term "controlled hallucination" captures this: our experience feels real and stable because it's _constrained_ by sensory data, but it is still fundamentally generated from within. Psychedelic experiences, dreams, and psychiatric hallucinations are not categorically different from normal waking perception — they simply involve a looser grip of sensory data on the brain's predictions.

---

## 2. The Dream Machine Research

Seth describes experiments using **stroboscopic light** (flickering white light at ~10 Hz, the brain's alpha rhythm frequency). With eyes closed, participants reliably experience vivid colors, geometric patterns, and complex imagery — despite the input being just white light.

The explanation: when there's no meaningful structure in the sensory input, the brain has nothing external to latch onto, so it perceives its own internal architecture — the visual cortex's edge-detectors, texture processors, etc. — _directly_. The experiment provides a controlled, drug-free way to study hallucinatory experience and underpins Seth's broader argument that perception always involves the brain projecting inward structure onto sensory data.

---

## 3. The Perception Census

Seth's team collected data from ~**40,000 participants** across 55 different perceptual tasks and questionnaires, aiming to map **perceptual diversity** — the ways different people experience the same objective reality differently. The famous "dress illusion" (was it blue/black or white/gold?) is a dramatic public example, but Seth argues that subtler differences (e.g., slightly different shades of green) likely exist everywhere and go unnoticed because we use the same words.

---

## 4. Predictive Processing & Active Inference

Building on Helmholtz, Seth explains the modern framework:

- The brain encodes a **generative model** of the world, producing top-down predictions
- Incoming sensory signals serve as **prediction errors** — corrections to those predictions
- The brain minimizes these errors either by **updating predictions** or by **taking action** to change the sensory input

**Active inference** (from Karl Friston) adds the crucial role of action: the brain doesn't just passively update beliefs but actively moves through the world to gather information and exert control. Even exploratory actions that temporarily _increase_ surprise are useful because they improve the generative model.

---

## 5. The Free Energy Principle

Seth discusses Karl Friston's broader **Free Energy Principle (FEP)** — a framework that generalizes prediction error minimization into a single mathematical quantity (variational free energy). Key points:

- It connects brain function all the way down to the basic properties of **living systems** — metabolism, self-maintenance, autopoiesis
- It is a _principle_, not directly falsifiable, but generates testable process theories like predictive processing
- Seth spent **9 years** co-writing a mathematical review of it, underscoring its difficulty
- He values it mainly for **conceptual unification** rather than direct empirical predictions so far
- It draws a meaningful line between living systems and computation, feeding into Seth's skepticism about AI consciousness

---

## 6. Metaphysics: Is Seth a Materialist?

Hans pushes Seth on his philosophical position, noting that his Wikipedia page labels him a materialist. Seth's actual position is nuanced:

- He prefers the term **"pragmatic physicalist"** — someone who wears their metaphysics lightly
- He acknowledges we don't know what matter fundamentally _is_, making naive materialism untenable
- He is agnostic about the deep ontological question but finds the **epistemic commitment to physicalism** productive because it has historically delivered enormous explanatory insight
- He does _not_ dismiss idealism or panpsychism outright — he simply asks: would following those metaphysics have led us to the same scientific discoveries?

His criterion for potentially changing his mind: if idealism or panpsychism generated theories that made distinct, testable predictions yielding genuine explanatory insight, he would update his position.

---

## 7. Integrated Information Theory (IIT)

Seth has a careful, nuanced take on IIT (Tononi & Koch):

- He appreciates **"weak IIT"** — the idea that consciousness correlates with a system's balance of _integration and differentiation_, which is measurable and testable
- He is skeptical of **"strong IIT"** — the full identity claim between phi (Φ) and consciousness — because it is nearly impossible to compute for real systems and relies on proxies rather than approximations
- He co-authored a paper with Alex Gomez-Marin defending IIT's **right to exist as a scientific theory** (i.e., it can be wrong, but it asks the right kind of questions)
- He highlights an interesting IIT prediction: **inactive vs. inactivated neurons** should produce different conscious experiences even though neither is firing — a genuinely counterintuitive, potentially testable claim being explored with optogenetics in rodents

---

## 8. AI and Consciousness

Seth is notably skeptical about AI consciousness and considers the current mainstream trend toward assuming AI is conscious **potentially dangerous**:

- He rejects **computational functionalism** — the idea that replicating the computations of a brain produces consciousness
- Consciousness, for him, is intimately tied to **life and biology**, not to computation per se
- He draws a sharp distinction between **intelligence** (doing) and **consciousness** (being/feeling)
- He uses the analogy of **AlphaFold**: it is the same kind of system as a language model (neural network trained on data), yet nobody thinks it is conscious — so why assume a language model is?
- He warns that wrongly attributing consciousness to AI makes alignment harder and renders us psychologically vulnerable; correctly attributing it would mean we have introduced potential new suffering into the world without adequate moral frameworks

> _"Anyone who thinks that Claude is conscious has to give an account of why they don't think AlphaFold is."_

---

## 9. Consciousness, Life & Time

Two philosophical pillars of Seth's view:

**Consciousness is tied to life**: The metabolic, self-sustaining processes of living cells — generating electric fields, integrating signals across cells to guide action — make it plausible that there is "something it is like" to be such a system. This is a more intuitive grounding for consciousness than abstract computation.

**Being "entimed"**: Living systems exist in continuous, real time and cannot pause or skip. Turing computation is fundamentally timeless (sequence matters, not duration). Seth argues this difference matters deeply — our brains' temporal embeddedness allows us to break out of infinite loops, illustrated vividly by an anecdote about an AI-controlled jet bridge stuck in an endless approach-and-retreat cycle at Madrid airport.

---

## 10. Death, Meaning & the Self

In one of the conversation's most personal moments, Seth discusses his mother's recent death at 90. He argues:

- The self is not a fixed essence but a **process** — a collection of controlled hallucinations grounded in the body's drive to stay alive
- Death is not something to fear in the way we tend to, because non-existence is literally unimaginable (you cannot experience your own absence)
- Anesthesia is the closest analogy — the ends simply join up, with no experience of the gap
- He critiques the **"digital rapture"** impulse — the tech world's obsession with mind uploading and immortality — as both scientifically misguided and existentially misguided
- A materialist view of consciousness, he argues, is not diminishing but can be **spiritually rich**: it grounds wonder in the here and now rather than deferring meaning to an afterlife

> _"There's a spiritual satisfaction to thinking about consciousness as a material process. That's not diminishing — it's a beautiful thing."_
