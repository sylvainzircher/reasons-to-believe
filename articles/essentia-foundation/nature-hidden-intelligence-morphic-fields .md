# Summary: Rupert Sheldrake Interview on Morphic Resonance

---

## Background

Sheldrake is a Cambridge-trained biologist who did his PhD on plant development and research on the plant hormone auxin. He later worked in India on tropical legume crops, during which time he developed his ideas on morphic resonance, published in _A New Science of Life_ (1981).

---

## The Missing Heritability Problem

The Human Genome Project (launched 1987, completed 2000) proved deeply disappointing. Fewer genes were found than expected (~20,000, fewer than a sea anemone and half as many as a rice plant), and genome analysis could only predict 5–10% of inherited traits like breast cancer susceptibility or schizophrenia risk — leaving 80–90% unexplained. This became known as the **"missing heritability problem."** Sheldrake argues much of this gap is explained by morphic resonance.

---

## What Is Morphic Resonance?

The core idea is that **nature has an inherent memory**. Similar self-organizing systems across time "resonate" with one another, so that past patterns influence present ones. Key concepts:

- **Morphic fields** — invisible organizing fields that shape form and behavior at every level of nature (molecules, cells, organisms, societies, ecosystems, galaxies)
- **Morphogenetic fields** — the subset of morphic fields governing biological development and form
- **Collective memory** — each species tunes into the accumulated experience of its predecessors; Sheldrake equates this with Jung's "collective unconscious"
- **Laws of nature as habits** — rather than fixed eternal laws set at the Big Bang, Sheldrake proposes nature operates through evolving habits; successful patterns reinforce themselves through repetition

Genes provide the right proteins (the "building materials"), but morphic fields provide the architectural plan — just as a house needs more than raw materials to assemble itself.

---

## How Does It Work?

Sheldrake is candid: **nobody knows the mechanism**. It is a hypothesis. Proposed frameworks include:

- **David Bohm's implicate order** — a hidden order underlying quantum processes
- **Bernard Carr's extra dimensions** — the additional spatial dimensions of superstring/M-theory could allow similarity-based connections

Sheldrake prioritizes empirical testing over theoretical modeling, drawing an analogy to Faraday, who postulated electromagnetic fields decades before Maxwell's equations explained them.

---

## Key Examples

- **Rat maze experiments** — Harvard rats trained on a water maze improved over generations; rats in Edinburgh and Melbourne (unrelated to the Harvard rats) subsequently learned faster, suggesting collective memory across the species
- **Crystallization** — new chemical compounds are initially hard to crystallize, but become easier worldwide over time; melting points of new compounds tend to rise gradually, which Sheldrake attributes to morphic resonance rather than improved purity
- **Wordle puzzles** — a student researcher found people solve the New York Times word puzzle more easily later in the day, after millions have already solved it — consistent with morphic resonance predictions
- **Times crossword** — reported to become easier the day after publication

## The Brain, Mind, and Memory

Sheldrake argues the brain is a **tuning system**, not a memory storage device — more like a TV set than a video recorder. Key positions:

- Memories are not stored as physical traces in the brain (despite over a century of searching, none have been found)
- The brain tunes into memories across time via morphic resonance
- The mind extends _beyond_ the brain — when you see something, the image is projected outward to where it appears, not constructed inside the head
- Pain in a toe is actually _in_ the toe, not merely referred there from the brain
- Dreams and psychedelic experiences take place in an "imaginal world" — a virtual realm of consciousness beyond the physical brain

---

## The Feeling of Being Stared At

Sheldrake has conducted extensive research on **scopesthesia** — the sense of being stared at. He notes 95% of people report the experience, tens of thousands of controlled experiments have been done, yet it remains taboo in academia because it contradicts the assumption that the mind is confined to the brain.

## Telepathy and Intuition

Sheldrake distinguishes morphic resonance (memory from the past) from presentiment (sensitivity to the future). Telepathy, he argues, operates through morphic resonance within bonded social groups:

- **Dogs anticipating owners' return** — dogs begin waiting at doors/windows well before the owner could be heard or smelled, even when owners return at random times in unfamiliar vehicles
- **Telephone telepathy** — thinking of someone who then immediately calls is the most common modern form

---

## Hierarchical Structure of Fields

Nature is organized as a **nested hierarchy** of morphic fields:

> subatomic particles → atoms → molecules → organelles → cells → tissues → organs → organisms → families → societies → ecosystems → planets → solar systems → galaxies → cosmos

At each level, the whole is more than the sum of its parts, with its own morphic field and memory.

---

## Collective Emotions and Social Fields

Minds are "porous" — we share in collective moods through social morphic fields. Examples include flocking starlings (modeled mathematically as a field), family constellation therapy (inherited family field patterns affecting behavior across generations), football crowds, orchestras, and religious rituals.

---

## Science and Spirituality

Sheldrake has written two books on the scientific investigation of spiritual practices (meditation, prayer, pilgrimage, fasting, chanting, etc.). He argues the evidence shows people with spiritual or religious practices are measurably happier, healthier, and longer-lived. He suggests **militant atheism should carry a health warning** for disconnecting people from beneficial practices.

---

## What Needs to Change

Sheldrake advocates replacing **mechanistic materialism** with a view of nature as alive, organic, and interconnected. His book _The Science Delusion_ (_Science Set Free_ in the US) addresses ten dogmas of contemporary science he argues have been undermined by science itself. He supports retaining the empirical scientific method while shedding its materialist constraints.

## Advice to Young Scientists

- Learn conventional science thoroughly — its methods, terminology, and publication conventions
- Don't expect rapid paradigm change; the institutional system actively preserves the existing one
- Look for opening areas: psychedelic research, consciousness studies, lucid dreaming, near-death experiences
- Seek unconventional funding (private foundations, philanthropists) rather than government agencies
- Use resources outside universities — YouTube, foundations like Essentia — to broaden thinking now
