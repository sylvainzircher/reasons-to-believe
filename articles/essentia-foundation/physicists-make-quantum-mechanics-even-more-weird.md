# "The End of Physics As We Know It?"

### A Summary of the Eensia Foundation's Interview with Brukner, Renner & Cavalcanti

---

## Overview

This document summarises a video by the Eensia Foundation, filmed at a quantum foundations conference in Vienna — celebrating 20 years of IQOQI and 60 years of Bell's theorem. The host interviews three award-winning quantum physicists: **Časlav Brukner**, **Renato Renner**, and **Eric Cavalcanti**, recipients of the **Paul Ehrenfest Best Paper Award** for work that pushes quantum foundations into genuinely unsettling territory.

---

## The Core Problem: Wigner's Friend Meets Bell's Theorem

The conversation builds on two foundational thought experiments.

**Bell's Theorem (1964)** showed that quantum particles cannot have predetermined, hidden properties that explain their correlations — ruling out so-called "local hidden variables." One widely accepted response was the motto coined by Asher Peres:

> _"Unperformed measurements have no results."_

Particles simply don't have definite properties until observed. Many physicists made peace with this.

**Wigner's Friend (1961)** asks something deeper: imagine a friend inside a sealed lab measures a quantum system and gets a definite outcome. To the friend, the measurement is done and real. But to Wigner outside the lab, the friend and the system together are still in quantum superposition — no measurement has "happened" from his perspective. Two observers, one event, two contradictory descriptions.

What these three physicists did was **combine and extend both experiments**, creating scenarios with multiple observers (Charlie, Debbie, Alice, Bob) nested inside and outside quantum "boxes." The result is a new class of **no-go theorems** that are far more disturbing than Bell's alone.

---

## The Key Insight: Performed Measurements May Also Have No Absolute Results

In standard quantum thinking, unperformed measurements have no results — but at least once you _do_ measure something, that outcome is real and agreed upon by everyone. The new theorems challenge even this. As Brukner puts it, the escalation goes:

> _"Unperformed measurements have no results"_  
> → _"Even **performed** measurements may have no absolute outcome."_

This is the central provocation. When you extend Wigner's Friend scenarios and combine them with Bell-type setups, you find that the observed outcome of one observer cannot be treated as a shared, universal fact by another observer — without running into logical contradictions.

## The Three Papers and Their Specific Contributions

### Časlav Brukner — _"A No-Go Theorem for Observer-Independent Facts"_

Brukner developed the original extended Wigner's Friend scenario. If two "Wigners" (Alice and Bob) each have a "Friend" in an isolated lab, and the Friends' outcomes are assumed to coexist as shared facts, Bell-type inequalities follow — which quantum mechanics violates.

**Conclusion:** You cannot assign joint probabilities to the outcomes of different observers without contradiction. Facts, it seems, can only exist _relative to an observer_.

---

### Renato Renner — _"Quantum Theory Cannot Consistently Describe the Use of Itself"_

With Daniela Frauchiger, Renner proved a no-go theorem using only two assumptions:

1. **Universality of quantum theory** — agents can reason about other agents using quantum theory (arbitrary Heisenberg cut)
2. **Transitivity of knowledge** — if I trust that you derived a conclusion using our shared theory, I can use that conclusion too

These two assumptions together lead to contradiction in extended Wigner's Friend scenarios.

**Conclusion:** None of the existing interpretations of quantum mechanics gives a satisfactory answer. The solution likely lies _outside_ the present landscape of interpretations entirely.

---

### Eric Cavalcanti — _"A Strong No-Go Theorem on the Wigner's Friend Paradox"_

With collaborators (including an experimental team), Cavalcanti developed **Local Friendliness (LF) theory** and derived Local Friendliness inequalities. These are **strictly stronger** than Bell inequalities: any violation of an LF inequality implies a Bell violation, but not vice versa.

The two key assumptions are:

- **Absoluteness of Observed Events (AOE)** — an observation, once made, is a real and universal fact (not just a hidden variable, but an _actual performed measurement_)
- **Local Agency** — free choices cannot affect distant events

Crucially, LF inequalities do _not_ assume hidden variables or predetermination. They only assume that observations which _were_ performed have definite, shared outcomes. This closes a loophole in Brukner's original argument.

A proof-of-principle experiment was conducted (using a photon's path as the "observer"), demonstrating the principle — though a convincing test with a genuinely plausible observer remains a future challenge.

---

## The Analogy with Relativity — and Where It Breaks Down

All three physicists compare the situation to special and general relativity. In relativity, different observers disagree on simultaneity, lengths, and time — but crucially, there is still a **shared spacetime manifold**. You can mathematically transform between any two reference frames and reconstruct a coherent global picture. There is a "perspective-neutral description."

In quantum mechanics, under these new theorems, **even that fails.**

|                             | Special / General Relativity          | Quantum Mechanics (Extended Wigner's Friend)        |
| --------------------------- | ------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Observers disagree on       | Coordinates, simultaneity, lengths    | Whether an event occurred at all                    |
| Shared global picture?      | Yes — the spacetime manifold          | No — no known transformation patches views together |
| Events themselves relative? | No — all observers agree events exist | Yes — the events themselves are observer-relative   |

As Cavalcanti summarises: in relativity, all observers agree on _what events exist_ in spacetime, even if they disagree on coordinates. In quantum theory, with these extended scenarios, even the events resist being placed on a shared stage.

---

## What Must We Give Up? The Landscape of Options

The theorems force a choice. At least one of the following long-held assumptions must be abandoned:

- **Absoluteness of observed events** — that a measurement outcome is a universal, shared fact
- **Locality** — that choices made here cannot instantaneously affect distant events
- **Freedom of choice** — that measurement settings can be freely and independently chosen
- **Transitivity of knowledge** — that if I know that you know something, I can use it too

The physicists sketch the landscape of available interpretations:

| Interpretation    | What it gives up                                                   |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Many Worlds       | Absoluteness of observed events (events are branch-relative)       |
| Bohmian Mechanics | Locality (instantaneous nonlocal influences)                       |
| Superdeterminism  | Freedom of choice (all choices are pre-correlated)                 |
| QBism             | Shared intersubjective reality (facts are agent-relative)          |
| Relational QM     | Absolute facts (all facts relative to a physical system)           |
| Retrocausality    | Standard causal order (future choices affect the past)             |
| Collapse models   | Universality of quantum mechanics (collapse is real and objective) |

All three physicists are notably unsatisfied with all options. Renner is most explicit: the answer likely lies _outside_ this entire collection.

---

## The Question of Consciousness and Observers

The conversation repeatedly circles around consciousness, though all three are careful not to over-commit. Wigner's Friend technically requires an "observer" but not strictly a _conscious_ one. What counts as an observation — where to draw the Heisenberg cut — remains unresolved in quantum mechanics textbooks.

Cavalcanti outlines a spectrum of increasingly plausible "observers" for future experiments:

1. Macroscopic objects detectable by the human eye
2. Systems with agency
3. Users of quantum theory (possibly AI)
4. Communities with shared intersubjective agreements
5. Artificial general intelligence

Renner suggests the minimal requirement for his version of the experiment is a system with the information-processing capacity to _use_ quantum theory — meaning a sufficiently powerful AI could in principle serve as the "friend."

Brukner is most philosophically adventurous, suggesting the theorems may point toward a picture in which **subjective experience is the starting point rather than the end product of physics**:

> _"I have this perception — that's the most certain thing in the world... it's much more certain than that atoms somewhere out there exist. So for me, this is a starting point."_

## Renner's Extension: The Black Hole Information Paradox

In his conference lecture, Renner draws a striking parallel between the extended Wigner's Friend paradox and the **black hole information paradox**.

In the black hole scenario:

- **Alice** falls into a black hole, makes measurements inside
- **Wigner** stays outside, collects Hawking radiation
- A third agent (**Ursula**) jumps in later, collects _both_ accounts

Ursula ends up knowing both an X-basis and Z-basis outcome of the same quantum system simultaneously — which quantum mechanics forbids. This is the contradiction.

**Renner's sharp point:** the assumptions that make the black hole information paradox contradictory are _the same assumptions_ already shown to be problematic in pure quantum Wigner's Friend scenarios, with no gravity involved at all. This suggests the black hole information paradox may not require new physics at the quantum-gravity interface — **the contradiction may already be internal to quantum theory itself.**

---

## The Broader Philosophical Stakes

The host frames this as a "silent revolution in the foundations of physics." The physicists use the phrase **experimental metaphysics** — the idea, first associated with physicist Abner Shimony in the context of Bell's theorem, that metaphysical assumptions (about reality, observation, what exists) can be empirically tested and ruled out.

What Einstein set in motion with the EPR paper in 1935 — intending to _refute_ quantum weirdness — instead paved the way for Bell's theorem, which undermined his own realist worldview. These new theorems continue that trajectory.

Not only do unobserved particles lack definite properties — even the act of observation may not produce universally shared facts.

The closing image the host offers, drawn from Lee Smolin, is that _"the universe is nothing but a collection of partial views upon itself."_ Brukner gently corrects this:

> _"The partial views cannot be put together as one God's-eye view... they are not merely partial — they may be genuinely incompatible."_

## Key Terms

| Term                                      | Definition                                                                                                         |
| ----------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Bell's Theorem**                        | Proves that no local hidden variable theory can reproduce all predictions of quantum mechanics                     |
| **Wigner's Friend**                       | Thought experiment showing that two observers can give contradictory quantum descriptions of the same event        |
| **Local Friendliness**                    | A set of assumptions (AOE + local agency) whose violation has strictly stronger implications than a Bell violation |
| **Absoluteness of Observed Events (AOE)** | The assumption that an observed outcome is a universal, shared fact                                                |
| **Heisenberg Cut**                        | The boundary between the quantum system being studied and the classical observer studying it                       |
| **No-Go Theorem**                         | A proof that certain physical models or assumptions are logically incompatible with quantum mechanical predictions |
| **Experimental Metaphysics**              | The field (coined by Shimony) in which metaphysical assumptions are tested via physical experiments                |
| **Transitivity of Knowledge**             | The assumption that if agent A knows that agent B knows X, then A can use X — challenged by Renner's theorem       |
