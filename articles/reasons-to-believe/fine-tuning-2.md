# Fine-Tuning for Intelligent Physical Life — Galaxy, Sun, Earth, Moon (Appendix C, Part 2)

## Overview

Part 2 of Appendix C from _Why the Universe Is the Way It Is_ (Hugh Ross, Reasons To Believe, 2008) extends the fine-tuning catalogue from cosmic-level parameters (Part 1) to the environmental level — the specific conditions required of the galaxy, stellar system, planet, moon, and biosphere to support advanced intelligent physical life capable of a long-lived globally distributed high-technology civilisation.

The document lists **402 parameters** across eight size scales, each with a "too much / too little" explanation of why the parameter must fall within a narrow range. Ross distinguishes **six levels of life** from least to most demanding:

1. Unicellular, low metabolism, brief duration
2. Unicellular, low metabolism, long duration
3. Unicellular, high metabolism, brief duration
4. Unicellular, high metabolism, long duration
5. Advanced life, brief duration
6. Advanced life (intelligent, civilisation-capable), long duration

Parameters for each level become progressively stricter. What follows is a detailed organised summary of all 402 parameters with the core reasoning.

---

## Scale 1: Galaxy Cluster (approximately 99 parameters)

These parameters concern the large-scale cosmic environment surrounding the Milky Way.

**Galaxy cluster type** — If too rich: galaxy collisions disrupt stellar orbits. If too sparse: insufficient gas to sustain star formation long enough for life.

**Galaxy cluster size, density, location** — Too large → gravitational disruption of galaxy structure. Too sparse → insufficient infusion of gas and metals.

**Density of quasars during early cosmic history** — Too few → insufficient cosmic dust for star formation stimulus, deadly radiation unblocked. Too many → excessive dust, star formation too early and too vigorous.

**Timing of hypernovae eruptions** — Too early or too late → wrong heavy element abundances available at the epoch of rocky planet formation.

**Number and masses of hypernovae** — Too few → insufficient heavy element production. Too massive → all metals collapse into black holes and are unavailable to future stars.

**Quantity of molecular hydrogen formed by Pop III supernovae** — Controls the rate and timing of the second generation of star formation.

**Intergalactic photon density** — Controls optical depth of the universe; affects both radiation hazards and the production of certain heavy elements needed for life.

**Distance from nearest giant galaxy and Seyfert galaxy** — Gravitational and radiation disruption risks.

**Number density of intracluster clouds** — Controls gas infusion rate into the Milky Way for sustaining star formation.

**Average size of cosmic voids** — Affects structure of the galactic environment and gas availability.

## Scale 2: Galaxy (approximately 200 parameters)

**Galaxy size** — Too large: gas and star infusion disturbs stellar orbits and ignites too many galactic eruptions. Too small: insufficient gas to sustain star formation long enough for life chemistry.

**Galaxy type** — Too elliptical: star formation ceases before sufficient heavy elements build up. Too irregular: radiation episodes too severe.

**Galaxy mass distribution** — Too much in central bulge: life planets exposed to too much radiation. Too much in spiral arms: gravitational instability.

**Galaxy location** — Too close to rich galaxy cluster: gravitational disruption. Too far from dwarf galaxies: insufficient infall of gas and dust.

**Quantity of galactic dust** — Too small: inadequate star and planet formation; too much ultraviolet exposure. Too large: obscures view of galaxy and universe; star formation too early.

**Giant star density** — Too few: insufficient heavy elements and galactic dust. Too many: too much dust, too many early stars, disrupts later stellar environment.

**Star's location relative to galactic centre** — Too close: too much radiation and gravitational disturbance. Too far: insufficient heavy elements for rocky planets.

**Star distance from corotation circle** — The corotation circle is where a star's orbital speed matches the rotation of the spiral arms, minimising the number of spiral arm crossings (each crossing brings radiation and gravitational hazards). Earth's sun is located just inside this circle — one of the most stable orbital positions in the galaxy.

**Ratio of inner dark halo mass to stellar mass** — Controls corotation distance and thus radiation exposure.

**Rate of growth of central spheroid** — Controls heavy element flow into the spiral disk and star formation history.

**Decay rate of cold dark matter particles** — Too slow: too few dwarf spheroidal galaxies, disrupting the Milky Way's long-term spiral structure. Too fast: too many dwarf galaxies, destabilising orbits.

**Supernova eruption rate when galaxy is young** — Controls early heavy element production.

**Epoch of peak galaxy merging** — Mergers inject gas and metals but disturb structure; timing must be right.

**Formation rate of molecular hydrogen on dust grains** — Controls early star formation rate.

**Number of medium or large galaxies merging with Milky Way since thick disk formation** — Even one large merger would disrupt the conditions needed for intelligent life.

**Metallicity of galaxy halo** — Controls infusion of metals into the spiral disk.

**Dark matter halo (ordinary and exotic) — diameter and mass** — Controls long-term spiral structure and star formation history.

**Galaxy mass central black hole** — Must be neither too small (insufficient suppression of star formation) nor too large (too aggressive suppression).

**Date of formation of galaxy's central black hole** — Controls when outflows begin suppressing star formation.

**Timing of initiation of s-process element enrichment** — Controls availability of certain heavy elements.

**Galaxy cluster density** — Too low: insufficient merger events to maintain spiral structure. Too high: gravitational disruption.

**Degree of flatness and concentration of light-emitting matter** — Controls spiral structure stability and heavy element distribution.

## Scale 3: Parent Star (Sun) (approximately 140 parameters)

**Star age** — Too old: luminosity changes too quickly. Too young: not yet reached stable burning phase.

**Star mass** — Too great: star burns too rapidly and luminosity changes too fast. Too little: habitable zone too narrow, tidal forces too strong, UV too low for photosynthesis.

**Star metallicity** — Too low: insufficient heavy elements for life chemistry. Too high: radioactivity too intense.

**Star colour** — Too red or too blue: photosynthetic response insufficient.

**Star luminosity change relative to speciation types, rates, and dates** — Must precisely track the rates at which life is speciated and at which the biosphere compensates for the sun's slowly increasing luminosity. This is one of the most precisely constrained parameters — requiring coincidence between biological and stellar timescales across billions of years.

**Star rotation rate and rate of change** — Controls magnetic field and chromospheric emission.

**Star magnetic field and variability** — Too low: insufficient solar wind protection from cosmic rays. Too high: too much X-ray flux.

**Stellar wind strength and variability** — Controls cosmic ray flux reaching Earth's troposphere (cloud formation) and ozone layer.

**Star's carbon to oxygen ratio** — Must be in narrow range for the right balance of element abundances in the planetary system.

**Star orbital eccentricity about galactic centre** — Too elliptical: crosses spiral arms too frequently.

**Star distance from closest spiral arm** — Too small: excessive radiation exposure.

**Z-axis height of star's orbit** — Too large: tidal interactions disrupt planetary orbits.

**Number of stars in the planetary system** — More than one: tidal interactions disrupt life-support planet orbit.

**Number of close stellar encounters and proximity** — Too many or too close: disrupts planetary orbits and comet cloud.

**Star's space velocity relative to local standard of rest** — Too high: disrupts planetary system stability.

**Amplitude and duration of star spot cycle** — Too variable: disrupts climate.

**Proximity of solar nebula to various supernova types** — Must be close enough to receive heavy elements (iron-60, etc.) but not so close as to destroy the nebula.

**Timing of solar nebula formation relative to nearby supernovae** — Narrow window for incorporation of radioactive elements essential for long-term plate tectonics.

**Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon abundance in solar nebula** — Controls asteroid formation and heavy element delivery to Earth.

**Gas dispersal rate from birthing star cluster** — Too slow: too many stars form nearby, disrupting orbits. Too fast: insufficient material for planet formation.

**Pebble density in protoplanetary disk** — Controls planet formation efficiency.

**Intensity of far ultraviolet radiation from nearby stars during disk condensation** — Controls sizes of gas giants and Kuiper Belt.

**Outer protoplanetary disk lifetime** — Too short: gas giants don't migrate enough. Too long: too much inward migration.

**Outer radius of the protoplanetary "dead zone"** — Controls where gas giants form.

**Cooling efficiency of protoplanetary disk** — Controls giant planet formation.

**Star's primordial gas-dust disk mass and longevity** — Controls planet formation.

**Average magnetic energy density of quiet solar photosphere** — Controls solar corona heating and UV radiation.

**Number density of solar surface spicules** — Controls mass transfer to corona.

## Scale 4: Planetary System (approximately 137 parameters)

**Position and mass of Jupiter** — The most important: Jupiter acts as a gravitational shield, deflecting or capturing comets and asteroids that would otherwise strike Earth catastrophically. Too small: too many impacts. Too large or too close: Earth's orbit destabilised.

**Position and mass of Saturn** — Works with Jupiter; ratio of Saturn to Jupiter mass is constrained.

**Position and mass of Uranus and Neptune** — Control Kuiper Belt structure, late heavy bombardment, and volatile delivery.

**All major planet orbital eccentricities and inclinations** — Must be low enough to maintain near-circular, coplanar orbits for long-term stability.

**Major planet orbital variations and instabilities** — Must remain small over billions of years.

**Inward drift of major planets during formation** — Controls final positions of all planets.

**Distance of gas giants from zones of mean motion resonances** — Resonances can destabilise terrestrial planet orbits.

**Timing of the 1:2 resonance event for Jupiter and Saturn** — This event drove the "Nice Model" migration of the outer solar system, triggering the Late Heavy Bombardment; timing critical for life.

**Initial and final Kuiper Belt mass and orbital distances** — Controls volatile delivery, comet flux, and Neptune's circularization.

**Total mass, mass distribution, and structure of Oort Cloud** — Controls long-period comet flux to inner solar system.

**Asteroidal and cometary collision rate and change rate** — Too many: too many mass extinctions. Too few: insufficient heavy element delivery and too little biosphere disruption to drive speciation.

**Frequency of late impacts by large asteroids and comets** — Some required for mass extinctions that open ecological niches for advanced life; too many destroys it.

**Mass and timing and location of body colliding with primordial Earth (the Moon-forming impact)** — The giant impactor must have been the right mass, right angle, right velocity, right location, right time. This single event determined: (a) Earth's final mass, (b) Earth's axial tilt, (c) loss of primordial atmosphere, (d) Moon mass, (e) Earth's rotation rate, (f) heavy element distribution in Earth's interior.

**Impact energy of moon-forming collision** — Too low: insufficient debris for large moon. Too high: debris disperses too rapidly.

**Angle of collision** — Too glancing: insufficient debris and atmosphere removal. Too head-on: too destructive.

**Velocity of collision** — Tightly constrained.

**Depth of primordial ocean at point of impact** — Affects atmosphere removal efficiency.

**Delivery rate of volatiles from asteroid-comet belts** — Controls water, carbon, nitrogen on Earth.

**Inward migration of icy rubble from outer disk** — Controls water delivery.

**Silicate dust annealing by nebular shocks** — Controls rocky planet and efficient tectonic plate formation.

**Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon abundance** — Controls asteroid formation rates and organic delivery.

**Ratio of asteroids to comets in the Late Heavy Bombardment** — Determines what was delivered to Earth's interior and surface.

## Scale 5: Planet (approximately 268 parameters)

**Distance from parent star** — The habitable zone: too far, water freezes; too close, water boils away. The zone is narrow and has shifted as the sun brightened over time.

**Orbital eccentricity** — Too great: temperature extremes incompatible with life.

**Axial tilt** — Too large or too small: temperature extremes become too great. Earth's 23.5° tilt is stabilised by the Moon.

**Rate of change of axial tilt** — Must be slow enough not to destabilise climate.

**Planetary rotation period** — Too slow: extreme day/night temperature contrast. Too fast: extreme wind velocities.

**Rate of change of rotation period** — Earth's rotation is gradually slowing due to lunar tidal braking; the rate must remain within bounds.

**Magnetic field strength and character of change** — Too strong: electromagnetic storms too severe; too few cosmic rays reach troposphere, reducing cloud formation. Too weak: ozone shield unprotected; too many magnetic reversals for stable civilisation.

**Thickness of crust** — Too thick: too much oxygen transferred from atmosphere to crust. Too thin: too much volcanic/tectonic activity.

**Tectonic activity** — Required for nutrient cycling (silicate-carbonate cycle), continental relief, and atmospheric carbon regulation, but if too vigorous destroys all life.

**Rate of decline in tectonic activity** — Must track the sun's increasing luminosity over billions of years.

**Volcanic activity** — Required to return CO₂ and water vapour to atmosphere; if too much, destroys advanced life.

**Location of volcanic eruptions** — Controls whether nutrients reach the right environments.

**Continental relief** — Controls rainfall distribution, snowpack, and erosion.

**Oceans-to-continents ratio** — Too much ocean: limited diversity. Too little: limited water cycling.

**Global distribution of continents** — Too much in southern hemisphere: seasonal differences too extreme.

**Frequency, timing, and extent of ice ages** — Required for fertile valleys, mineral concentrations, quality harbors; but runaway glaciation fatal.

**Frequency, timing, and extent of global snowball events** — Some may be required for driving evolutionary transitions; too many or too severe is fatal.

**Carbon dioxide, water vapour, and methane in atmosphere** — Must be precisely regulated throughout 4 billion years as solar luminosity increases, the greenhouse effect must decrease to compensate. This requires a complex interplay of life forms, weathering, tectonics, and ocean chemistry operating together.

**Rates of change in CO₂, water vapour, methane throughout history** — The rates of change are as important as the absolute levels.

**Oxygen quantity and rate of change** — Too much: fires destroy all plant life. Too little: advanced animals cannot breathe. Must increase from near-zero to 21% over billions of years at precisely the right pace.

**Planet's oxygenation time** — Too long: advanced life never occurs during the sun's stable phase. Too short: too much oxygen, too many fires.

**Timing of great oxygenation events (three separate events identified)** — Each must occur within a narrow window relative to the sun's luminosity and the history of life.

**Nitrogen quantity in atmosphere** — Buffer gas for oxygen; too much or too little disrupts both respiration and nitrogen fixation for plants.

**Atmospheric pressure** — Too low: liquid water evaporates; weather too extreme; lungs cannot function. Too high: insufficient sunlight; wrong weather patterns; lungs cannot function.

**Atmospheric transparency** — Controls range of wavelengths reaching the surface.

**Surface gravity (escape velocity)** — Too strong: retains too much ammonia and methane. Too weak: loses water vapour.

**Albedo** — Too high: runaway glaciation. Too low: runaway greenhouse.

**Density of planet** — Controls internal heat, tectonics, and magnetic field longevity.

**Magnetic Reynold's number, elasticity of inner core, electromagnetic Maxwell shear stresses, ratio of inner core to shell dimensions** — All control the Earth's dynamo, which generates the magnetic field.

**Core precession frequency** — Controls dynamo operation.

**Rate of interior heat loss** — Controls tectonic activity duration.

**Quantities of uranium, thorium, potassium-40 in inner core, outer core, lower mantle, upper mantle, and crust** — These radioactive elements are the heat source driving plate tectonics and the magnetic dynamo over billions of years. Their distribution must be precisely specified at each depth.

**Viscosity at core boundaries, lithosphere, upper and lower mantle** — Controls convection dynamics and thus tectonics.

**Quantity of water at subduction zones** — Required for subduction to occur efficiently.

**Average pore pressure at subduction zones** — Lubricates fault zones; controls earthquake intensity.

**Rate of sedimentary loading at crustal subduction zones** — Controls silicate-carbonate cycle.

**Seismic activity** — Required for nutrient recycling but if too great destroys life.

**Continental timing of formation** — Must allow marine microorganisms to transform Earth's chemistry before land life appears.

**Depth of primordial ocean** — Affects Moon-forming impact outcome and subsequent water balance.

**Timing of late heavy bombardment** — Delivered heavy elements and oxygen to Earth's core; transformed Earth's crust and mantle chemistry.

**Quantity of mountains on land** — Controls water capture from fog and snowpack; soil building.

**Average height of mountains** — Controls water cycling.

**Average slope of coastlines** — Controls nutrient delivery to continental shelves and ocean circulation.

**Degree of continental land mass barrier to oceans along rotation axis** — Controls rate at which Earth's rotation slows.

**Methane from plants, plant litter, animals, fossil fuel production; methane lifetimes in atmospheric layers** — All must fall in range to prevent both runaway greenhouse and runaway glaciation.

**Timing of continental growth spurts** — Must allow sufficient time for land life to transform soils before the window for advanced life closes.

**Timing of humanity's arrival relative to magnetic reversal** — Too close to a reversal: increased cosmic radiation; civilisational disruption.

**Interval between magnetic reversals during human occupation** — If too short: insufficient time for stable advanced civilisation.

**Availability of fossil fuels** — Too little: burning wood instead releases more greenhouse gases and causes more deforestation. Too much: accelerated fossil fuel use causes more warming.

**Time window between kerogen production peak and appearance of intelligent life** — Too short: insufficient time for geological processes to convert kerogen into petroleum. Too long: bacterial decomposition destroys the petroleum.

**Time window between cistern formation and intelligent life** — Controls petroleum reserves available for technological civilisation launch.

## Scale 6: Moon (27 parameters)

**Number of moons** — One large moon is required.

**Mass of moon** — Too small (less than ~1/3 of current mass): insufficient to stabilise Earth's axial tilt; weak ocean tides. Too large (more than ~2% greater): destabilises axial tilt; ocean tides too extreme; rotation slows too fast.

**Ratio of moon's mass to planet's mass** — Precise.

**Eccentricity and inclination of moon's orbit** — Low values required for stable tidal patterns.

**Tidal force exerted by moon** — Precisely in range for adequate ocean tides, plate tectonic lubrication, and slowing of Earth's rotation to the right rate.

**Moon-forming collision parameters** — Mass, angle, velocity, depth of water at impact, surface air pressure and composition of primordial Earth at time of impact — all constrained.

**Q-value (rigidity) of moon in its early history** — Controls rate of moon's spiral away from Earth.

**Rate at which moon spirals away from planet** — Must remain in range throughout history of life.

**Moon's distance from planet at epoch of intelligent life** — Currently 384,400 km; must be in the right range for axial stabilisation and tidal effects.

**Moon's albedo** — Affects climate.

## Scale 7: Planet's Surface (137 parameters)

Covers all parameters concerning ocean chemistry, soil chemistry, atmospheric chemistry, continent geometry, ice age cycles, and climate — the detailed environmental conditions on Earth's surface that must be sustained over billions of years.

Key categories:

**Soil and crust chemistry**: Cobalt, arsenic, copper, boron, cadmium, calcium, fluorine, iodine, magnesium, manganese, nickel, phosphorus, potassium, tin, zinc, molybdenum, vanadium, chromium, selenium — each must fall in a range. Both deficiency and toxicity are lethal to the biosphere.

**Iron in oceans** — Too little: phytoplankton growth fails; marine food chain collapses; carbon dioxide not removed from atmosphere. Too much: iron poisoning.

**Ozone** — Three separate atmospheric layers (tropospheric, stratospheric, mesospheric) must each be within range.

**Rainfall and erosion** — Average rainfall, variation and timing, average continental slope — all constrained for proper silicate-carbonate weathering and nutrient delivery.

**Continental distribution** — Controls seasonal patterns globally.

**Ice age frequency and extent** — Ice ages are required for valley creation, mineral exposure, and harbour formation that enable advanced civilisation.

**Soil moisture coupling strength** — Controls regional rainfall patterns.

**Clay production quantity and timing** — Clay is essential for soil conditioning for advanced plants, removal of CO₂ from atmosphere, and oxygenation timing. Life forms that produce clay must arrive at the right time.

**Opening of Drake Passage (South America/Antarctica)** — Controls ocean circulation and global temperature; must occur at the right time relative to solar luminosity.

**Heinrich events (iceberg armadas)** — Frequency, intensity, and timing relative to human civilisation.

**Frequency of mega-volcanic eruptions** — Some required for soil fertility renewal; too many destroy advanced life.

**Timing of mega-volcanic eruptions relative to the appearance of humans** — Too close: climate and ozone disruption.

**High latitude precipitation** — Controls high-latitude biomass and ice sheet dynamics.

**Duration of El Niño events** — Affects global rainfall distribution.

**Percentage of planet's surface covered by forests** — Too little: insufficient CO₂ absorption and rainfall generation. Too much: too much CO₂ absorption and cooling.

**Aeration and oxidation of soil** — Controls nutrient availability.

**Quantity of wetland ecosystems** — Controls burial of organic carbon and atmospheric CO₂.

**Poleward heat transport** — Controls climate distribution and storm activity.

**Quantity and extent of forest and grass fires** — Both too few and too many are problematic; fires are part of nutrient cycling.

**Sea salt aerosol density** — Controls cloud condensation nuclei and rainfall.

**Availability of fossil fuels** — See planetary section above.

## Scale 8: Planet's Other Life (Ecosystem) (159 parameters)

This section identifies the specific life forms whose presence, abundance, diversity, and timing must be precisely tuned to make advanced human civilisation possible. It reveals that Ross views the entire history of life on Earth as teleologically structured — not random evolution but a precisely choreographed sequence of biological events preparing the planet for intelligent life.

Key categories:

**Atmospheric chemistry regulators**: Quantity, variety, and timing of methanogens (methanogen bacteria must appear at exactly the right time relative to photosynthetic bacteria — too early causes runaway methane greenhouse; too late, the planet freezes because the faint early sun is uncompensated). Relative abundance of methanogenic vs. photosynthetic life must stay in range.

**Nitrogen cycling**: Anaerobic nitrogen-fixing bacteria (early oceans), large-celled and small-celled nitrogen-fixing cyanobacteria, nitrogen-fixing bacterioplankton, Trichodesmium (including depth distribution and variation), anammox bacteria (anaerobic ammonium oxidation) — all must be present in right quantities at the right times.

**Carbon cycling**: Phytoplankton (quantity, diversity, cell size), marine snow, large-celled sulfur bacteria, sub-seafloor hypersaline anoxic bacteria, aerobic photoheterotrophic bacteria, aerobic and anaerobic ocean bacteria, geobacteraceae, actinide-bioreducing bacteria.

**Oxygenation sequence**: Sulfate-reducing bacteria → iron-oxidising bacteria → cyanobacteria → banded iron formations absorb phosphorus and iron → three great oxygenation events must occur at precisely defined times. The timing of each great oxygenation event is separately constrained.

**Soil ecosystem**: Decomposer bacteria, nitrifying microbes, mycorrhizal fungi (arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi quantity, location, and variation separately constrained), earthworms and soil-dwelling invertebrates, bacteriophages (quantity and diversity both constrained), chlorinated-toxins-consuming bacteria.

**Forest ecosystems**: Quantity, diversity, and distribution of trees and grasses; cicada resource pulses; herbivore and carnivore species diversity and feeding specialisation; plant and animal parasite diversity and quantity.

**Marine chemistry regulators**: Viruses in the oceans (quantity and diversity both separately constrained — viruses control planktonic species balance and nutrient cycling), siderophore-secreting bacteria, phosphonate-mining bacteria, iodocarbon-emitting marine organisms (control marine cloud cover and water cycling), ammonium sulfate aerosols from phytoplankton.

**Methane regulators**: Methanotrophic symbionts in wetlands, marine methanotrophic archaea, endophytic methanotrophic bacteria in freshwater wetlands — all constrained, because methane is a powerful greenhouse gas that must be precisely regulated throughout Earth's history.

**Fungi**: Quantity, diversity, and timing of fungal spread across continental land masses. Fungi are essential for clay production (and thus soil conditioning and CO₂ removal) — and they must colonise the continents at precisely the right time.

**Biogenic bromide regulation**: Rate of release and decomposition of biogenic bromides by marine organisms controls tropospheric ozone, the greenhouse effect, and the stratospheric ozone layer simultaneously. Must be in range.

**Clay-producing life forms**: Quantity, diversity, and timing of introduction of life forms that enhance clay production. Must arrive neither too early (CO₂ reduction would outpace solar luminosity increase, causing global cooling) nor too late (the reverse).

**Bacteriophages**: Quantity and diversity must be in range. Bacteriophages protect advanced life from bacterial diseases; too few means pandemic bacterial disease; too many destroys beneficial bacteria.

**Ecosystem-level requirements**: Prokaryote diversity, eukaryote diversity, level of synergistic interactions among bacterial species, oxygen-tolerant anaerobes (required during the transition from anaerobic to aerobic conditions) — all constrained.

## Summary: The Cascade Structure

Ross's argument has a cascade structure: each level of fine-tuning presupposes the previous level. For intelligent life to exist you need not only the right universe, but the right galaxy cluster, the right galaxy, the right star, the right planetary system, the right planet, the right moon, the right surface conditions, and the right biological history — simultaneously. The probability of all of these being satisfied simultaneously is calculated in Part 3.

The document ends with the observation that the requirements for advanced life are far more demanding than for simple microbial life, and that the Earth appears to have been specifically configured — at every scale from cosmic to microbial — to produce and sustain a technological civilisation.
