# The Mystery of Time — An Interview with Étienne Klein

## Overview

This is a translated summary of a French-language interview with **Étienne Klein**, a French physicist and philosopher of science. The conversation covers the fundamental nature of time — why it cannot be properly defined, the two great philosophical traditions around it, the motor problem, relativistic proper time, the prohibition on time travel, the role of causality, the Big Bang and what came before it, and how our earthly environment systematically misleads us about the laws of the universe. Klein also discusses his recently published book _Transports physiques_ (Gallimard).

---

## 1. What Is Time? — The Definitional Problem

Klein opens with a fundamental observation: **every attempt to define time already presupposes that it exists**, and defines it by reference to itself — which is the opposite of a proper definition. To define time, one would need to refer to more fundamental concepts, and it is not clear such concepts exist.

He cites **Paul Valéry's insight**: we understand each other when we talk about time only because we speak quickly. It is the _sentence_ in which the word "time" is embedded that gives the word its meaning in context. Strip the word from its verbal flow and it becomes a mystery: is it a physical entity? A kind of fluid? A product of consciousness? A cultural construction?

Klein's conclusion: **time imposes itself on us without our being able to say exactly what it consists of**.

---

## 2. Two Philosophical Traditions — and the Paradox of Ancestrality

Philosophers have split into two broad camps on time:

### Camp 1 — Time Depends on Consciousness (Correlationism)

Thinkers including **Saint Augustine, Kant, Hegel, Merleau-Ponty, and Bergson** hold that it is consciousness that _temporalises_ the universe — that our awareness projects a succession onto phenomena that do not have it intrinsically. These are called **correlationist** philosophies: they correlate time to our consciousness of it.

The problem: this position faces the **paradox of ancestrality**. We have learned — primarily in the 20th century — that the universe is at least 13.7 billion years old, while the genus _Homo_ and its consciousness have existed for only a few million years. The universe spent the vast majority of its existence _without us_.

If time depends on the subject, and the subject appeared within time, how did time pass before the subject who makes it pass came into being?

This paradox is widely considered severe enough to support the alternative view.

### Camp 2 — Time Is Independent of Consciousness

Time as a physical entity — represented in physics by the parameter _t_ — is entirely indifferent to whether there is anyone to experience it.

## 3. The Motor Problem — What Drives Time Forward?

Whether or not time depends on consciousness, there is a second unresolved problem: **what is the motor of time?**

- Every present moment, as soon as it arises, is immediately replaced by another.
- What drives this perpetual renewal?
- The response of physicists: _we don't know_.

We draw an arrow on the time axis to indicate it advances — but we cannot say what makes it advance.

A related problem: when we draw a time axis as a **line segment**, we implicitly treat time like space. But there is a critical difference:

- The points of a spatial segment **coexist** — they are all there at the same time.
- The instants of a time segment **do not coexist** by definition — they are a succession, not a simultaneous collection.

This was the central philosophical objection **Bergson** raised when he met Einstein in Paris in 1921: "You physicists treat time as if it were space. But it must be of a fundamentally different nature."

## 4. Relativistic Time — Proper Time, Not "Speed of Time"

Klein is emphatic about a common misrepresentation of Einstein's relativity:

**The wrong popular account**: "The speed of the flow of time depends on the speed of the observer in space." This appears in French high school philosophy textbooks and popular science books, but it is conceptually meaningless.

**Why it is meaningless**: speed is defined as distance per unit time — a derivative _with respect to_ time. To ask for the speed of time is to ask how many hours time covers per hour. The only answer is: 24 hours per 24 hours. It is a tautology.

**What Einstein actually said**: there is no universal time. There is only **proper time** — each observer has their own. Two observers have synchronised proper times as long as their relative velocities are negligible compared to the speed of light. When they are not, their proper times desynchronise.

### The Twin Scenario

Klein describes the classic case: he takes off in a spaceship at a significant fraction of the speed of light, taking books and his own watch. Inside the ship, everything functions normally — the ship is its own inertial reference frame (the principle of relativity: movement is like nothing within your own frame). He reads the same books in the same 2-hour durations he would on Earth.

He returns after 2 years of ship time. His interlocutor has been dead for decades — not because the interlocutor had a shorter life, but because their **proper times desynchronised**.

The key: it is not the _speed of time_ that changed. There are simply **as many proper times as there are reference frames or observers**. Each is equally valid. They become practically indistinguishable only because all humans on Earth move at speeds utterly negligible compared to light.

## 5. Can You Travel Back in Time? — The Causality Prohibition

Klein is unequivocal: **no, you cannot travel backwards in your own proper time**.

Setting aside the psychological impossibility (if you know history from 1800 to 2025, you cannot bear to live in 1800 — the dentist alone would break you), there is a fundamental physical prohibition:

### The Principle of Causality

In its classical form: every event is the effect of a cause that preceded it. Causality imposes a **chronological constraint** — cause precedes effect. This rules out circular time.

**Linear time** (as opposed to cyclical time) means every instant is novel and the same instant can never occur twice. This is a constraint imposed directly by the principle of causality.

In its modern, more refined form, the principle of causality states: **if an event has occurred, it is eternally and irrevocably true that it occurred** — even if there are no witnesses, even if history is falsified, even if it left no trace. The principle protects every past event from being retroactively undone by influences coming from the future.

### The Dirac Equation and Antiparticles

Klein considers the most powerful manifestation of the causality principle:

In the late 1920s, **Paul Dirac** attempted to write equations unifying quantum mechanics (describing small objects) with special relativity (describing fast objects) — to describe cosmic ray particles that are both fast and quantum.

He wrote his equation — and discovered that for the equation to **respect the principle of causality**, it necessarily implied the existence of **antiparticles**: for every particle, there must exist a corresponding antiparticle of the same mass but opposite electric charge.

Antiparticles were subsequently discovered.

**The implication**: antiparticles exist as a direct physical consequence of the principle of causality. Their existence is therefore evidence for the impossibility of travelling backwards in one's own proper time.

Klein's precision: you _can_ travel into someone else's future (by moving at high speed relative to them). You cannot travel backwards in your own proper time. The distinction matters.

## 6. Laws of Physics Are Invariant Under Time Translation

A counterintuitive but important point: **the parameter _t_ in physics equations does not itself depend on time**.

- The equation of gravitation at any instant _t_ is identical to the equation at any other instant.
- The laws of physics are **invariant under time translation**.
- This means the universe evolves — temperatures, densities, and physical conditions change — but **the laws governing that evolution do not change**.

What evolves: the physical conditions (temperature, density, etc.).
What does not evolve: the underlying laws.

This principle underlies the science of the CERN Large Hadron Collider in Geneva: by smashing protons together at very high energies, physicists recreate the physical conditions of the early universe. Because the laws of physics today are the same as in the primordial universe, physicists can observe today what happened in the distant past. This is how the Higgs boson was found — the laws did not change, only the conditions were recreated. This is not time travel but **a recreation of conditions**.

## 7. Before the Big Bang — The Planck Wall and the Open Question

Klein addresses the question of what preceded the Big Bang with careful precision.

### The Old Picture (1950s)

Extrapolating Einstein's equations backwards: the universe gets smaller, hotter, denser, until reaching a pointlike singularity — a zero-size, infinite-temperature origin — to which we assign _t = 0_. This raises the question: what was there before? What was the "spark"?

### Why This Picture Is Incomplete

Einstein's equations describe gravitation only — the dominant force at large scales. In the early universe at extreme temperatures and densities, the other forces (electromagnetism, nuclear forces) also become important. These are described by quantum mechanics — a completely different theoretical framework.

The extrapolation using general relativity alone breaks down at the **Planck time** (the **Planck wall**): the moment in the universe's history before which you must combine general relativity and quantum mechanics to describe what happened. Before that point, purely Einsteinian calculations are **physically wrong** (even if mathematically valid).

Since the 1980s, physicists have been working on theories that marry quantum mechanics and general relativity. These include:

- **Superstring theory**
- **Loop quantum gravity**
- **Twistor theory**

### What These Theories Say About the Singularity

Klein takes superstring theory as an example: it predicts that there is a **maximum possible temperature** in the universe (finite but very large). A temperature of infinity — associated with the _t = 0_ singularity — is therefore physically forbidden.

When superstring theory is applied before the Planck wall:

- There was a **pre-existing contracting universe** (a "before") that was growing hotter.
- It reached the maximum allowed temperature — and could not continue contracting.
- Instead, it **rebounded** (a "bounce"), and the Big Bang becomes not a singularity but a **phase transition between a pre-existing universe and our current one**.

Klein's caveat: superstring theory may well be wrong. But the crucial point is: **whatever theory you choose to describe the universe before the Planck wall**, when you apply it, **the initial singularity disappears**. This is a robust finding across different theoretical approaches.

**The bottom line**: we do not have proof that the universe _has_ an origin, and we do not have proof that it does _not_. The question remains genuinely open.

### The Logical Trap of "Origin"

Klein points out that the word "origin" carries a logical trap: to describe the origin of _X_ means telling the story of what came before _X_, of which _X_ was the conclusion. You can describe the origin of atoms (via stellar nucleosynthesis), because atoms are the conclusion of a prior history.

To describe "the origin of the universe" using the same method, you must describe what came _before the universe_ — but that contradicts the idea of an absolute origin (which would mean nothing preceded it). Therefore:

- If something preceded the universe → the universe has a genealogy, not an absolute origin.
- If nothing preceded the universe → there is no story to tell, and the word "origin" becomes meaningless.

**Moral**: be suspicious of any narrative that begins "In the beginning there was X" — whether X is a primordial ocean, an egg, chaos, the void, or the Word. Either X always existed (in which case there was always something, and the universe has no origin), or X came from something else (in which case X is not the origin).

## 8. The Polysemy of "Time" — A Verbal Mess

Klein and a colleague (Vincent Bontems) organised a 2022 colloquium at Cerisy, inviting physicists, biologists, mathematicians, cosmologists, psychoanalysts, historians, and philosophers. Each participant was asked to give their usual talk **without using the word "time"** — losing a point each time they said it (starting with 20 points).

Result: very few participants reached the end of their talk with any points remaining. The word is so deeply embedded in thought and language that it escapes automatically.

The conclusion: the word "time" is **radically polysemous** — it means at least: duration, causality, simultaneity, succession, change, becoming, speed, and more. This is too many meanings for one word. The word covers so many different concepts that using it without specifying which meaning invites confusion.

Klein proposes — following Paul Valéry — a "cleaning of the verbal situation": a critical examination of the word, asking in each usage precisely what it means. Duration is not the same as time. Temporality is not the same as time. Yet all are referred to with the same word.

The physicist's parameter _t_ does not resemble what most people mean by "time":

- It does not change over time.
- It has no velocity.
- It is not affected by physical events.

If Newton had called his parameter something other than "time" — say, "thingum" — physicists would hold colloquia about "thingum" while biologists, linguists, and others held separate colloquia about "time." The confusion between the physical parameter and the lived phenomenon was arguably a **naming accident**.

## 9. The World Deceives Us About the Universe

The last theme of the interview concerns Klein's book _Transports physiques_ (Gallimard, recently published).

The central idea: we are enclosed in our world (the Earth, its atmosphere, the sky as seen from Earth), and **this world systematically misleads us about the laws of the universe**.

### Galileo and Free Fall

When objects of different mass fall near Earth's surface, they appear to fall at different speeds (due to air resistance). Yet Galileo's law states all bodies fall at the same speed in vacuum — which _contradicts_ direct observation. Galileo announced this law in 1604 when no one could create a vacuum and it was believed vacuum could not exist.

How did he find the right law by contradicting what is directly observable? This is Klein's question. The true laws of physics must be discovered by _escaping the world mentally_ — by finding principles that transcend local conditions.

### The Principle of Relativity

"Movement is like nothing" — within your own reference frame, being in motion is indistinguishable from rest. Yet this contradicts everyday experience: you pay train fares precisely because moving costs effort. The principle only becomes visible when you abstract away the friction and resistance that mask it in our specific world.

### The Violent Universe We Cannot See

From Earth, the sky looks ordered and stable — fixed stars, the Milky Way, a cosmic model of permanence. This was the basis of Greek cosmological thinking.

But if we had particle and radiation detectors, we would see a universe of extraordinary violence:

- Black hole mergers
- Gravitational waves travelling at the speed of light
- Invisible radiation streams crossing space
- A neutrino was recently detected deep in the Mediterranean Sea (off Sicily, at 3,500 metres depth), detected through photomultiplier cables. It came from another galaxy and carried as much energy as a ping-pong ball moving at high speed — a single particle with the energy of a macroscopic object, implying a catastrophically violent source.

All of this violence is **hidden from us** because our visual perception is limited to a narrow frequency band of the electromagnetic spectrum.

**Conclusion**: if Aristotle or Plato had had modern detectors, Greek cosmological thought would have been radically different. The world we inhabit is an exceptionally specific and locally calm location in an otherwise violent universe — and we have mistaken this local calm for a universal condition.
