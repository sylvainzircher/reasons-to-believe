# Time Does Not Exist — An Interview with Marc Lachièze-Rey

## Overview

This is a translated summary of a French-language interview with **Marc Lachièze-Rey**, a French theoretical astrophysicist and physicist. The interview covers the non-existence of time as demonstrated by modern physics — from Einstein's theory of relativity to atomic clocks, GPS systems, and practical applications in altimetry and geological prospecting. It extends to the philosophy of spacetime, the nature of duration vs. time, time travel, the grandfather paradox, and free will.

---

## 1. Who Is Marc Lachièze-Rey?

Lachièze-Rey describes himself primarily as a **theoretical physicist** rather than just an astrophysicist, because doing astrophysics requires mastery of modern physics — both Einsteinian relativity and quantum mechanics. He has written on topics including time travel and the age of the universe (see books section at the end).

## 2. The Flat Earth Analogy — Why We Don't Notice

The non-existence of time is counterintuitive for the same reason the curvature of the Earth is counterintuitive: **at everyday scales, the approximation works perfectly well**.

- When you play football or walk through a city, the ground seems flat. The Earth's curvature is imperceptible at that scale.
- Similarly, at everyday speeds, distances, and durations, time functions as a valid working concept.
- The effects that reveal the **non-existence of time** manifest at the level of roughly **one billionth**, or fractions of a billionth — far beyond the precision of ordinary watches.

A good wristwatch has a precision of perhaps one second per year — roughly one part in a million. The relativistic effects are at the level of one part in a billion or one part in a billion of a billion. Atomic clocks can now detect these differences; wristwatches cannot.

**Conclusion**: in everyday life, you can perfectly well act as if time exists — just as you can act as if the Earth is flat when walking around a city. But that does not mean either belief is fundamentally correct.

Lachièze-Rey therefore says: **time is more a social construction than a valid physical concept**.

## 3. The Evidence — Clocks That Desynchronise

If time existed as a universal background, two perfectly synchronised atomic clocks placed anywhere would stay synchronised indefinitely, since their mechanical precision eliminates drift. **They do not stay synchronised.**

### Example 1 — Altitude

Take two atomic clocks of extraordinary precision (clocks so accurate they would not gain or lose more than one second over the entire 13.8 billion year history of the universe). Synchronise them. Move one up one floor of a building — even a few centimetres — then bring it back. **They are now desynchronised.** They have measured different durations because they have different gravitational environments. Neither is "wrong" — they have each correctly measured the duration of their own history (their own _worldline_).

This is not a failure of the clocks. It is the direct physical demonstration that there is no universal time for them to agree on.

### Practical Application — Atomic Altimetry

This desynchronisation is proportional to altitude differences. Atomic clocks are now being used as **altimeters** — measuring altitude by exploiting the desynchronisation between a reference clock and a field clock. Within 5–10 years, Lachièze-Rey expects all precision altimetry (used in construction, surveying, and terrain studies) to operate this way.

### Application — Geological Prospecting

Gravitational fields vary with the density of material beneath the surface. An underground oil reservoir, water table, or dense mineral deposit changes the local gravitational field, which in turn causes detectable desynchronisation between atomic clocks. This is beginning to be used for **subsurface prospecting** — detecting what lies underground from clock desynchronisation at the surface.

### Example 2 — GPS (Global Positioning System)

GPS works by triangulating from three or more satellites. The system measures the travel time of radio signals from satellites to the receiver, converts that to distance (at 300,000 km/s), and calculates position.

The critical problem: the clocks on GPS satellites and clocks on Earth **do not agree** because the satellites are moving at speed and are in a weaker gravitational field. When NASA first designed GPS, they did not account for this. If you assume clocks on satellites and clocks on Earth show the same time (i.e. that time exists universally), the GPS system accumulates an error of approximately **22 kilometres per day** — making it useless from the first day of operation.

The GPS system was corrected to account for relativistic effects. It works precisely _because_ the non-existence of universal time is built into its design.

## 4. Einstein's Insight — Simultaneity Does Not Exist

Einstein arrived at the non-existence of time not by proposing a theory first, but by analysing practical problems — specifically, **the problem of synchronising railway clocks** across a European network (a major engineering challenge at the turn of the 20th century).

The problem: to tell Berlin that it is noon in Paris, you must send a signal. But a signal takes time to travel from Paris to Berlin. By the time the "it is noon" signal arrives in Berlin, it is no longer noon — it is noon plus a few milliseconds. No signal can be instantaneous; light is the fastest possible signal, and even light takes time to travel.

**Einstein's conclusion**: it is impossible to define a universal synchronisation. No matter what procedure you use, if you and a colleague in Berlin each independently apply the same synchronisation procedure, you will disagree about whether your two events are simultaneous.

This impossibility of defining simultaneity leads directly to a key conclusion: **if simultaneity cannot be defined, time cannot exist**. Because if time existed, "it is noon simultaneously here and in Berlin" would have an objective meaning. It does not.

This argument generalises to any two spatially separated locations — Earth and the Moon, Earth and Mars, Earth and a satellite. "What is happening on Mars right now?" has no answer — not merely because the light hasn't reached us yet, but because **"right now"** has no meaning across space.

## 5. The Michelson-Morley Experiment and the Speed of Light

A separate line of evidence reinforces the non-existence of time: the **constant speed of light**.

From the late 17th century, astronomers knew light travels at a finite speed. By the mid-19th century, it was established that this speed is constant — it does not add to the speed of the emitter. If a plane moving at 1,000 km/h emits a beam of light, an observer on the ground sees the light at exactly 300,000 km/s — not 300,001 km/s.

The **Michelson-Morley experiment** (mid-19th century) confirmed this with high precision. This was deeply disturbing to the physics community because it is **incompatible with Newtonian physics**, which is founded on the existence of absolute space and time.

Yet no physicist before Einstein had the resolve to draw the obvious conclusion: if the constant speed of light is incompatible with the existence of time, then **time does not exist**. They spent half a century offering partial fixes without confronting the central issue.

Einstein, in **1905**, in his theory of special relativity, drew the conclusion: time cannot exist, and we must build physics on something else — **spacetime**.

## 6. Spacetime — Not Just "Space Plus Time"

A common misunderstanding is that spacetime is simply space and time combined. Lachièze-Rey is emphatic: **spacetime is something entirely new and different**. Space and time cannot be separately recovered from spacetime in any observer-independent way.

### What Is Spacetime?

- **Space** is the framework of relations between objects — distances, angles, proximities.
- **Spacetime** is the framework of relations between **events**. An event is something that occurs at a particular location _and_ a particular "coordinate" (what we would ordinarily call "moment" — but that word is already misleading).
- Relations between events in spacetime include: one event causing another, two events being spatially close or distant, and what ordinarily seems like "temporal" ordering — but this ordering turns out to be observer-dependent.

### The Worldline

Each physical object traces a **worldline** in spacetime — a continuous line connecting all the events that constitute its existence from birth to death. You are your worldline.

- The point on your worldline labelled "yesterday" is you yesterday.
- The point labelled "tomorrow" is you tomorrow.
- The point labelled "now" is you now.

What we call the **passage of time** is not time flowing past us — it is **us moving through spacetime**, point by point, event by event. Lachièze-Rey's analogy: when you drive along a motorway from Paris to Lyon, it is not the motorway that passes you — you pass along the motorway. Similarly, spacetime does not flow — you flow through spacetime.

### Duration Is Defined; Time Is Not

What is well-defined in modern physics is not time, but **proper duration** — the length of a worldline between two events on it.

- Duration (proper duration) is real, measurable, and physically meaningful.
- Time — a universal background against which all events are measured — does not exist.
- The **second** is not a unit of time; it is a unit of duration.
- Every clock (pendulum, quartz, atomic) measures duration — specifically, the duration of its own history. It counts oscillations, each of which has a certain duration, and adds them up.
- **No instrument in the world measures time. All instruments measure duration.**

This is Einstein's **clock hypothesis** — originally a hypothesis, now a confirmed affirmation.

## 7. Why Do We Experience Life Moment by Moment?

Lachièze-Rey identifies what he considers **the great unsolved mystery**: why do we experience our worldline point by point rather than all at once?

In spacetime, the event "me yesterday" and the event "me tomorrow" are both points on my worldline. There is nothing in the physical structure of spacetime that distinguishes them — except that I remember yesterday and I do not remember tomorrow.

What makes yesterday different from tomorrow? It is not a physical asymmetry in the structure of spacetime. It is something in memory, in consciousness, in information — and **physics does not answer this question**. Neither does neuroscience or philosophy, at present.

This mystery is, he suggests, what gives us our _illusion_ of temporal flow.

## 8. The Langevin Twins (Twin Paradox)

The classic illustration of proper duration vs. universal time:

Two twins are born and turn 20 on the same day. One stays on Earth; the other travels into space at high speed, turns around, and returns. When they reunite, the traveller is 30 years old and the Earth-twin is 40. They have lived different durations.

Crucially: **both have lived entirely normally**. Each heartbeat, each breath, each meal — all at normal rates, in their own reference frame. Neither was slowed or accelerated in any subjective sense. They simply travelled along worldlines of different **lengths** in spacetime, and the lengths correspond to different durations.

In spacetime geometry, counter-intuitively, the worldline that _looks longer_ on a diagram is actually _shorter_ in duration. This is a specific feature of spacetime geometry (which has a pseudo-Euclidean rather than Euclidean metric). The traveller's "bent" path (going out and coming back) is geometrically shorter in spacetime duration than the Earth-twin's straight path, even though it looks longer drawn on a diagram.

## 9. Time Travel — Is It Physically Possible?

Lachièze-Rey wrote a book specifically on this subject (_Voyage temporel_, Éditions du Seuil).

In spacetime, time travel amounts to asking: **can worldlines form closed or nearly-closed loops** — i.e. can the future path of a worldline pass through, or very close to, a point that was already on the past portion of that worldline?

**Kurt Gödel's contribution (1949)**:

Gödel — the mathematician best known for his incompleteness theorems — was a colleague and friend of Einstein at Princeton. He showed that in formal terms, the equations of **general relativity do admit solutions** in which such closed or nearly-closed worldlines exist. He presented this as a mathematical possibility within the framework of GR.

Einstein was not pleased with these solutions, believing they could not physically occur.

**Stephen Hawking's response**:

Hawking proposed an additional principle — the **Chronological Protection Conjecture** — effectively a proposed supplementary law of physics stating that no admissible solution of GR can contain closed timelike curves (worldline loops). He called it a conjecture because it could not be proved from GR alone.

Current physicists fall into three camps:

1. Those who follow Hawking — closed worldlines cannot exist; reject such solutions.
2. Those who say — until someone proves it impossible, it remains possible.
3. Those who say — whether or not physically real, these solutions are worth studying because they illuminate the structure of spacetime and the nature of relativity.

## 10. The Grandfather Paradox and the Principle of Coherence

The **grandfather paradox**: I travel into the past, kill my grandfather before my father is conceived. My father is never born. I am never born. So I never travel to the past. Contradiction.

Lachièze-Rey's resolution — the **principle of coherence** (also called the Novikov self-consistency principle):

If I were in a situation where I might kill my grandfather, the laws of physics would guarantee I would fail. My gun would jam. I would have a last-minute change of heart. Something would prevent it. What has happened in the past has happened — it cannot be undone. The past has the same ontological status as everything else in spacetime: it exists. What exists cannot be un-made.

This is not a logical trick — it follows from the consistency of the physical equations. If such a situation arose, the only self-consistent outcome is one where the past remains what it was.

**Implication for free will**: in such a scenario, **free will would be absent** with respect to that specific action. If at age 40 I meet my 20-year-old self and tell him not to buy that house (which I know burned down, causing me great suffering), my 20-year-old self can try with all his will to avoid the house — and he will fail. Because the purchase is already in my past. It happened. The laws of physics require consistency.

Lachièze-Rey connects this to the story of **Oedipus**: Oedipus learns he will kill his father and marry his mother. He flees to avoid it. His very flight turns out to be the mechanism by which the prophecy is fulfilled. He kills his father without recognising him, and marries his mother without knowing who she is. What he did to prevent the future caused the future.

## 11. Does the Past, Present, and Future All Exist?

The only cosmological position **consistent with relativity** is what philosophers call **eternalism** or the **block universe**: past, present, and future all exist equally. All events exist — those that "will" happen tomorrow have the same ontological status as those that "have" happened yesterday.

Lachièze-Rey's argument:

- **Presentism** (only the present exists) is incompatible with relativity, because the present cannot be defined across space. "What is happening on Mars right now?" has no objective answer. If only the present exists, and the present cannot be defined, then we know nothing of what exists.
- **Past-and-present-ism** (the present and past exist, the future does not) also fails, because what counts as the past is observer-dependent. What is in my past may be in your future.
- Therefore, the only consistent position is that **all events exist** — past, present, and future — and what distinguishes them is not some difference in their mode of being, but a difference in our epistemic relation to them (we remember the past, we don't remember the future; we can influence what is "ahead" along our worldline, we cannot change what is "behind").

What makes tomorrow different from yesterday? Not anything in the physics. Not anything in spacetime. Only something in memory and consciousness — and **no one knows why**.

## 12. Language and the Difficulty of Expressing These Ideas

Lachièze-Rey notes that even discussing these ideas requires care, because **all ordinary language is saturated with temporal assumptions**:

- The word "time" cannot be used properly.
- "Simultaneous" cannot be used.
- "Chronological order" cannot be used.
- Even "moment" and "when" carry assumptions that don't hold.

The only properly permissible term in modern physics is **duration** (_durée propre_ — proper duration), which refers to the length of a worldline segment — something measurable, local, and well-defined.

He says, laughing, that he is "obsessed with time" and constantly has to catch himself using words that are technically forbidden by the physics he is describing.
