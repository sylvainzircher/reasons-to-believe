# Einstein and Relativity — Étienne Klein (Public Lecture, Translated from French)

## Overview

A public lecture by **Étienne Klein**, physicist and director of the Laboratory for Research on Matter Sciences at the French Alternative Energies and Atomic Energy Commission (CEA), and host of the weekly programme _La Conversation scientifique_ on France Culture. Part of a lecture series on great scientific thinkers ("Thinkers of Yesterday and Today") at a Paris amphitheatre. The talk traces the construction of special relativity (1905) and general relativity (1915), the real meaning of E=mc², and closes with a reflection on Einstein's unusual relationship with space, place, and national identity. A Q&A session follows.

---

## 1. Einstein as a Figure

Einstein occupies a unique status in cultural history: he was the equivalent of a hundred intellectuals condensed into one, and also a popular hero. During his lifetime and after, more than 2,000 books and thousands of articles were written about him. His face is as well-known as the surface of the moon. His brain was sliced into sections for analysis after his death. In the year 2000, Time magazine readers chose him as the person who best symbolised the 20th century.

Einstein worked on many subjects: light, space and time, gravitation, and he also held patents (he was an engineer by training). The theories for which he is most famous are the two relativities: special relativity (1905) and general relativity (1915).

## 2. The Background: Light, the Ether, and the Crisis in Physics

### Newton's Corpuscular Theory of Light

At the end of the 17th century, Newton held that light was a form of "grapeshot" — a stream of corpuscles travelling through empty space.

### Young and Fresnel: Light as a Wave

A century later, at the start of the 19th century, experiments by **Thomas Young** (England) and **Augustin-Jean Fresnel** (France) established that light is a wave phenomenon, not a particle one. The key evidence was interference: light can add to itself constructively or destructively, a property of waves.

This led almost all physicists to postulate the existence of the **ether** — a medium that filled all space and through which light vibrated, as water vibrates for a water wave. **Maxwell's theory** of electromagnetism (formalised in the 1860s) confirmed that light consists of electromagnetic waves propagating at the speed of light — but waves require a medium to vibrate _through_.

### Properties of the Ether

Maxwell's theory implied the ether had to be:

- Massless
- Invisible
- Zero viscosity
- **Absolutely immobile** — this was its only property of significance

This absolute immobility was the fatal contradiction: it conflicted with **Galileo's principle of relativity** (adopted but not credited from Giordano Bruno, burnt in 1600), which states that _motion is like nothing_ — you cannot detect absolute motion from within your own reference frame. Newton's mechanics says nothing is absolutely at rest or in motion. Yet the ether, if it existed, defined an absolute rest frame — a contradiction with the rest of classical mechanics.

### The Michelson-Morley Experiment

American physicists **Albert Michelson** and **Edward Morley** used an interferometer to try to measure the Earth's velocity relative to the (supposedly stationary) ether. Result: **zero**. No variation in the speed of light was detected regardless of direction. The Earth appeared to be at rest relative to the ether — which made no sense.

### The Lorentz Transformations

Three physicists independently developed formulae that could account for this strange kinematic behaviour of light:

- **George FitzGerald** (Dublin, 1889)
- **Hendrik Lorentz** (Netherlands, 1892)
- **Henri Poincaré** (France, later)

These **Lorentz transformations** showed that velocities don't simply add together (as in everyday experience), but combine in a subtler way. Their peculiar consequence: when objects move at high speeds, their lengths appear to contract and time intervals change. Space coordinates partially transform into time coordinates and vice versa — contradicting Newtonian absolutes.

The physicists who derived these formulae understood them as mathematical tools but misinterpreted their physical meaning: they thought the length contraction was caused by a physical force exerted by the ether on moving bodies. It took Einstein to understand what these formulae were _really_ saying.

## 3. Einstein's Long Road to Special Relativity

### 1895 — The 16-Year-Old's Essay

Einstein (age 15–16), living near Milan after resigning from his militaristic Munich school — swearing never to enter a classroom again — was de-schooled for a year. He wrote a small essay for his uncle César Koch titled: "On the Present State of Research Concerning the Ether in Magnetic Fields." In it he listed the questions he intended to explore throughout his life, all related to the propagation of light.

### The First Thought Experiment: What If Light Emits Light?

At 15–16, Einstein posed a thought experiment: if a light source emits light, and if velocities simply add, then the emitted light should travel at twice the speed of light; if that emitted light also emits light, at three times; and so on, generating infinite speed. He understood this couldn't be right — but wasn't yet sure why.

### 1896 — The Second Thought Experiment: Riding a Light Beam

At 17 (now re-enrolled at a more liberal school in Aarau, Switzerland), Einstein imagined a different question — deliberately thinking with his own body, which would become his signature method: _What would I perceive if I were riding a light beam?_

If you ride the crest of a wave, the waves in front and behind you appear stationary. Light consists of electromagnetic waves — so if you travelled alongside one, you would perceive a stationary electromagnetic wave. But Einstein already knew that **Maxwell's equations have no solution corresponding to a stationary electromagnetic wave**. So: is his thought experiment impossible, or are Maxwell's equations wrong?

He spent nine years wrestling with this question. The answer came in June 1905.

### June 1905 — The Special Relativity Paper

Written in Berne, where Einstein was a patent examiner (employed at the Federal Intellectual Property Office). The paper is remarkably readable — few formulas, written almost like a patent application, in plain language. It begins with disarmingly simple questions such as: "What do I mean when I say a train arrives at this station at 7 o'clock?" and then rigorously follows the implications.

The paper has two key moves:

**1. Abolish the ether.** The ether is superfluous. Light does not need a medium to propagate — it vibrates through nothing, it is a self-sustaining oscillation of the electromagnetic field. Empty space is truly empty. This conceptual move eliminates the contradiction.

**2. Declare the speed of light invariant.** The speed of light is the same in all reference frames, regardless of the speed of the source or the speed of the observer. If a car approaches you at near the speed of light with its headlights on, the speed at which that light reaches you is still _c_ — the speed of light — not _c_ plus the car's speed. This is what the Lorentz transformations had been saying all along.

With these two moves, Einstein answered both of his adolescent questions:

- _If light emits light, what speed does it travel at?_ — The speed of light. Always.
- _What would I perceive if I rode a light beam?_ — Impossible. No observer can be stationary relative to light, because light always moves at _c_ relative to any observer.

The consequence: **space and length, and time and duration, are no longer absolute** — they depend on the relative velocity between observer and object. The Lorentz transformations are not artefacts of the ether; they are properties of spacetime itself. Length contraction is an effect of perspective in spacetime, not a physical pressure.

## 4. E = mc² — What It Really Means and What It Doesn't

### September 1905 — The Three-Page Paper

Two months after the special relativity paper, Einstein published a short follow-up article: "Does the Inertia of a Body Depend Upon Its Energy Content?" In under three pages he derived what would become E = mc².

**The argument**: Consider a body that emits light. When it does, it loses energy — the energy carried away by the electromagnetic waves. Since the emitted waves have no mass, one would intuitively expect no change in the body's mass. But Einstein's calculation shows otherwise: **the body loses mass equal to the emitted energy divided by c².** If a body loses energy _E_, it simultaneously loses mass equal to _E/c²_.

Einstein went further: he declared this universal — valid even when _no light is involved_. Any time a body loses energy in any form, it loses mass. Therefore: mass is a measure of energy content. Any massive body at rest possesses an "energy of mass": **E = mc²**.

The appearance of _c_ in the formula is not because light is involved in every process — it's because Einstein derived the result by considering an electromagnetic emission. But the constant _c_ thereby acquires a new universal status: it ceases to be merely the speed of a particular physical phenomenon and becomes a **universal constant of physics**, appearing in all processes whether or not light is present.

### The Atomic Bomb Myth

In the collective imagination, E=mc² is fused with Hiroshima (Little Boy, 6 August 1945) and Nagasaki (Fat Man, 9 August 1945). This association is completely unjustified. E=mc² applies to all processes in which energy is exchanged — including a lit match or a falling pencil.

**What actually opened the door to the atomic bomb**: the 1938 discovery — by **Otto Hahn** and **Lisa Meitner** — that uranium-235, when struck by a slow neutron, can split into two fragments, releasing energy and more neutrons capable of striking more uranium-235 nuclei: a chain reaction. This discovery of nuclear fission was entirely accidental and unrelated to E=mc².

Einstein's own role was indirect:

- He had no interest in nuclear physics and barely understood fission initially.
- Niels Bohr visited him in Princeton in early 1939 to discuss fission; Einstein received him with "polite indifference."
- Only when his Hungarian friend **Leó Szilárd** came to him personally did Einstein finally grasp the possibility of an explosive chain reaction — and said: _"I had never thought of that. I had never thought of that."_
- Once convinced, he acted as a **whistleblower** — concerned that Hitler might develop the bomb first. He signed a letter drafted by Szilárd (and co-signed by physicist Sachs) addressed to President Roosevelt, dated **2 August 1939**, alerting him to the possibility. This letter eventually helped trigger the **Manhattan Project** after the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbour (December 1941).
- Einstein never collaborated on the Manhattan Project and was not told it was underway. When he heard about Hiroshima on the radio he said only: _"Poor me."_ He was deeply affected — he had visited Japan in 1921.
- **J. Robert Oppenheimer** (scientific father of the bomb) said of him: "Einstein was a solitary who loved his fellow men. He never envisioned this crime against humanity."

Einstein wrote in November 1945: _"I do not consider myself the father of the liberation of atomic energy. My role was entirely indirect. In fact, I did not foresee that it would be released in my lifetime. It only became possible following the accidental discovery of the chain reaction, and it is not something I would have predicted, nor could anyone have predicted."_

**A final irony**: after the hydrogen bomb test at Enewetak Atoll in November 1950, analysis of the debris at the Berkeley laboratory revealed two new chemical elements. Element 100 was named **fermium** (for Enrico Fermi). Element 99, a silvery-white metal, was named **einsteinium**. This was classified information until after Einstein's death in 1955. Klein remarks: probably wise — it would have been a painful honour.

## 5. General Relativity (1915)

### November 1907 — "The Happiest Thought of My Life"

Einstein, still a patent examiner in Berne with no academic post, was half-dozing on his stool at his desk when he had the idea he later called the happiest of his life:

> _"If a person falls freely, they will not feel their own weight."_

He was thinking with his body again: if he fell and simultaneously released a book, Galileo's principle (all bodies fall at the same rate regardless of mass) means the book would fall alongside him — apparently in levitation. From his perspective, there would be no gravity. Yet he is being pulled by gravity. Therefore: the acceleration of free fall _cancels_ the effects of gravity that caused it. The effect annuls its own cause.

### The Equivalence Principle

From this thought experiment, Einstein derived the **principle of equivalence**: _acceleration is physically indistinguishable from gravity_. A person in a sealed lift cannot tell whether:

- The lift is stationary in a gravitational field, or
- The lift is accelerating in empty space at the same rate as the gravitational field

In both cases, objects fall the same way and they feel the same weight.

### 1911 Prague — Light is Bent by Gravity

Now a professor at the German University in Prague, Einstein asked: what does the equivalence principle imply for light?

He imagined an accelerating elevator with a hole in the wall. Light enters horizontally; the elevator accelerates upward while the light crosses. Since the elevator moves up during the transit, the light strikes the opposite wall at a point lower than the entry point — from the perspective of someone inside, the light beam is curved downward.

By the equivalence principle, the same must happen in a gravitational field: **gravity curves the path of light** even though light has no mass. This was revolutionary.

Einstein needed the mathematics of **non-Euclidean geometry** to describe curved spacetime. He returned to Zurich specifically to work with his old classmate **Marcel Grossmann**, who had attended all the maths lectures Einstein had skipped — and who knew everything about Riemannian (curved) geometry.

A total solar eclipse was predicted for August 1914 (observable from Crimea). An expedition was organised to measure the deflection of starlight around the sun — but it was interrupted by the outbreak of World War I. Fortunately: Einstein's equations were still wrong at that point.

### November 1915 — The Field Equations of General Relativity

Einstein published the final correct equations of general relativity in November 1915. Rather than gravity being a force acting across space (Newton's model), **gravity is the curvature of spacetime caused by mass and energy**. Objects follow the straightest possible paths (geodesics) through curved spacetime. The moon's orbit is not a curved path in flat space — it is a straight path in curved spacetime.

The first test: Einstein calculated the anomalous precession of Mercury's perihelion — a small deviation from Newtonian predictions that had puzzled astronomers for decades. His new equations predicted it exactly. Not proof, but a powerful indication.

### 1919 — Eddington's Expedition and World Fame

The British astronomer **Arthur Eddington** — a pacifist who wanted to demonstrate that science could transcend war — organised two expeditions to observe the May 1919 solar eclipse in Brazil and Príncipe (West Africa). The measured deflection of starlight agreed with Einstein's prediction. Einstein's name appeared on front pages worldwide — in the US, South America, Africa, Asia. (France was a notable exception.) This was the moment Einstein became a figure of world celebrity, as if transcending ordinary humanity.

Einstein had predicted gravitational waves in 1916 but believed they would never be detected. They were finally detected by **LIGO** in 2015 — a century later — and were caused by the merger of two black holes. Einstein also didn't believe black holes were real (though they emerge directly from his equations). Had he been alive: _"I would love to have seen his reaction."_

## 6. Einstein, Space, and Non-Belonging

Klein closes with a reflection on Einstein's unusual relationship with place.

Einstein wrote: _"I am truly a solitary traveller. I have never felt fully at home — not in my country, not in my home, not with my friends, not even with my close family."_

His nationalities:

- German by birth
- Renounced German nationality at 16 (to avoid military service) — temporarily stateless
- Swiss
- Swiss and German (dual)
- Renounced German nationality when Hitler came to power
- Swiss, then American (emigrated 1933)

Three anecdotes:

**1. The visitor's book (1933, England)**: In July 1933, Einstein was living in Belgium (at De Haan on the sea — he understood he couldn't return to Germany, where the Nazis had burned his books and searched his apartment). He visited England, met Churchill and Lloyd George at a dinner, and was asked to sign the visitors' book. The entry for "Address": _"None."_ Factually true at that moment — and metaphysically true throughout his life.

**2. Letter to Max Born (1920)**: When Born asked him whether to take a post in one city or another, Einstein replied: _"Where one settles, it doesn't matter as such. I have wandered constantly. I buried my mother in Berlin a few days ago. My children are in Switzerland and circumstances make it hard for me to visit them. The ideal for a man like me is to be at home anywhere, with those one loves."_

**3. The street name (1946)**: In his hometown of Ulm (where he was born), a street had been named "Einstein-Strasse." After the Nazis came to power in the early 1930s, the first Nazi mayor renamed it. After the Allied victory in 1945, it was renamed back. When Einstein heard about the various renamings he wrote: _"I had heard about the amusing street-name story and it made me laugh. I imagine it will be changed again, and then again. But I am quite capable of moderating my curiosity on this matter. I think a neutral name like 'Weathervane Street' would better suit the German political mentality and would make future renamings unnecessary."_

### The Connection to Physics

Klein argues this is not merely biographical: Einstein's indifference to location was a lived expression of his own central theoretical principle. In general relativity, **coordinates have no physical meaning** — all coordinate systems in spacetime are equivalent. No location is fundamentally privileged. What happens in spacetime is more essential than the coordinates used to describe it (invariance under diffeomorphism). Einstein lived this principle: _what happens in a mind is far more important than the coordinates of the place where it happens._

His biographer **Philipp Frank** records: when they planned to meet at a Berlin observatory, Klein warned Einstein he might be late. Einstein replied: _"It doesn't matter. I'll wait on the Voigt Bridge. My sort of work can be done anywhere. Why should I think less well about my problems on a bridge than at home?"_

## Q&A Session (Key Points)

**How was special relativity received?**
In 1905, Einstein was unknown. He expected rapid reaction to his papers published in Annalen der Physik — there was almost none. He fell into a kind of depression. It was **Max Planck** — widely considered the founding father of quantum mechanics — who took his work seriously first and began propagating it among physicists. The slow recognition only accelerated when Einstein finally met other theorists in person at the first **Solvay Conference** in Brussels (1911), where he met Poincaré (only once), Marie Curie, and many others.

General relativity was different: by 1916 it had already explained Mercury's precession, and after Eddington's 1919 confirmation it brought world fame overnight.

**Was Einstein wrong on quantum mechanics?**
Not exactly: Einstein never denied quantum mechanics. In 1935, with **Podolsky and Rosen**, he wrote the famous four-page EPR paper arguing that if quantum mechanics is complete, it predicts something seemingly impossible — correlations between distant particles at the instant of measurement (quantum entanglement). He thought this proved QM was incomplete. He was right that the phenomenon exists and right that it's bizarre — but wrong that it implies incompleteness. Experiments in the 1980s confirmed that entanglement is real. _"When Einstein is wrong, he is right."_

**Can we travel at the speed of light?**
No. Even if you ran at near the speed of light behind a photon, the speed of that photon relative to you would still be _c_. There is no point running.

**How did Einstein die?**
He died in 1955 (aged 76) of a ruptured aortic aneurysm. He refused surgery. He had asked that his body be cremated and his ashes scattered so as not to become an object of cult. A disobedient doctor removed his brain, preserved it in formaldehyde, had it sliced and analysed in various institutions — looking for the structural basis of his genius. Result: nothing special was found, except that it was a slightly older brain than average. Klein: _"Fundamentally very satisfying and very encouraging."_

**Did Einstein intentionally push for the atomic bomb?**
No. He signed the letter to Roosevelt purely as a whistleblower, motivated by fear that Nazi Germany would develop the bomb first. He never collaborated with the Manhattan Project and was not told it was underway. He learned of Hiroshima on the radio. He had no role in designing the bomb. E = mc² did not in itself suggest nuclear weapons — that required the 1938 discovery of fission, which Einstein had not worked on and initially failed to grasp.

**What are the biggest open questions in Einstein's legacy today?**

1. **Unification of general relativity and quantum mechanics** — the two theories use fundamentally different spacetimes. The quantum vacuum has nothing in common with the relativistic vacuum. When all four forces operate together (as they must have done in the primordial universe), neither theory alone is adequate. The leading candidate approaches are **string theory** (particles as one-dimensional vibrating strings in higher-dimensional spacetime — elegant but multiply redundant) and **loop quantum gravity**. The experimental challenge: testing quantum gravity requires energies near the Planck scale, billions of times beyond the reach of the Large Hadron Collider. The only accessible test sites would be near black holes or in the early universe.

2. **Dark matter and dark energy** — galaxy dynamics agree with Einstein's equations only if the universe contains ~5× more mass than we can see (dark matter) and is accelerating in its expansion due to some form of energy embedded in space (dark energy). These two unknown quantities account for ~95% of the universe's total energy budget. Two philosophical approaches: (a) _ontological_ — dark matter and dark energy are real undiscovered entities that save Einstein's equations; (b) _legislative_ — modify Einstein's theory (MOND-type theories) to account for the observations without invoking unseen matter. _"This debate is very active — it is the central debate in astrophysics today."_

**Was the Big Bang the absolute origin of the universe?**
Not necessarily. Extrapolating Einstein's equations backwards reaches a singularity (infinite temperature, infinite density) that we call the Big Bang — but that extrapolation only uses general relativity. At extreme energies, quantum mechanics and the other forces also matter. When any quantum gravity theory is applied to the pre-Planck era, **the singularity disappears** — the Big Bang may be a phase transition between a prior universe and our current one (as the Étienne Klein lecture on time also discussed). _"We do not yet have proof that the universe has an origin. We may never get out of this inn — we are in it, and can't leave."_
