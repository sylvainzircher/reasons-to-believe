# Christian Practice in Justin Martyr's _First Apology_

Justin's account of Christian worship (chapters 61–67) is historically remarkable — it's one of the earliest surviving descriptions of how Christians actually gathered and what they did. He includes it deliberately: rather than let Romans imagine secret, depraved rituals, he opens the door and describes everything plainly.

---

## Why He Includes It

Justin is making a transparency argument. Throughout the _Apology_ he insists Christians have nothing to hide. Describing their practices in detail to the emperor serves two purposes simultaneously: it refutes the rumors of immorality, and it demonstrates that Christian worship is _rational_ — ordered around scripture, prayer, and ethical instruction rather than superstition or magic.

---

## Baptism — Entry into the Community

The process Justin describes is deliberate and communal, not a private or spontaneous act.

A candidate must first be **convinced** — they have to genuinely believe the teaching and commit to living by it. Then follows a period of fasting and prayer, shared by both the candidate and the existing community. This is not a formality; it signals that entry into the community is a serious moral transition.

The washing itself takes place in water, pronounced "in the name of God the Father and Lord of the universe, and of Jesus Christ, and of the Holy Spirit." Justin cites Christ's words — _"unless you be born again, you shall not enter the kingdom of heaven"_ — and notes the obvious objection: no one can re-enter the womb. His answer is that baptism is a spiritual rebirth, not a physical one — a passage from unchosen ignorance into chosen knowledge and belonging.

He calls baptism **illumination**. The candidate is not simply washed clean of past sins; they are enlightened — their understanding is changed. The rite marks the boundary between a life shaped by external forces and a life shaped by deliberate choice.

He also draws a contrast with pagan purification rites, noting that demons, having learned of baptism through the prophets, invented their own imitation washings in temple rituals — which Justin takes as back-handed confirmation that baptism is the real thing.

---

## The Eucharist — The Central Meal

Immediately after baptism, the newly illuminated person is brought into the assembly of the "brethren" for the first time. Baptism is the door; the Eucharist is the table you find inside it.

The ritual unfolds in a clear order:

1. **Communal prayer** — the whole assembly prays together, for themselves and for the newly baptized
2. **The kiss of peace** — members greet one another
3. **Presentation of bread and wine mixed with water** to the president (the presiding leader)
4. **Thanksgiving prayer** — the president offers extended praise to the Father, through the Son, in the Holy Spirit
5. **The people's assent** — everyone responds _Amen_, the Hebrew word Justin translates as "so be it"
6. **Distribution** — deacons bring the elements to each person present, and carry portions to those who are absent

Justin is careful about what this food _is_. He explicitly rejects the idea that it is ordinary bread and wine: "not as common bread and common drink do we receive these." The food blessed by the prayer of Christ's word is the flesh and blood of Jesus — received by faith, nourishing the believer in a way that mirrors how Christ's incarnation nourished humanity.

He also restricts participation sharply: only those who believe, have been baptized, and are living as Christ taught may receive. The Eucharist is not open to observers or the unbaptized.

---

## Sunday Worship — The Weekly Rhythm

Justin's description of the regular Sunday assembly has a recognizable shape to anyone familiar with later Christian liturgy.

- **The gathering** — Christians from both the city and the surrounding countryside come together in one place.
- **Scripture reading** — The "memoirs of the apostles" (the Gospels) or the writings of the prophets are read aloud "for as long as time permits."
- **The homily** — The president speaks verbally, exhorting the community to imitate the good things they have just heard.
- **Standing prayer** — The whole assembly rises together and prays. Standing was a mark of resurrection joy, as opposed to prostration for grief or penitence.
- **The Eucharist** — Bread, wine, and water are brought forward; the president gives thanks; the people respond _Amen_; deacons distribute.
- **The collection** — Those who are able give what they choose. The president uses it to care for orphans, widows, the sick, prisoners, and strangers passing through.

---

## Why Sunday

Justin provides an explicit theological rationale for the day:

> Sunday is the day on which we all hold our common assembly, because it is the first day on which God made the world, and Jesus Christ our Savior on the same day rose from the dead.

Two events, one day: the first creation and the new creation. The weekly gathering re-enacts both. He also notes the sequential logic: Christ was crucified on Saturn's day (Saturday) eve, and appeared to his apostles on the day after — the day of the sun.

---

## The Picture as a Whole

What Justin describes is a community structured around **word, meal, and mutual care**. Scripture is read and explained; the Eucharist is shared among the baptized; money flows from the prosperous to the vulnerable.

The overall effect Justin is aiming for is to show the emperor a community that is morally serious, rationally ordered, and transparently beneficial — the opposite of the secret, dangerous cult the rumors described.
