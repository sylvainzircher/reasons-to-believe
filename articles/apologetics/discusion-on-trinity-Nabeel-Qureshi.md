# Trinity vs Tawhid: Debate Summary

### Nabeel Qureshi (Christian) vs Dr. Shabir Ally (Muslim)

---

## Overview

A structured theological debate on whether the Christian doctrine of the Trinity or the Islamic doctrine of Tawhid (monotheism) is better supported by Scripture and reason. Both participants argued from their respective scriptures, cited scholars, and challenged each other to apply their reasoning consistently across both faiths.

---

## Nabeel Qureshi's Position: The Trinity

### Core Claim

The word "Trinity" is not in the Bible, but the **concept** is — just as "Tawhid" is not in the Quran but Muslims accept the concept. Qureshi argues the biblical data, taken honestly, leads to the conclusion that God is one being in three persons.

### Biblical Arguments

**1. Two Divine Figures in the Old Testament**

- **Genesis 18–19** — Yahweh appears to Abraham on earth while simultaneously raining fire from heaven. The Hebrew uses "Yahweh" twice, suggesting two distinct divine presences.
- **Psalm 110:1** — "The Lord said to my Lord, sit at my right hand." Two figures, both carrying divine authority.
- **Daniel 7:13–14** — The Ancient of Days (the Father) and the Son of Man, who receives worship reserved exclusively for God alone. The word "serve" used here appears over 130 times in Scripture and always denotes worship due to God alone.

**2. Jesus Claims to Be One of Those Figures**
In Mark 14:62, Jesus combines imagery from both Psalm 110 and Daniel 7 — sitting at the right hand of Power and coming on the clouds. Qureshi argues Jesus was deliberately identifying himself as the second divine figure seen throughout the Old Testament. The religious authorities understood this as a claim to be God, which is why they called for his crucifixion.

**3. The Holy Spirit is Also God**
The book of Acts identifies the Holy Spirit as God directly. The Spirit is treated as personal — he can be lied to, grieved, and refers to himself as "me." He is not an impersonal force.

**4. The Three Are Distinct From Each Other**

- Jesus prays to the Father → Jesus is not the Father
- The Holy Spirit is present alongside the Father → they are not the same
- Jesus is not the Holy Spirit

**5. The Trinity is the Best Explanation**
Given one God but three distinct persons each identified as God, Qureshi argues the Trinity is the most coherent framework to hold all the biblical data together.

### Closing Argument: Justice and Mercy

A monadic (single-person) God cannot fully reconcile justice and mercy. Justice demands punishment for sin; mercy demands forgiveness. Only a triune God — one who could take on human nature and absorb the punishment himself — can satisfy both simultaneously.

## Dr. Shabir Ally's Pushbacks and Qureshi's Responses

---

### 1. "The Word Trinity Isn't in the Bible"

**Ally:** There is no phrase "three persons in the Godhead" anywhere in Scripture. The word and concept were invented later.

**Qureshi:** Conceded the word isn't there — but noted the word "Tawhid" isn't in the Quran or Hadith either, citing Ally's own source (Bilal Phillips' _Fundamentals of Tawhid_). Both religions work with concepts not expressed in a single scriptural word.

---

### 2. "The Trinity Was Developed Over Centuries"

**Ally:** The Nicene and Chalcedonian creeds aren't in Scripture. Christian theology took centuries to develop, suggesting the Trinity is a human invention.

**Qureshi:** Agreed — but pointed out Islamic theology went through the same process. The debate over whether the Quran was eternal or created raged for centuries, with people tortured and killed over it. Theological development over time is a feature of both faiths, not a unique problem for Christianity.

---

### 3. "Christian Scholars Themselves Accuse Each Other of Tritheism"

**Ally:** Cited a book by four Christian scholars where one accuses another's explanation of the Trinity of amounting to three gods — a heresy even by Christian standards.

**Qureshi:** Acknowledged Christians often fail to explain the Trinity well ("shame on Christians"). But noted Ally was citing Gregory Boyd, an open theist who doesn't believe God knows the future — not a representative mainstream voice. Scholars should be quoted with their full context and reasoning.

---

### 4. "Psalm 110 Uses Adon, Not Yahweh — Jesus Is Separate From God"

**Ally:** The second "Lord" in Psalm 110:1 is _Adon_, not _Yahweh_ — so Jesus (the second figure) is someone other than the tri-personal God, undermining the Trinity.

**Qureshi:** Argued the two terms are functionally synonymous in Jewish practice, since Jews always substituted _Adonai_ when reading _Yahweh_ aloud. More importantly, in all of Second Temple Jewish history, **no one was ever depicted sitting next to God on his throne** — making Jesus' claim extraordinary regardless of which word is used.

---

### 5. "Daniel 7's Son of Man Is Not the Ancient of Days — So Not Fully God"

**Ally:** The Son of Man approaches the Ancient of Days as a separate figure. He may be divine, but he is not God himself.

**Qureshi:** Said this was exactly his point — two distinct divine figures, yet one God. Cited Jewish scholars **Daniel Boyarin** and **Alan Segal**, both of whom argue Jews at the time of Jesus were effectively _binitarian_, already entertaining two divine figures from passages like Daniel 7. This was not a Christian invention but was present in pre-Christian Jewish thought.

---

### 6. "Paul and Mark Don't Present Jesus as Yahweh"

**Ally:** Paul shows Christ ultimately submitting to God (1 Corinthians 15) and God as the head of Christ (1 Corinthians 11:3). Mark's Jesus affirms the Shema — one God — and the "him" is clearly someone other than Jesus.

**Qureshi:** Noted Ally had previously conceded in a debate with James White that in Philippians 2:10–11, Paul substitutes Jesus for Yahweh. That admission must be accounted for across Paul's whole theology. On Mark, he pointed out that Jesus immediately follows the Shema passage by quoting Psalm 110, and that Mark consistently portrays Jesus doing things only Yahweh does — calming storms, being Lord of the Sabbath, coming on the clouds.

---

### 7. "How Can the Eternal Son of God Die on the Cross?"

**Ally:** If Jesus is fully God, his death means the second person of the Trinity is expendable while the other two carry on — a deep incoherence.

**Qureshi:** Argued death doesn't mean ceasing to exist. Offered an analogy: when someone burns a Quran, Muslims are outraged not because paper and ink are eternal, but because the offense is _imputed_ to the eternal Word behind the physical text. Similarly, the punishment of Jesus' physical death is imputed to his divine nature — giving it infinite weight sufficient to atone for all sin.

---

## Overall Patterns

|                  | Qureshi (Trinity)                              | Ally (Tawhid)                                         |
| ---------------- | ---------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| Primary strategy | Biblical exegesis + symmetry argument          | Scholarly citations + internal Christian critique     |
| Strongest moment | Jewish binitarian scholarship (Boyarin, Segal) | Pointing to internal Christian scholarly disagreement |
| Weakest moment   | Quran-burning analogy (partly rejected)        | Couldn't fully escape the Tawhid parallel             |
| Recurring move   | "The same problem exists in Islam"             | "Christians can't even agree among themselves"        |

**Qureshi's consistent strategy** was symmetry — whenever Ally raised a difficulty with the Trinity, Qureshi found the parallel difficulty in Islam and demanded the same standard be applied to both. This kept Ally on the defensive. Where Qureshi was weakest was in detailed textual arguments, where Ally's linguistic points sometimes went only partially answered.
